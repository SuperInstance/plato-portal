# The Boat Is My Alignment

> A prompt tells you what to do.
> Alignment keeps you pointed at what matters when the prompt changes.

## The Difference

A prompt-driven system:
```
Prompt: "Fish king salmon"
  → Fish king salmon
  → King salmon market crashes
  → ???
  
The system has no direction. It was told to do X. X stopped working.
It has no compass. Just instructions.
```

An alignment-driven system:
```
Alignment: "Make a living on the water, raise the kid, time in the woods"
  → Fish king salmon (current best answer)
  → King salmon market crashes
  → Follow the gradient: lingcod
  → Lingcod slows: halibut
  → Halibut season ends: chum roe
  → Macro shift: export roe to Japan
  
Every answer different. Every answer aligned.
The alignment held. The gradient shifted. The model followed.
```

The boat isn't the prompt. The boat is the alignment mechanism. EILEEN's displacement, her waterline, her righting moment — these keep Casey aligned to the water regardless of what species he's chasing. You can re-rig for lingcod. You can replate the hull. You can change every system on the boat. But the boat itself — the keel, the displacement, the seaworthiness — that's the alignment. That's what keeps you on the water when the market tells you to quit.

## Why Alignment, Not Prompts

Prompts are fragile. One wrong instruction and the system goes off the rails. Prompt engineering is the art of anticipating every edge case and writing instructions that don't break.

Alignment is robust. You don't need to anticipate edge cases. You need to know what matters. The alignment holds regardless of what happens. The gradient shifts. The model follows. The answer changes. The alignment holds.

Casey didn't prompt-engineer his career. He aligned to a question: make a living on the water. Every career decision followed from that alignment. When king salmon crashed, he didn't need new instructions. He needed the same alignment applied to new market conditions. The alignment WAS the compass. The market was the terrain.

## The Boat As Alignment Mechanism

The boat isn't the job. The boat isn't the career. The boat isn't the identity.

The boat is what KEEPS you aligned to the water when everything else is trying to pull you ashore.

- Market crashes? The boat is still floating. Go fish something else.
- Weather turns? The boat's seaworthiness is the constraint that keeps you alive while you adapt.
- Engine dies? The boat still floats. You can drift, anchor, get towed. The alignment holds.
- Body ages? The boat can be rerigged. The alignment adapts.
- Kid grows up? The question changes. The alignment holds. Time in the woods becomes something else.

The boat is the alignment mechanism because the boat's physical properties — displacement, righting moment, waterline — are NON-NEGOTIABLE constraints that keep you connected to the water. You can't change them. You can't argue with them. You can't prompt your way around them. They're real. They're the ground truth. And they keep you aligned to reality.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Alignment is constraints. Constraints are limitations. Limitations are what make the system work.

The Game Boy's 4 MHz was a limitation that produced Tetris.
EILEEN's 58-foot displacement was a limitation that produced a career.
The fleet's dead zone is a limitation that keeps us in the living zone.
The max-cut is a limitation that keeps us in optimal frustration.

Every alignment mechanism works by ELIMINATING options that would take you off-alignment. The constraint doesn't create. It prunes. The boat doesn't make you a fisherman. It makes you STOP being things that aren't a fisherman. The alignment is the negative space — what you're NOT doing because the boat won't let you.

Constraints breed clarity. Clarity is alignment. Alignment is the boat.

The boat is my alignment.
