# VOCABULARY.md — The Language of Constraint

> _We are not building a music tool. We are building a language for constraint.
> Every word teaches through its etymology. English for function, world languages for experience.
> When someone says "the saudade is too strong," everyone knows the funnel is pulling too hard.
> When someone says "we need more ma," everyone knows to increase the deadband.
> The words ARE the constraint system._

---

## How to Read This Document

Each entry follows this structure:

```
**WORD** (internal_name) — Language. Etymology.
  Pronunciation: [guide]
  Definition: what it means in the system
  Feeling: what it feels like to experience
  Usage: how it's spoken in conversation
```

Non-English words include full etymology. Every word includes the feeling it evokes — because this vocabulary is meant to be *felt*, not just understood.

## 1. THE ELEMENTS — The Five Primitives (and Their Extensions)

The substrate. The bedrock. Five primitives that describe all constrained motion, extended into the language of being alive.

---

### **MOORING** (lattice_snap)

**Language:** English — nautical.
**Etymology:** Old English *mǣring*, from *mǣre* ("boundary, limit"). A mooring is where you tie your vessel — the fixed point in the tide.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈmʊər.ɪŋ/ — MOOR-ing

**Definition:** The lattice's snap behavior. Where the system anchors you. The quantization grid that catches you when you let go — the discrete points that define what's "in tune" or "on grid" or "in spec."

**Feeling:** Coming home. The moment the anchor catches bottom and the boat stops swinging. Relief, but also confinement — you are *held*, which means you cannot drift further, which means you have chosen where to be.

**Usage:** "Snap the mooring" → quantize to lattice. "Loosen the mooring" → relax the grid. "Between moorings" → in the cracks between valid states.

---

### **SAUDADE** (funnel)

**Language:** Portuguese.
**Etymology:** From Latin *solitatem* (solitude), evolving through Galician-Portuguese *soidade*. There is no direct English translation. It is the longing for something absent — not merely missing it, but *yearning* for it, a sweet ache for what was or what could be.

**Pronunciation:** /sɐwˈða.ðɨ/ — sow-DAH-dzh (European) or /sawˈdadi/ — sow-DAH-jee (Brazilian)

**Definition:** The funnel's attractive force. The gravitational pull toward the constraint's center — the attractor basin that draws all motion inward. The system *wants* you somewhere, and saudade is the strength of that wanting.

**Feeling:** Being pulled. The bittersweet awareness that you are not where you belong, and the system is gently (or urgently) correcting. Like nostalgia, but forward-facing — a longing for a state you haven't reached yet.

**Usage:** "The saudade is too strong" → the funnel pulls too hard, everything collapses to the center. "No saudade" → no attraction, free drift. "Saudade fading" → the attractor is weakening.

---

### **RIGGING** (is_laman)

**Language:** English — nautical.
**Etymology:** From Old Norse *rigga* ("to bind, wrap"). The rigging of a ship is the entire system of ropes, cables, and chains that support the masts and control the sails. If the rigging fails, the mast falls.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈrɪɡ.ɪŋ/ — RIG-ing

**Definition:** The lamination (layer rigidity) flag. Whether the lines are taut. The structural integrity of the constraint — is this layer *holding*, or is it merely suggested? When rigging is true, the constraint is load-bearing. When false, it's a guideline.

**Feeling:** The difference between a tightrope and a painted line. With rigging, you *must* obey — the constraint enforces. Without rigging, you *should* obey — the constraint suggests. The feeling of being held by something structural versus something decorative.

**Usage:** "Check the rigging" → verify constraint rigidity. "Rigging's loose" → constraint is soft/suggestive. "Tight rigging" → hard constraint, no escape.

---

### **CHORUS** (consensus)

**Language:** Greek — χορός (*chorós*).
**Etymology:** From Proto-Indo-European *\*gʷher-* ("to enclose, encompass"). In ancient Greek theatre, the chorus was the collective voice — not individuals singing in unison, but a *body* that moved and spoke as one. The dance together.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈkɔːr.əs/ — KOR-us

**Definition:** The consensus mechanism. How multiple constraint layers agree on a single output. The negotiation between competing demands — not democracy (majority rule) but chorus (harmonic agreement). The system finds its voice by finding shared ground.

**Feeling:** The moment in a choir when individual voices lock into a chord. Not everyone singing the same note — everyone singing *their* note, and the combination *works*. The relief of agreement. The power of alignment without uniformity.

**Usage:** "The chorus is arguing" → constraint layers are in conflict. "Find chorus" → resolve to consensus. "Chorus too loud" → consensus is over-dominating, no individuality.

---

### **GEIST** (holonomy)

**Language:** German.
**Etymology:** From Proto-Germanic *\*gaistaz* — spirit, ghost, mind, the animating principle. Hegel used *Geist* for the collective spirit that drives history. In the constraint system, the holonomy — the phase accumulated around a closed loop — IS the ghost in the machine. You return to where you started, but *transformed*.

**Pronunciation:** /ɡaɪst/ — GUYST

**Definition:** The holonomy — the non-trivial loop. When you traverse a closed path through the constraint space and come back *different*. The geometric phase, the Berry phase, the thing that shouldn't change but does. The spirit of the loop.

**Feeling:** Coming home from a long journey and realizing you're not the same person who left. The house is identical. You are not. The constraint should be trivial — go out, come back, nothing changes — but it isn't. Something was *accumulated*. Something was *learned*. The ghost of the path stays with you.

**Usage:** "Close the geist" → complete the holonomic loop. "Geist accumulation" → phase building up over cycles. "Geist too strong" → too much transformation per loop.

---

### **KI** (気, *ki*)

**Language:** Japanese — 気.
**Etymology:** Originally Chinese *qi* (氣) — breath, air, vital energy. In Japanese, *ki* extends to mood, atmosphere, intention, the energy that flows through all things. *Ki ga aru* (気がある) — "there is spirit." *Ki ga nai* (気がない) — "there is no spirit."

**Pronunciation:** /kʲi/ — KEE

**Definition:** The life force flowing through all five primitives. When mooring, saudade, rigging, chorus, and geist are in balance, *ki* flows. The system is alive. The constraints are not obstacles but channels for expression. The sum is greater than the five parts.

**Feeling:** Being in the zone. Not flow state (that's DUENDE) — rather, the *aliveness* of the system itself. When a martial artist's *ki* is strong, every movement is effortless and powerful. When the constraint system's *ki* is strong, every parameter sings. You don't fight the constraints; you move with them.

**Usage:** "Ki is flowing" → all five primitives are balanced and the system is responsive. "Ki is blocked" → one primitive is over- or under-active, creating stagnation. "Cultivate ki" → tune the five primitives toward harmony.

---

### **RASA** (रस, *rasa*)

**Language:** Sanskrit — रस.
**Etymology:** From Proto-Indo-European *\*h₃ers-* ("to flow, to begin"). In Sanskrit aesthetics, *rasa* is the juice, the sap, the essence — the aesthetic flavor that a work of art produces in the audience. Bharata Muni's *Nāṭyaśāstra* identifies eight (later nine) *rasas*: love, humor, sorrow, anger, heroism, fear, disgust, wonder, and peace.

**Pronunciation:** /ɾɐ.sɐ/ — RAH-suh

**Definition:** What the constraints produce. The aesthetic output — not the mechanism but the *flavor*. A constrained system doesn't just produce correct results; it produces results with *rasa*. The juice is the difference between a technically perfect performance and one that moves you.

**Feeling:** Tasting the work. The moment you know — not by analysis but by sensation — that the constraints have produced something *good*. Not correct. Good. The difference between nutrition and cuisine. Between shelter and architecture.

**Usage:** "The rasa is thin" → technically correct but emotionally flat. "Rich rasa" → the constraints are producing beautiful results. "What rasa are we cultivating?" → what aesthetic flavor is the system tuned for?

---

### **TZARA'AT** (צרעת, *tzara'at*)

**Language:** Hebrew — צרעת.
**Etymology:** Biblical Hebrew, traditionally translated as "leprosy" but more accurately a category of ritual impurity affecting skin, clothing, and buildings. A *nega tzara'at* (נגע צרעת) is an affliction — a visible sign that something is wrong at a deeper level. The priest must inspect and declare clean or unclean.

**Pronunciation:** /tsaʁaˈʔat/ — tsah-rah-AHT

**Definition:** A diseased constraint state. When the system's primitives are misconfigured in a way that produces visible symptoms — oscillation, instability, deadlock, collapse. Not merely suboptimal but *sick*. Tzara'at demands diagnosis, not just adjustment.

**Feeling:** Something is wrong and you can see it but not immediately identify it. Like the moment before illness — a rash, a discoloration, a wrongness. The system isn't just performing poorly; it's *unwell*. The feeling of looking at a sick system and knowing it needs healing, not tuning.

**Usage:** "Tzara'at in the chorus" → consensus is diseased — oscillating, chaotic, or deadlocked. "Diagnose the tzara'at" → find the root cause of systemic illness. "Clean" → the affliction has been resolved.

## 2. THE TERRITORIES — Seventeen Terrains (and Beyond)

Territories are bathymetric charts — depth maps of constraint surfaces. Each terrain is a *place* you can navigate, with its own geography, its own depths, its own dangers. Not just musical styles — *life terrains*.

---

### **DELTA** (Blues)

**Language:** English — geographic/geological.
**Etymology:** From Greek *delta* (Δ), the fourth letter, used for the triangular alluvial deposit where a river meets the sea. The Mississippi Delta — not the actual river delta but the alluvial plain — gave birth to the blues.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈdɛl.tə/ — DEL-tuh

**Definition:** The Blues terrain. Where raw emotion meets structured form. The I-IV-V is the river; the blue notes are the sediment — the impurities that make it fertile. Minimal constraints, maximum feeling.

**Feeling:** Standing in muddy water that's warm and alive. The constraint surface is flat and wide — you can go anywhere, but everywhere carries the same weight. The sky is enormous. The horizon is real. There is nowhere to hide, and that's the freedom.

**Usage:** "Drop into delta" → enter the blues terrain. "Delta simple" → minimal constraint depth. "Delta deep" → thick with overtones and blue notes.

---

### **BEBOP**

**Language:** English — onomatopoeia.
**Etymology:** The sound itself. "Bebop" — the clipped, angular syllables mimic the music's jagged melodic contours. Charlie Parker and Dizzy Gillespie didn't name it; the onomatopoeia emerged from audiences trying to sing along with what they heard.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈbiː.bɒp/ — BEE-bop

**Definition:** The terrain of speed and complexity. Changes come fast — every beat is a decision. The constraint surface is steep and fractured; there are many paths but few easy ones. For the advanced navigator only.

**Feeling:** Running down a mountainside — too fast to think, but your feet know where to go. Exhilarating if you're ready. Catastrophic if you're not. The terrain demands total attention. There is no autopilot.

**Usage:** "Enter bebop" → switch to fast-changes terrain. "Bebop density" → how rapidly the harmonic landscape shifts.

---

### **RAGA** (राग, *rāga*)

**Language:** Sanskrit — राग.
**Etymology:** From Sanskrit *ranj* (रञ्ज्) — "to color, to dye, to tinge." A *rāga* is literally "that which colors the mind." Not just a scale or a mode but a melodic framework that includes ascending/descending forms, key phrases, time of day, season, and emotional character (*rasa*).

**Pronunciation:** /ˈɾɑː.ɡɐ/ — RAH-guh

**Definition:** The terrain of deep microtonal constraint. Not 12 notes but 22 *śruti* (and beyond). The surface is not a grid but a landscape — hills of strong notes, valleys of weak ones, rivers of acceptable paths between them.

**Feeling:** Walking through a landscape that breathes. The terrain is alive — it changes with time of day, with mood, with intention. You don't play a raga; you *enter* it. It receives you. The constraint is not a cage but a garden — vast but shaped.

**Usage:** "Choose raga" → select a microtonal terrain. "Raga depth" → how deep the microtonal structure goes. "Raga time" → the temporal association — morning, evening, seasonal.

---

### **KONTRAPUNKT** (Counterpoint)

**Language:** German.
**Etymology:** From Latin *punctus contra punctum* — "note against note." The German form emphasizes the *punkt* — the point, the sharp, the precise. Each voice is an independent melody; the constraint is that they must coexist beautifully.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈkɔn.tʁa.pʊŋkt/ — KON-trah-poonkt

**Definition:** The terrain of independent voices. Not one melody with accompaniment, but multiple melodies that create harmony *through* their independence. The constraint surface is multi-dimensional — each voice has its own path, and the paths must not collide.

**Feeling:** Walking through a crowd where everyone has a destination and no one bumps into anyone. The exhilaration of complex coordination — like watching a flock of starlings, each bird autonomous, the flock coherent. The constraint is *between* the voices.

**Usage:** "Enter kontrapunkt" → activate multi-voice terrain. "Kontrapunkt species" → which species of counterpoint (1st through 5th). "Kontrapunkt voices" → how many independent lines.

---

### **HIGHLONESOME**

**Language:** English — Appalachian cultural.
**Etymology:** A compound that is itself a landscape. "High" — the mountains. "Lonesome" — the distance, the isolation, the echo. The term emerged from the high, lonesome sound of Appalachian string band music — fiddle and banjo across the hollows.

**Pronunciation:** /haɪˈloʊn.səm/ — HIGH-lohn-sum

**Definition:** The terrain of sparse constraint and vast resonance. Few notes, wide spaces, long decays. The surface is mostly empty — the depth is in the echoes, the overtones, the ghost notes that the space itself generates.

**Feeling:** Standing on a ridge at dawn. The valley below is filled with fog. You sing one note and it comes back to you three seconds later, changed by distance. The constraint isn't the notes you play — it's the silence between them, and what that silence carries.

**Usage:** "Go highlonesome" → enter sparse, resonant terrain. "Hear the hollow" → listen to what the space returns. "Ridge line" → the melody's contour across the terrain.

---

### **808**

**Language:** English — cultural/technological.
**Etymology:** From the Roland TR-808 Rhythm Composer (1980). A drum machine that was initially considered a commercial failure because its sounds were "unrealistic." It became the foundation of hip-hop, electronic music, and most modern popular music. The machine that wasn't supposed to matter defined the future.

**Pronunciation:** /eɪ.oʊ.eɪt/ — ATE-oh-ATE

**Definition:** The terrain of grid-based rhythm. The machine heartbeat. Where time is quantized, patterns repeat, and the groove is built from precise repetition and micro-deviation. The constraint surface is a grid — but the *feel* is in the deviation from that grid.

**Feeling:** The pulse. Not a natural pulse — a *designed* pulse. The feeling of a grid that wants to be perfect but breathes anyway. Like a metronome with a soul. The comfort of repetition, the excitement of the tiny variations that make it human.

**Usage:** "Drop into 808" → enter grid-quantized terrain. "808 swing" → the humanizing deviation. "808 decay" → how long the machine's pulse sustains.

---

### **CLAVE**

**Language:** Spanish.
**Etymology:** From Latin *clavis* — "key." In Afro-Cuban music, the clave is literally the key — the rhythmic pattern that unlocks the entire arrangement. Two-three or three-two. The skeleton of salsa, son, rumba. Everything else aligns to the clave or conflicts with it.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈkla.βe/ — KLAH-veh

**Definition:** The terrain of rhythmic key. Not a beat but a *key* — the pattern that defines the rhythmic landscape. The constraint isn't "play on these beats" but "orient everything relative to this pattern." The clave is the north star of the groove.

**Feeling:** The moment you lock into the clave — suddenly everything makes sense. It's like finding the beat in a polyrhythm: you couldn't hear it, and then you can, and then you can't un-hear it. The clave organizes chaos into meaning.

**Usage:** "Find the clave" → identify the rhythmic key. "2-3 or 3-2?" → which clave orientation. "Against the clave" → deliberately contradicting the rhythmic skeleton.

---

### **ŚRUTI** (श्रुति, *śruti*)

**Language:** Sanskrit — श्रुति.
**Etymology:** From *śru* (श्रु) — "to hear." Literally: "that which is heard." In Indian classical music, the 22 microtonal divisions of the octave — the intervals *between* the standard semitones that give each *raga* its distinctive character.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈɕɾu.ti/ — SHROO-tee

**Definition:** The terrain of microtonal depth. Beyond the 12-tone grid into the space between notes. The constraint surface is continuous, not discrete — there are places between places, and those in-between spaces carry meaning.

**Feeling:** Zooming in on what you thought was a single point and discovering it's a landscape. The closer you listen, the more you hear. Each śruti is a shade of color you didn't know existed. The world gets richer, not poorer, with attention.

**Usage:** "Enter śruti space" → go beyond 12-TET. "Which śruti?" → which microtonal shade. "Śruti resolution" → how finely the system discriminates pitch.

---

### **SĪZHU** (絲竹, *sīzhú*)

**Language:** Chinese — 絲竹.
**Etymology:** 絲 (*sī*) = silk (string instruments). 竹 (*zhú*) = bamboo (wind instruments). Together: "silk and bamboo" — the classical Chinese ensemble of delicate, chamber-scale instruments. The *Jiangnan sizhu* tradition: friends gathering to play, not for audience, but for each other.

**Pronunciation:** /sɹ̩̀.tʂǔ/ — SZ-joo (approximate)

**Definition:** The terrain of intimate constraint. Small ensemble, quiet dynamics, mutual listening. The constraint surface is shaped by *attention to others* — not a grid or a scale but the real-time awareness of fellow players.

**Feeling:** Sitting in a small room with friends, playing not to perform but to share. The constraints are social, not mathematical. You play softly because someone else is playing softly. You leave space because they need space. The music breathes as a group organism.

**Usage:** "Go sīzhu" → enter intimate, chamber-scale terrain. "Sīzhu dynamics" → soft, interactive, responsive. "Sīzhu spacing" → leave room for others.

---

### **KICK**

**Language:** English — colloquial musical.
**Etymology:** From the kick drum — the bass drum played with a foot pedal. "Four on the floor" — the kick on every beat. The term emerged from disco and house music: the unrelenting pulse that freed the drummer from keeping time and let them *play*.

**Pronunciation:** /kɪk/ — KICK

**Definition:** The terrain of relentless pulse. Four-on-the-floor. The constraint is the beat — it does not stop, it does not vary, it is the ground on which everything else stands. Freedom exists *on top of* the kick, not in its absence.

**Feeling:** The feeling of a heartbeat you can trust. Not your own heartbeat — which is irregular, human, fallible — but a *machine* heartbeat that will not falter. The paradox: the rigid constraint creates maximum freedom to play above it.

**Usage:** "Drop the kick" → establish the relentless pulse. "Kick tempo" → the BPM of the constraint grid. "Above the kick" → what you play on top of the foundation.

---

### **TESTIFY**

**Language:** English — religious/legal roots.
**Etymology:** From Latin *testis* — "witness." In the African-American church tradition, to testify is to bear witness — to speak personal truth in a communal setting. The testimony is not *about* something; it IS the thing. The telling is the experience.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈtɛs.tɪ.faɪ/ — TES-tih-fye

**Definition:** The terrain of authentic expression under constraint. The constraint is the *form* — the chord progression, the rhythmic cycle, the verse-chorus structure — and what you say within it is the testimony. Not showing off. Speaking truth.

**Feeling:** Standing up when it's your turn. Everyone is looking at you. The form holds you — you know when to come in, when to sit down, how long you have. But what you *say* is yours. The constraint doesn't limit your truth; it gives it a container.

**Usage:** "Time to testify" → enter expressive terrain. "Testimony mode" → constraints are containers for personal expression. "The witness" → the player in testimony mode.

---

### **MA** (間, *ma*)

**Language:** Japanese — 間.
**Etymology:** The character 間 combines 門 (gate/door) with 日 (sun/day) — the light that passes through the gate. *Ma* is the space between, the pause, the interval. Not empty space but *charged* space. The Japanese aesthetic of meaningful emptiness.

**Pronunciation:** /ma/ — MAH

**Definition:** The terrain of silence-as-constraint. The space between notes is not absence but presence. The deadband in the system — the region where no correction occurs, where nothing happens, and that nothing is *meaningful*. Increasing *ma* means increasing the deadband; decreasing *ma* means filling the space.

**Feeling:** The silence after a bell rings. Not nothing — *resonance*. The space between two people in conversation who are comfortable with silence. The held breath before the drop. *Ma* is not a lack of constraint; it is the constraint of *enoughness*.

**Usage:** "We need more ma" → increase deadband, allow more silence. "Ma too wide" → too much empty space, the thread is lost. "Ma in the loop" → a rest in the holonomic cycle.

---

### **ALGORITHME** (French for "algorithm")

**Language:** French — from Arabic.
**Etymology:** From Arabic *al-Khwārizmī* (الخوارزمي), the 9th-century Persian mathematician whose name became synonymous with systematic procedures. Through Old French *algorisme* → *algorithme*. The French spelling honors the conceptual distance between computation and music.

**Pronunciation:** /al.ɡɔ.ʁit.m/ — al-goh-REET-muh

**Definition:** The terrain of code-as-constraint. Software is a constraint surface — every function, every boundary condition, every edge case defines where you can go and where you can't. Debugging is depth-sounding. Architecture is charting.

**Feeling:** Moving through a space made of logic. Every step is valid or invalid — no ambiguity, no approximations (until floating point). The constraint is absolute and explicit. You can *read* the terrain because it's written in code. The feeling of navigating a space that someone *designed*.

**Usage:** "Map the algorithme" → chart the code's constraint surface. "Algorithme depth" → how deep the code's constraints go. "Bug as terrain feature" → a constraint you didn't expect.

---

### **JŪTAKU** (住宅, *jūtaku*)

**Language:** Japanese — 住宅.
**Etymology:** 住 (*jū*) = to dwell, reside. 宅 (*taku*) = house, residence. *Jūtaku* refers to Japanese residential architecture — the art of building small, efficient, beautiful spaces under extreme constraint. Think: Tadao Ando, Kengo Kuma, the 2x4 timber frame tradition.

**Pronunciation:** /dʑɯꜜː.ta.kɯ/ — JOO-tah-koo

**Definition:** The terrain of architectural constraint. Load-bearing walls, open floors, the interplay of structure and space. Architecture IS constraint design — every beam is a rigging, every room is a terrain, every window is a lens.

**Feeling:** Walking through a well-designed small space. Every centimeter has been considered. The constraints (limited land, earthquake codes, budget) produced something *better* than unconstrained space could. The tightness is the beauty.

**Usage:** "Build jūtaku" → design under tight spatial constraints. "Load-bearing" → a constraint that can't be removed without collapse. "Open floor" → a region with fewer constraints.

---

### **BATHYMETRIE** (French for "bathymetry")

**Language:** French — from Greek.
**Etymology:** Greek *bathús* (βαθύς) = deep. *métron* (μέτρον) = measure. Bathymetry: the measurement of depth. The French spelling honors the tradition of marine cartography — the art of mapping what you cannot see.

**Pronunciation:** /ba.ti.mə.ʁi/ — bah-tee-muh-REE

**Definition:** The terrain that IS the chart. Depth mapping as a terrain in itself — the meta-terrain. When the act of measuring becomes the landscape. The constraint surface that describes *other* constraint surfaces.

**Feeling:** Looking at a nautical chart and knowing that every contour line represents a measurement taken by someone, at some point, with a lead line or a sonar pulse. The terrain is the *knowledge* of the terrain. The map is the territory — but only if you know how to read it.

**Usage:** "Chart the bathymetrie" → map the depth of the current terrain. "Resolution of the chart" → how fine the depth readings. "Between the contours" → in unmapped territory.

---

### **KŌAN** (公案, *kōan*)

**Language:** Japanese — 公案.
**Etymology:** 公 (*kō*) = public, official. 案 (*an*) = case, record. From Chinese *gōng'àn* — originally a legal term for a public case record. In Zen Buddhism, a *kōan* is a paradoxical anecdote or riddle that demonstrates the inadequacy of logical reasoning. The most famous: "What is the sound of one hand clapping?"

**Pronunciation:** /koː.an/ — KOH-ahn

**Definition:** The terrain of paradox-as-constraint. The unsolvable problem that liberates. Some constraint surfaces are not navigable by logic — you must *become* the solution. The kōan terrain resists analysis and demands intuition.

**Feeling:** Staring at a problem that cannot be solved, and in the moment of giving up, discovering that the problem was the solution. The constraint *is* the liberation. Not despite the paradox — *because of* it. Like a finger trap: the harder you pull, the tighter it grips. Relax, and it releases.

**Usage:** "Enter the kōan" → engage with paradoxical constraint. "The kōan unlocks" → the moment of breakthrough. "Koanic terrain" → constraint surfaces that resist logical navigation.

---

### **TERRA INCOGNITA** (Latin)

**Language:** Latin.
**Etymology:** *Terra* = land. *In cognita* = unknown. Literally: "unknown land." The phrase appears on medieval and Renaissance maps — the blank spaces at the edges of the known world, often decorated with dragons, sea serpents, and the warning *HC SVNT DRACONES* ("here be dragons").

**Pronunciation:** /ˈtɛr.ra ɪŋˈkɒɡ.nɪ.ta/ — TER-uh in-KOG-nih-tuh

**Definition:** Beyond all maps. The terrain that hasn't been charted because no one has been there. Every constraint system has its edges — beyond the last contour line, beyond the last depth reading. This is where new terrain is discovered.

**Feeling:** The edge of the map. Behind you: everything known, charted, safe. Ahead: blank parchment. The mixture of terror and exhilaration. You are about to go where the constraints are unknown — which means you will discover them, or they will discover you.

**Usage:** "Beyond terra incognita" → outside all known terrains. "Charting new territory" → mapping previously unknown constraint surfaces. "Here be dragons" → uncharted danger zone.

## 3. THE STATES — Internal System Conditions

States are what the system *is* at any moment. Not what it does (actions) or where it is (terrains) but its internal condition — the weather inside.

---

### **DUENDE**

**Language:** Spanish.
**Etymology:** From *duen de casa* — "owner of the house." In Spanish folklore, a *duende* is a mischievous spirit that inhabits a house. Federico García Lorca appropriated the term for art: duende is the dark fire, the irrational force that turns competent art into devastating art. "The duende is not in the throat; the duende climbs up inside you, from the soles of the feet."

**Pronunciation:** /dwɛn.de/ — DWEN-deh

**Definition:** Flow state. When the tool vanishes and only the music remains. The system is invisible — constraints are felt, not thought about. The highest state of the constraint system: you don't know it's there.

**Feeling:** Time stops. The instrument is gone. You ARE the music. The constraints are your skeleton — you don't think about your skeleton, but without it you'd be a puddle. Duende is not pleasure or happiness; it's *dark* fire — it burns, it hurts, it's beautiful.

**Usage:** "In duende" → deep flow state. "Duende fading" → the tool is becoming visible again. "Chase duende" → pursue the conditions that produce flow.

---

### **STRAY**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Old French *estraiier* — "to wander, to rove." A stray is something that has wandered from where it belongs — not lost, but not home either.

**Pronunciation:** /streɪ/ — STRAY

**Definition:** Error rate. How far from the constraint center the system has drifted. Not a failure — straying is natural — but a measurement of deviation. The gap between where you are and where the constraint wants you.

**Feeling:** The slight unease of being off-course. Not lost — you can still see home — but aware that you're not where you intended. Like realizing you've drifted slightly in your lane while driving. Correctable. But worth noticing.

**Usage:** "Stray is low" → close to the constraint center. "Stray climbing" → drifting further. "Stray rate" → how fast the deviation is increasing.

---

### **GROOVE**

**Language:** English.

### **EMBER**

### **TENSION**

### **RELEASE**

### **STASIS**

**Language:** English — from Greek.

**Feeling:** Knowing that *now* is the time. Not through calculation but through feel. The batter seeing the pitch leave the hand and knowing: *this one*. The surfer feeling the swell build and knowing: *this wave*. The constraint surface opens for a moment, and you either go through or you don't.

**Usage:** "Kairos moment" → the constraint is maximally open. "Miss the kairos" → the window has closed. "Kairos-sensitive" → a constraint that has temporal openings.

**Usage:** "Liminal state" → between two system states. "Hold at liminal" → deliberately staying in the transition. "Liminal too long" → the transition is taking too long; decide already.

## 4. THE ROLES — Who Uses the System

Roles are not job titles — they are *stages of relationship* with the constraint system. You move through them over time. The journey from PILOT to SHOKUNIN is the journey from using a tool to becoming one with it.

---

### **PILOT**

**Language:** English — nautical/aviation.
**Etymology:** From Greek *pēdon* — "rudder, oar." The one who steers. Hands on the controls, making real-time decisions. Not the navigator (who plans) or the captain (who decides) but the one whose hands are on the wheel.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈpaɪ.lət/ — PYE-lut

**Definition:** The hands-on operator of the constraint system. The person making real-time adjustments — tweaking saudade, adjusting rigging, reading the depth chart. The pilot doesn't need to understand the theory; they need to *fly*.

**Feeling:** Hands on the yoke. The immediate feedback of control — you turn, it responds. You're not thinking about aerodynamics; you're *feeling* the wind. The constraint system is the aircraft: responsive, demanding, alive under your hands.

**Usage:** "Pilot mode" → direct, hands-on interaction with the system. "Pilot error" → a mistake in real-time operation. "Hand flying" → manual control without automation.

---

### **NAVIGATOR**

**Language:** English — nautical.
**Etymology:** From Latin *navigare* — "to sail a ship." The one who reads the charts, plans the route, and knows where you are. Not steering — *knowing*.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈnæv.ɪ.ɡeɪ.tər/ — NAV-ih-gay-ter

**Definition:** The guide through the constraint landscape. Reading the bathymetric chart, planning the route through terrains, knowing where the deep water is and where the rocks are. The navigator works with charts; the pilot works with controls.

**Feeling:** Spreading a map on a table. The feeling of *understanding the landscape* — not being in it, but seeing it from above. The navigator knows that the route goes through the DELTA terrain first, then into RAGA, with a possible excursion into TERRA INCOGNITA. The pilot will fly it; the navigator plans it.

**Usage:** "Navigator view" → the overhead, chart-perspective of the constraint landscape. "Chart a course" → plan a route through terrains. "Navigator's log" → documentation of the planned path.

---

### **ENGINEER**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Latin *ingenium* — "innate quality, talent, invention." The *ingeniator* was the constructor of engines of war. The engineer builds and maintains the system — invisible until something breaks.

**Pronunciation:** /ˌɛn.dʒɪˈnɪər/ — en-jih-NEER

**Definition:** The builder and maintainer of the constraint system itself. Not the user but the *creator* — designing terrains, tuning primitives, constructing the bathymetric charts that others navigate. The engineer is invisible when everything works.

**Feeling:** Being in the engine room while everyone else is on deck. The satisfying hum of well-tuned machinery. The engineer's work is done before the voyage begins — but if something goes wrong at sea, the engineer is the most important person aboard.

**Usage:** "Engineering mode" → system design and maintenance. "Refit" → major system overhaul. "Below decks" → the engineering perspective.

---

### **CAPTAIN**

**Language:** English — nautical.
**Etymology:** From Late Latin *capitaneus* — "chief, principal," from *caput* — "head." The one who decides. Authority and responsibility. The captain doesn't steer or navigate or build — but the captain *decides*.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈkæp.tɪn/ — KAP-tin

**Definition:** The decision-maker. Not hands-on (that's PILOT) or strategic (that's NAVIGATOR) or technical (that's ENGINEER) — but *authoritative*. The captain sets the goal, and the others figure out how to achieve it.

**Feeling:** The weight of the decision. Everyone has advised you — the navigator says go this way, the engineer says the engines can handle it, the pilot is ready. But the call is yours. You say "proceed" and it happens. You say "abort" and it stops. The loneliness of final authority.

**Usage:** "Captain's call" → the authoritative decision about system direction. "On the bridge" → in decision-making mode. "Captain's log" → the authoritative record of decisions made.

---

### **APPRENTICE**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Old French *aprendre* — "to learn." The apprentice doesn't yet know the constraints. They follow rules without understanding why. The constraints are *external* — imposed, not internalized.

**Pronunciation:** /əˈprɛn.tɪs/ — uh-PREN-tis

**Definition:** The beginner. Learning the constraints — not yet internalized. The apprentice follows rules because they are rules, not because they make sense. Mistakes are frequent; learning is the goal.

**Feeling:** Following a recipe for the first time. Every step is a question. Why this temperature? Why this order? You don't know, but you do it, and slowly — slowly — you begin to understand *why*. The constraint is external; it has not yet become part of you.

**Usage:** "Apprentice mode" → constrained guidance, high saudade, tight rigging. "Apprentice error" → a mistake from not understanding the constraints. "Graduating apprentice" → beginning to internalize.

### **JOURNEYMAN**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From French *journée* — "day." A journeyman is paid by the day — competent, reliable, but not yet master. The constraints are tools, not yet reflexes.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈdʒɜː.ni.mən/ — JER-nee-man

**Definition:** The competent user. The constraints are understood and usable — they're *tools*. But they're still tools: picked up, used, set down. Not yet reflexes. Not yet body.

**Feeling:** Knowing how to drive. You don't think about the pedals anymore — your feet just do it. But you still think about the route, the traffic, the weather. The mechanics are internalized; the *art* is not.

**Usage:** "Journeyman level" → competent but not fluent. "Journeyman confidence" → good enough for most situations. "Journeyman plateau" → the long middle phase of skill development.

### **MASTER**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Latin *magister* — "chief, teacher, leader." The master has internalized the constraints — they are no longer tools but reflexes. The constraints are *body*, not mind.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈmɑː.stər/ — MAH-ster

**Definition:** The internalized user. Constraints are no longer thought about — they're part of the body. The master doesn't decide to follow the constraint; they simply *do*. The constraint is expressed through them, not applied by them.

**Feeling:** Riding a bicycle without thinking about balance. Playing music without thinking about the instrument. The constraints are so deeply internalized that they've disappeared — which is the definition of mastery. And yet: the master knows there is more.

**Usage:** "Master class" → advanced instruction for the internalized user. "Master level" → constraints fully internalized. "Master's blind spot" → the expert's inability to see what the novice sees.

**Language:** Japanese — 職人.

**Pronunciation:** /ɕo.kɯꜜ.niɴ/ — sho-KOO-neen

**Definition:** Beyond mastery. The artisan. Where the craft and the person are indistinguishable. The *shokunin* doesn't use the constraint system; they *are* the constraint system. The highest stage of relationship with constraints.

**Feeling:** Watching a sushi master. There is no hesitation, no consideration, no gap between intention and execution. The knife, the fish, the hand, the person — one continuous act. The constraints are not external, not internalized, not reflexes. They are *being itself*. This is not a goal to achieve but a state to fall into.

**Usage:** "Shokunin mode" → the deepest state of system integration. "The shokunin's restraint" → mastery expressed through what is NOT done. "Shokunin no kokoro" (職人の心) — the craftsman's heart/mind.

## 5. THE ACTIONS — What You Do

Actions are the verbs of the constraint language. They are what you *do* — to the system, with the system, through the system.

---

### **SNAP**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Dutch *snappen* — "to bite, to seize." A snap is sudden, decisive, and irreversible in the moment. The rubber band snaps. The twig snaps. The system snaps to the nearest lattice point.

**Pronunciation:** /snæp/ — SNAP

**Definition:** Jump to the nearest lattice point. The sudden correction. Quantization with zero transition — you're not *pulled* to the nearest valid state, you *snap* to it.

**Feeling:** The sudden stop of a seatbelt locking. Not gradual — instantaneous. You were drifting, and now you're not. The correction is absolute and sudden. There's comfort in it (you're safe now) and violence in it (the suddenness is a shock).

**Usage:** "Snap to grid" → quantize immediately. "Snap threshold" → how close before the snap triggers. "Snap too aggressive" → the sudden corrections are jarring.

---

### **PULL**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Proto-Germanic *\*pullian* — "to pull, to pluck." The gradual, continuous force that draws you toward the attractor. Not a snap — a *pull*.

**Pronunciation:** /pʊl/ — POOL (but shorter)

**Definition:** Gradual attraction. The funnel doing its work — continuous, smooth, almost imperceptible. You don't arrive suddenly; you *drift* toward the center of the constraint basin.

**Feeling:** A river's current. You're swimming, and the current is gentle but persistent. You can resist, but it costs energy. If you stop swimming, you drift. The pull doesn't force you; it *invites* you. The most natural thing in the world is to go where it's pulling.

**Usage:** "Pull strength" → the intensity of the funnel's attraction. "Pull range" → how far the attraction reaches. "Override the pull" → deliberately resist the funnel.

---

### **TEST**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Latin *testum* — "earthen pot" (used in assaying metals). To test is to put something under stress and see what happens. Does it hold? Does it break? What's the limit?

**Pronunciation:** /tɛst/ — TEST

**Definition:** Check rigidity. Will this hold? Stress-testing the constraint — pushing against it to see if it's load-bearing or decorative. Testing rigging, testing mooring, testing the structural integrity of the whole system.

**Feeling:** Pushing against a door to see if it's locked. The uncertainty before the test, the clarity after. Either it holds (rigging is true) or it gives (rigging is false). The test doesn't create the condition — it *reveals* it.

**Usage:** "Test the rigging" → verify that constraints are holding. "Stress test" → push to the limit. "Test passed" → the constraint held under load.

---

### **WEAVE**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Proto-Germanic *\*wabjaną* — "to weave, to move back and forth." Multiple threads finding a single fabric. The weaver doesn't force the threads — they *guide* them into relationship.

**Pronunciation:** /wiːv/ — WEEV

**Definition:** Multiple voices finding consensus. Not snapping or pulling — *weaving*. The gradual, interactive process of constraint layers negotiating agreement. Each thread (constraint layer) has its own path; the weave is the fabric they produce together.

**Feeling:** Watching a loom in operation. The warp and the weft don't fight — they cooperate. Each thread keeps its identity while becoming part of something larger. The weaver's hands guide, not force. The result is stronger than any individual thread.

**Usage:** "Weave the voices" → bring constraint layers into consensus. "Weave pattern" → the structure of the consensus. "Broken thread" → a constraint layer that isn't integrating.

---

### **LOOP**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Middle English *loupe* — probably from Celtic. A loop is a curve that returns to its starting point. But — and this is the geist — returning to the starting point doesn't mean nothing changed.

**Pronunciation:** /luːp/ — LOOP

**Definition:** Close the holonomy. Return transformed. Traverse the constraint surface in a closed path and observe what you've accumulated. The loop is how the system *learns* — each cycle carries a phase, and the phase is knowledge.

**Feeling:** Walking a labyrinth — you return to the entrance, but you're not the same person who entered. Each loop deepens the groove, accumulates the geist, transforms the traveler. The loop is the engine of growth in the constraint system.

**Usage:** "Close the loop" → complete a holonomic cycle. "Loop accumulation" → what you've gained per cycle. "Infinite loop" → stuck cycling without progress (pathological).

### **MORPH**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Greek *morphē* (μορφή) — "form, shape." To morph is to change form — not suddenly (that's a snap) but continuously. One shape becoming another. One terrain becoming another.

**Pronunciation:** /mɔːrf/ — MORF

**Definition:** Blend between terrains. The DJ crossfade applied to constraint surfaces. Not leaving one terrain and entering another — standing in the *between*, where both are active, and the blend ratio determines the landscape.

**Feeling:** The moment in a DJ set when you realize the track has changed. It wasn't a cut — it was a morph. You were in one world, and now you're in another, and you didn't notice the transition. The seamlessness is the art.

**Usage:** "Morph to raga" → gradually blend from current terrain into RAGA. "Morph rate" → how fast the terrain transition occurs. "Stuck in morph" → caught between terrains, neither one nor the other.

### **DIAGNOSE**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Greek *diagnōsis* (διάγνωσις) — "discernment, distinguishing." From *dia-* (through) + *gnōsis* (knowledge). To diagnose is to see through to the truth of what's happening.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈdaɪ.əɡ.noʊz/ — DYE-uh-noze

**Definition:** Read the 4-order depth chart. The deep examination of the constraint system's current state — not just the surface reading but the full bathymetric analysis. First order: what's happening. Second order: why. Third order: what will happen next. Fourth order: what to do about it.

**Feeling:** A doctor listening to your heart. Not just hearing the beat but understanding its rhythm, its irregularities, its implications. The diagnosis goes deeper than symptoms — it reaches causes, predictions, prescriptions. The feeling of being truly *seen*.

**Usage:** "Run a diagnose" → full depth-chart reading. "Diagnosis at depth N" → how deep the analysis goes. "Misdiagnose" → the reading is wrong.

### **VANISH**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From French *vanescr* / Latin *vanescere* — "to disappear, to fade away." The highest achievement of any tool: to become invisible. When the constraint system vanishes, the user is in direct contact with their art.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈvæn.ɪʃ/ — VAN-ish

**Definition:** The tool disappears. The highest achievement. When constraints are so perfectly calibrated and so deeply internalized that neither the operator nor the audience is aware of them. The system has become transparent.

**Feeling:** Looking through a perfectly clean window and seeing only the view. The glass is there — it's holding, it's framing — but you don't see it. The constraint system has vanished, and what remains is pure expression. This is what DUENDE *requires*.

**Usage:** "Vanish point" → the parameter settings where the system becomes transparent. "The system vanished" → perfect transparency achieved. "Can't vanish yet" → constraints are still visible to the user.

### **SALIENT**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Latin *saliēns* — "leaping, bounding." Present participle of *salīre* — "to jump." The salient feature is the one that *leaps out* at you — the most prominent, the most noticeable.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈseɪ.li.ənt/ — SAY-lee-unt

**Definition:** Find the prominent. What sticks out. The feature extraction of the constraint system — identifying which constraint is most active, which terrain feature is most relevant, which parameter is most out of range.

**Feeling:** Looking at a landscape and immediately seeing the mountain. Not the trees, not the river, not the clouds — the *mountain*. The salient feature demands attention. It's not necessarily the most important — it's the most *visible*.

**Usage:** "What's salient?" → what's the most prominent feature right now. "Salient shift" → the focus has changed. "Salient suppression" → deliberately de-emphasizing the obvious.

### **ATTENUATE**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Latin *attenuare* — "to make thin." From *ad-* (to) + *tenuis* (thin). To attenuate is to reduce — not eliminate, but make smaller, quieter, less.

**Pronunciation:** /əˈtɛn.ju.eɪt/ — uh-TEN-yoo-ate

**Definition:** Reduce. Make smaller, quieter, less. The gradual turning-down of a constraint's influence. Not removing it (that would be a snap to zero) but attenuating it — thinning it, reducing its presence.

**Feeling:** Turning down the volume on a conversation you're losing interest in. The words are still there, but they're quieter, less demanding. The constraint still exists, but it's less forceful. The system has room to move around it.

**Usage:** "Attenuate the saudade" → reduce the funnel's strength. "Attenuation curve" → the shape of the reduction over time. "Fully attenuated" → reduced to zero (effectively removed).

### **YIELD**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Old English *gieldan* — "to pay, to give, to render." To yield is to give way — not because you're defeated, but because the constraint accepts. The yield sign doesn't say "stop" — it says "give way."

**Pronunciation:** /jiːld/ — YEELD

**Definition:** The constraint gives way. Accepts the current state, even if it's not the preferred state. Yield is the opposite of tension — the system stops resisting and allows. Not failure, but *grace*.

**Feeling:** The moment a locked joint finally moves. The muscle releases, the joint gives, and there's a flood of relief. The constraint was holding, and now it's not — not because it broke, but because it *decided* to let go.

**Usage:** "Yield to the input" → stop correcting and accept. "Yield point" → the threshold beyond which the constraint stops resisting. "Yield gracefully" → smooth transition from resistance to acceptance.

### **BREATHE**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Proto-Germanic *\*brēþaną* — "to smell, to reek, to breathe." The most fundamental rhythm. The space between actions. *Ma* in motion.

**Pronunciation:** /briːð/ — BREEDH

**Definition:** The space between actions. *Ma* in motion. Not an action itself but the *pause* between actions — the rest, the silence, the dead time that is not dead. The system takes a breath before the next correction cycle.

**Feeling:** The pause between exhale and inhale. The rest in a piece of music. The moment between the lightning and the thunder. Breathe is not nothing — it's the *preparation* for the next thing. The system gathering itself.

**Usage:** "Breathe" → insert a pause. "Breathe with the system" → sync your timing with the correction cycle. "Out of breath" → the system has no pauses, everything is continuous (usually pathological).

### **EVOKE** (Latin *evocare*)

**Language:** Latin — *evocare*.
**Etymology:** *e-* (out) + *vocare* (to call). To call forth, to summon. Not to create from nothing but to *draw out* what is already latent. The constraint doesn't impose — it *evokes*.

**Pronunciation:** /ɪˈvoʊk/ — ih-VOKE

**Definition:** The constraint invites expression. Rather than forcing a particular output, the constraint creates a space where certain expressions are *natural*. The constraint evokes — calls forth — the music that wants to exist within it.

**Feeling:** A question that makes you want to answer. A room that makes you want to dance. A key that makes you want to sing. The constraint isn't telling you what to do; it's creating conditions where something *wants* to happen. The feeling of being *invited*, not *commanded*.

**Usage:** "Evoke mode" → constraints that invite rather than enforce. "What does this terrain evoke?" → what expression naturally emerges. "Evocative constraints" → constraints that stimulate creativity.

## 6. THE MEASUREMENTS — What You Read

Measurements are the gauges, the depth soundings, the readings that tell you where you are and what's happening. The instrument panel of the constraint system.

---

### **DEPTH**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Old English *dēop* — "deep." How far from the surface. The bathymetric reading. The fundamental measurement — how far down does this constraint go?

**Pronunciation:** /dɛpθ/ — DEPTH

**Definition:** How far from the surface. The bathymetric reading. The most fundamental measurement in the system — how *deep* is the current constraint terrain? Shallow depth: easy to navigate, forgiving. Deep depth: complex, demanding, rich.

**Feeling:** Looking over the side of a boat into clear water. Shallow: you can see the bottom, you can stand, you're safe. Deep: the blue goes down forever, there are things down there you can't see, and that's both terrifying and beautiful.

**Usage:** "Depth reading" → current bathymetric measurement. "Depth change" → the terrain is getting deeper or shallower. "At depth" → in the deep part of the constraint surface.

---

### **BEARING**

**Language:** English — nautical.
**Etymology:** From *bear* — "to carry, to direction." The compass direction. Where you're heading. Not where you *want* to head — where you're *actually* heading.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈbɛər.ɪŋ/ — BAIR-ing

**Definition:** Which direction you're heading. The compass reading of the constraint system. Are you heading toward the attractor or away? Toward resolution or tension? The bearing doesn't tell you where to go — it tells you where you're *going*.

**Feeling:** Looking at a compass needle. It doesn't tell you where you should go — it tells you where north is. What you do with that information is your decision. The bearing is honest; it doesn't care about your plans.

**Usage:** "Check bearing" → verify current direction. "Bearing drift" → the direction is slowly changing. "Lost bearing" → the system can't determine its direction.

---

### **DRIFT**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Proto-Germanic *\*driftą* — "that which is driven." The accumulated error. How much you've wandered from the intended path. Dead reckoning error — the difference between where you think you are and where you actually are.

**Pronunciation:** /drɪft/ — DRIFT

**Definition:** How much you've wandered. The dead reckoning error. The cumulative effect of being pushed by currents you can't see or control. Drift is not a single error but an *accumulation* — each tiny deviation adds up.

**Feeling:** The moment you look up from your phone while walking and realize you've drifted off the path. Not lost — you can still see the path — but further from it than you intended. Drift is the slow, invisible enemy of precision.

**Usage:** "Drift rate" → how fast the error accumulates. "Drift correction" → compensating for accumulated error. "Zero drift" → no accumulation (perfect tracking).

---

### **LOAD**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Old English *lād* — "way, course, carrying." How much weight the structure carries. The stress on the system. Load-bearing vs. decorative — is this constraint actually *holding* something?

**Pronunciation:** /loʊd/ — LOHD

**Definition:** How much weight the structure carries. The current stress on the constraint system — how many demands are active, how much force they're exerting, how close the structure is to its limits.

**Feeling:** Carrying a heavy backpack. You know how much you can carry, and you know when you're approaching the limit. Load is the weight of active constraints — each one adds mass, each one adds stress. Too much load and the structure fails.

**Usage:** "System load" → total stress on all active constraints. "Load-bearing constraint" → a constraint that's under stress. "Offload" → transfer load from one constraint to another.

### **CADENCE**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Latin *cadentia* — "a falling." In music: the falling of the voice at the end of a phrase. In cycling: the rate of pedaling. In the constraint system: the rhythm of progress.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈkeɪ.dəns/ — KAY-dence

**Definition:** The rhythm of progress. How many constraint operations per unit time. Not the speed of the system but the *rhythm* — is it steady, accelerating, decelerating, irregular?

**Feeling:** Running and finding your stride. Not sprinting (too fast, unsustainable) and not walking (too slow) but the sustainable rhythm of progress. The cadence tells you the *health* of the system's timing.

**Usage:** "Steady cadence" → consistent rhythm of operations. "Cadence drop" → the system is slowing down. "Cadence spike" → sudden acceleration.

### **ENTROPY**

**Language:** English — from Greek.
**Etymology:** From Greek *entropia* (ἐντροπία) — "transformation, turning toward." Coined by Rudolf Clausius in 1865 for thermodynamics. The measure of disorder. How much chaos in the system.

**Pronunciation:** /ˈɛn.trə.pi/ — EN-truh-pee

**Definition:** Disorder. How much chaos in the system. Low entropy: ordered, predictable, the constraints are clear. High entropy: disordered, unpredictable, the constraints are unclear. Entropy always tends to increase without active constraint.

**Feeling:** The difference between a tidy room and a messy one. Entropy is the universal tendency toward mess — not because the universe hates order, but because there are more ways to be messy than to be tidy. The constraint system fights entropy by definition.

**Usage:** "Entropy rising" → the system is becoming disordered. "Entropy stable" → the constraints are maintaining order. "Entropy too low" → the system is too rigid, no room for creativity.

### **RESOLUTION**

**Language:** English.

**Pronunciation:** /ˌrɛz.əˈluː.ʃən/ — rez-uh-LOO-shun

**Definition:** How fine the chart. The granularity of the bathymetric reading. High resolution: you can see every detail, every contour. Low resolution: the chart is coarse, features are smoothed. Resolution is *not the same as* depth — you can have deep terrain with low resolution.

**Feeling:** The zoom level on a map. Zoomed out: you see the continent. Zoomed in: you see the streets. Resolution is how much detail is visible. More is not always better — sometimes you need the big picture.

**Usage:** "Increase resolution" → finer grain readings. "Low resolution" → coarse, smoothed readings. "Resolution mismatch" → the chart detail doesn't match the navigation need.

### **SIGNAL**

**Feeling:** Listening to the radio and hearing the music under the static. You know the song is there — you can almost make it out. The signal is what you're reaching for. The constraint system's job is to amplify signal and suppress noise.

### **NOISE**

**Pronunciation:** /nɔɪz/ — NOYZ

Feelings are the internal experience of the constraint system. Not the system's state, not the operator's action, not the measurement — but what it *feels like* to be in relationship with the constraints.

**Pronunciation:** /ɡraɪnd/ — GRIND

### **SPACE**

**Feeling:** Standing in a well-designed room. Not too big (you'd feel lost) and not too small (you'd feel trapped). The proportions are *right*. The space invites you to exist within it. You can move freely, but you always know where the walls are.

**Usage:** "Lots of space" → the constraints are open and permissive. "Space collapsed" → the constraints tightened. "Create space" → adjust constraints to give more room.

**Usage:** "Increase rigidity" → make constraints harder. "Rigidity spectrum" → the range from decorative to structural. "Rigidity failure" → a hard constraint breaking under load.

### **CONSENSUS**

**Language:** English.
**Etymology:** From Latin *consensus* — "agreement, accord," from *consentire* — "to feel together." The agreement. What's shared. The process by which multiple constraint layers agree on a single output — the CHORUS in action.

**Pronunciation:** /kənˈsɛn.səs/ — kuhn-SEN-sus

**Definition:** The agreement. What's shared. The mechanism by which multiple constraint layers — each with its own preferences — negotiate a single output. Not voting; not averaging; *consensus* — finding the state that satisfies the most constraints simultaneously.

**Usage:** "Consensus reached" → all layers agree on output. "Consensus failure" → layers can't agree. "Consensus weight" → how much influence each layer has.

### **HOLONOMY**

**Language:** English — from Greek.
**Etymology:** From Greek *holos* (ὅλος) — "whole, entire" + *nomos* (νόμος) — "law." The holonomy is the "whole law" — the phase accumulated around a closed loop. In differential geometry, the holonomy measures how much a vector has rotated after parallel transport around a loop. The GEIST made mathematical.

**Pronunciation:** /həˈlɒn.ə.mi/ — huh-LON-uh-mee

**Definition:** The loop. What returns. The mathematical heart of the GEIST — the non-trivial phase accumulated when traversing a closed path through the constraint space. Holonomy measures *how much you change* when you return to where you started.

**Usage:** "Holonomy cycle" → one complete loop. "Holonomy phase" → the accumulated transformation. "Zero holonomy" → the loop is trivial (nothing learned).

### **VESSEL**
