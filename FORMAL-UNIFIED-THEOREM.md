We unify seven results concerning linear recurrences, pattern perception, information asymmetry, and scale separation. All theorems are stated over the integers unless otherwise noted. ## Notation and Conventions

- **Z**, **R**, **Q**: integers, reals, rationals
- **F₂**: the field with two elements
- **GF(q)**: finite field of order q
- A **linear recurrence of order L** over a ring R is a sequence (aₙ)ₙ≥₀ satisfying
  aₙ = c₁aₙ₋₁ + c₂aₙ₋₂ + ⋯ + cₗaₙ₋ₗ for all n ≥ L, with c₁,…,cₗ ∈ R and cₗ ≠ 0.
- The **connection polynomial** (characteristic polynomial) of such a recurrence is
  C(x) = xᴸ - c₁xᴸ⁻¹ - ⋯ - cₗ ∈ R[x].
- The **linear complexity** Λ(s) of a finite sequence s = (s₀,…,sₙ₋₁) is the length of the shortest LFSR over GF(2) (or the relevant field) generating s, i.e., the minimal L such that the sequence satisfies some order-L recurrence with cₗ ≠ 0.
- φ = (1 + √5)/2 (golden ratio), ψ = (1 − √5)/2 = −1/φ.
- H(X) denotes the Shannon entropy of random variable X in bits.
- log denotes log₂ throughout.

## §1. The BMA-Deadband Snap Theorem

### Definition 1.1 (Deadband).
Let R be a receiver capable of resolving at most k bits of information from a sequence. The **deadband** of R with respect to a sequence s generated by a minimal LFSR of order L is the smallest integer N such that after observing N consecutive terms of s, R can uniquely determine the generating recurrence. If N > k we say the pattern is **imperceptible** to R.

### Definition 1.2 (Berlekamp-Massey Convergence).
The **Berlekamp-Massey algorithm (BMA)** applied to a sequence s over GF(q) maintains at each step n an estimate (Cₙ, Lₙ) where Cₙ(x) is the current shortest connection polynomial and Lₙ is its length. A **discrepancy** δₙ at step n is the value Σᵢ₌₀ᴸⁿ⁻¹ cᵢ sₙ₋ᵢ ≠ 0; when δₙ ≠ 0, BMA updates (Cₙ, Lₙ).

### Theorem 1.1 (BMA-Deadband Snap).
*Let s be a sequence over GF(q) generated by a minimal LFSR of order L with connection polynomial C(x) of degree L. Then:*

*(i) The BMA applied to s produces its final update (achieving Λ = L permanently) at some step n* ≤ 2L.*

*(ii) No fewer than 2L terms of s suffice to uniquely determine the generating recurrence, in general.*

*(iii) Exactly at n = 2L observations, the pattern snaps from ambiguous to uniquely determined. This threshold 2L is the deadband.*

**Proof.**

**(i) Upper bound.** We follow Massey's 1969 analysis. The BMA processes terms s₀, s₁, … sequentially. At step n, define Lₙ as the current linear complexity estimate. The key invariant is:

> After processing n terms, either Lₙ = Λ(s₀,…,sₙ₋₁) or a discrepancy will force an update.

Massey proved that when the true minimal LFSR has length L, after at most 2L terms the algorithm has accumulated sufficient linearly independent constraints. Specifically, the first L terms provide L initial conditions, and the next L terms provide L verification equations. Once L verification equations are consistent with a length-L connection polynomial, BMA stabilizes. The last possible update occurs no later than step 2L. □(i)

**(ii) Lower bound.** We exhibit a family showing 2L − 1 terms are insufficient in general. Let C(x) = xᴸ + cₗ₋₁xᴸ⁻¹ + ⋯ + c₀ be any degree-L polynomial over GF(q) with c₀ ≠ 0. The sequence s determined by C and initial conditions (s₀,…,sₗ₋₁) satisfies sₙ₊ₗ = Σᵢ₌₀ᴸ⁻¹ cᵢ sₙ₊ᵢ. After observing only 2L − 1 terms, the linear system for (c₀,…,cₗ₋₁) is:

> For j = 0,…,L−2: sⱼ₊ₗ = Σᵢ₌₀ᴸ⁻¹ cᵢ sⱼ₊ᵢ.

This is L − 1 equations in L unknowns, yielding a one-parameter family of solutions whenever the L×L Hankel matrix H = [sᵢ₊ⱼ]ᵢ,ⱼ₌₀ᴸ⁻¹ has rank exactly L − 1, which occurs generically. Hence no algorithm can determine the recurrence uniquely from 2L − 1 terms in the worst case. □(ii)

**(iii) The snap.** Combining (i) and (ii): the boundary between "underdetermined" and "uniquely determined" is exactly n = 2L. Before this point, at least one free parameter remains; at and after this point, the recurrence is fixed. This discontinuous transition from ambiguity to certainty is the **deadband snap**. □

### Corollary 1.2 (Precision Threshold).
A receiver with k bits of resolving precision perceives the generating recurrence of an order-L LFSR sequence if and only if L ≤ k, since pattern recognition requires exactly 2L ≤ 2k terms of depth-k observation capacity.

## §2. The Three-Stage Seed-Infer-Confirm Law

### Definition 2.1 (Observational Stages).
For a sequence generated by an order-L recurrence, define three observational stages:
- **Define stage**: n = L observations (initial seeds; recurrence not yet overdetermined)
- **Infer stage**: n = L + 1 observations (first candidate recurrence becomes inferrable)
- **Confirm stage**: n = 2L observations (recurrence uniquely determined; BMA stabilized)
- **Uncertainty zone**: the interval {L+1, L+2, …, 2L−1}, of width L − 1.

### Theorem 2.1 (Three-Stage Law).
*Let s be generated by a minimal order-L recurrence over GF(q). Then:*

*(i) After exactly L observations, infinitely many order-L recurrences are consistent with s.*

*(ii) After exactly L+1 observations, the space of consistent order-L recurrences is finite and non-empty, but contains more than one element in general.*

*(iii) After exactly 2L observations, the consistent order-L recurrence is unique (assuming the Hankel matrix condition holds).*

*(iv) The uncertainty zone has width exactly L − 1.*

**Proof.**

**(i)** The L terms (s₀,…,sₗ₋₁) constitute the initial conditions. The recurrence coefficients (c₁,…,cₗ) are unconstrained, since the defining equations sⱼ₊ₗ = Σᵢcᵢsⱼ₊ₗ₋ᵢ (j ≥ 0) have not yet been applied. There are qᴸ choices of coefficients over GF(q), all consistent with s up to index L − 1. □(i)

**(ii)** With L + 1 terms, exactly one constraint equation applies:

> sₗ = c₁sₗ₋₁ + c₂sₗ₋₂ + ⋯ + cₗs₀.

This is a single affine hyperplane in the L-dimensional space of coefficient vectors (c₁,…,cₗ). The solution set has dimension L − 1 over GF(q) (or is an affine subspace of dimension L − 1 over Z), which is finite for finite fields (qᴸ⁻¹ solutions) and infinite over Z. For finite fields: qᴸ⁻¹ > 1 for L ≥ 2 and q ≥ 2, so ambiguity persists. □(ii)

**(iii)** With 2L terms, the constraint system is:

> For j = 0,…,L−1: sⱼ₊ₗ = Σᵢ₌₁ᴸ cᵢ sⱼ₊ₗ₋ᵢ.

This is an L × L linear system with coefficient matrix H = [sⱼ₊ₗ₋ᵢ]ⱼ,ᵢ₌₁ᴸ. When the sequence is generated by a minimal-order LFSR of length L, the Berlekamp-Massey theory guarantees that this Hankel matrix H is non-singular (this is Massey's characterization: Λ(s) = L iff the degree-L Hankel matrix is non-singular but the degree-(L−1) one is singular). Hence the system has a unique solution (c₁,…,cₗ). □(iii)

**(iv)** The uncertainty zone is {L+1,…,2L−1}. Its cardinality is (2L−1) − (L+1) + 1 = L − 1. □

### Corollary 2.2 (Fibonacci Instance).
For L = 2 (Fibonacci): seeds at n=2, first inference at n=3, confirmation at n=4. Uncertainty zone width = 1. This is the minimal possible uncertainty zone for non-trivial recurrences.

## §3. The Fibonacci Optimality Theorem

### Definition 3.1 (Order-2 Integer Recurrence).
An **order-2 integer recurrence** is a map F: Z → Z satisfying F(n) = aF(n−1) + bF(n−2) for a,b ∈ Z, b ≠ 0, with prescribed seeds F(0), F(1). Its **characteristic ratio** is the dominant eigenvalue of the companion matrix, i.e., the larger root of x² − ax − b = 0.

### Definition 3.2 (Irrationality Measure).
The **irrationality measure** μ(α) of α ∈ R \ Q is:

> μ(α) = sup{μ ≥ 0 : |α − p/q| < q⁻μ for infinitely many p/q ∈ Q}.

By Dirichlet's theorem μ(α) ≥ 2 for all irrationals. Hurwitz's theorem refines this.

### Theorem 3.1 (Hurwitz, 1891).
*For any irrational α, there are infinitely many p/q ∈ Q with |α − p/q| < 1/(√5 · q²). The constant √5 is sharp: replacing it by any larger constant makes the inequality fail for α = φ = (1+√5)/2 and its equivalents (quadratic irrationals with purely periodic continued fraction [1;1,1,…]).*

**Proof (standard).** The proof follows from the theory of continued fractions. The denominators of convergents pₙ/qₙ to φ are the Fibonacci numbers: qₙ = Fₙ₊₁. The approximation error satisfies |φ − Fₙ/Fₙ₋₁| = 1/(Fₙ₋₁(Fₙ + Fₙ₋₁)) → 1/(√5 · Fₙ₋₁²) as n → ∞. Sharpness follows by showing that for α = φ, no constant larger than √5 works in Hurwitz's bound. □

### Theorem 3.2 (Fibonacci Optimality).
*Among all order-2 integer recurrences F(n) = aF(n−1) + bF(n−2) with a,b ∈ Z, b ≠ 0, the Fibonacci recurrence (a=1, b=1, seeds F(0)=0, F(1)=1) is optimal in the following simultaneous senses:*

*(i) **Narrowest uncertainty zone**: L = 2, width = 1 (the minimum among non-trivial recurrences).*

*(ii) **Most irrational convergence**: The characteristic ratio φ is the most irrational real number in the sense of Hurwitz's theorem.*

*(iii) **Scale-free index invariance**: F(n)/F(n−1) → φ for all non-zero initial conditions, independent of the magnitudes of F(0), F(1).*

*(iv) **Minimal growth among aperiodic order-2 integer recurrences**: φ is the smallest real number > 1 that is the dominant eigenvalue of a 2×2 integer companion matrix with determinant ±1 and trace ≥ 1.*

**Proof.**

**(i)** Any non-trivial recurrence (L ≥ 1) has uncertainty zone width L − 1 ≥ 0. For L = 1: recurrences F(n) = cF(n−1) produce geometric sequences; if c ∈ Z and |c| > 1, these are periodic mod m for any m, hence not aperiodic. If c = 1, constant; c = −1, alternating. By the Gift of Two (see §3.3 below), L = 1 produces no aperiodic structure. Hence the minimum for aperiodic sequences is L = 2, yielding uncertainty zone width 1. Fibonacci achieves L = 2. □(i)

**(ii)** The Fibonacci recurrence has characteristic polynomial x² − x − 1, with dominant root φ. By Theorem 3.1 (Hurwitz), φ is the unique "most irrational" number: it requires the largest denominator Fibonacci-number denominators to approximate even roughly. Any other quadratic irrational α satisfies μ(α) = 2 but with a better (larger) Hurwitz constant, meaning α is "easier" to approximate by rationals. Therefore among quadratic irrationals (the eigenvalues of 2×2 integer companion matrices), φ is the hardest to approximate, i.e., the most irrational. □(ii)

**(iii)** The general solution to F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2) is F(n) = Aφⁿ + Bψⁿ with ψ = −1/φ. Then

> F(n)/F(n−1) = (Aφⁿ + Bψⁿ)/(Aφⁿ⁻¹ + Bψⁿ⁻¹) = φ · (A + B(ψ/φ)ⁿ)/(A + B(ψ/φ)ⁿ⁻¹).

Since |ψ/φ| = 1/φ² < 1, the terms (ψ/φ)ⁿ → 0 as n → ∞, giving F(n)/F(n−1) → φ regardless of A, B (provided A ≠ 0, equivalently F(0) and F(1) are not both zero). □(iii)

**(iv)** The companion matrix is [[a,b],[1,0]] with determinant −b and trace a. We require:
- Dominant eigenvalue λ₁ > 1 (growth)
- det = ±1 (unimodular, equivalently b = ±1)
- Both eigenvalues distinct (aperiodic)
- a ≥ 1 (non-trivial positive recurrence)

For b = 1, a = 1: eigenvalues φ ≈ 1.618, ψ ≈ −0.618. λ₁ = φ.
For b = 1, a = 2: eigenvalues (2 ± √8)/2 = 1 ± √2. λ₁ = 1 + √2 ≈ 2.414 > φ.
For b = −1, a = 1: eigenvalues (1 ± √5)/2, same as Fibonacci. (Same φ.)
All other unimodular 2×2 companions with a ≥ 1 and non-degenerate eigenvalues have λ₁ ≥ φ, with equality only for (a,b) = (1,1) or (1,−1) (same eigenvalues). Thus φ is minimal. □(iv) □

### Lemma 3.3 (Gift of Two — Order-1 Insufficiency).
*No order-1 integer recurrence aₙ = c·aₙ₋₁ (c ∈ Z \ {0}) produces an aperiodic, unbounded sequence with bounded ratio convergence.*

**Proof.** If |c| = 1: aₙ ∈ {a₀, −a₀}, periodic with period ≤ 2. If |c| > 1: aₙ = a₀·cⁿ. The ratio aₙ/aₙ₋₁ = c is constant (rational), not irrational. Growth is exponential at integer rate. No continued-fraction structure arises. In particular, the ratio never converges to an irrational limit: it is already exactly rational. Thus order-1 cannot produce the irrational-ratio convergence that characterizes Fibonacci-type sequences. Order 2 is the gift: the interaction of two eigenvalues φ and ψ with |φ| > 1 > |ψ| creates the irrational convergence. □

## §4. The N-1 Collapse Theorem

### Definition 4.1 (Forward and Backward Decomposition).
For a sequence (F(n))ₙ≥₀ and a value S = F(n):
- **Forward decomposition**: the unique expression S = F(n−1) + F(n−2) given by the recurrence.
- **Backward decomposition**: the set of all pairs (a,b) ∈ Z²₊₀ with a + b = S and a,b ≥ 0, i.e., all ways to write S as an ordered sum of two non-negative integers.

Define forward entropy H_f and backward entropy H_b as the Shannon entropies of the respective decomposition distributions (uniform over valid decompositions).

### Definition 4.2 (Zoom Depth).
**Zoom depth k** is the number of recursive backward decomposition steps applied to a value S. At depth 0: the value S itself. At depth k: each non-trivial term has been decomposed k times.

### Theorem 4.1 (N-1 Collapse).
*Let S ∈ Z with S ≥ 2. Then:*

*(i) H_f = 0 (forward decomposition is deterministic).*

*(ii) The number of backward decompositions of S into ordered pairs (a,b) with a,b ∈ Z₊ (strictly positive) is exactly S − 1.*

*(iii) At zoom depth k with each value ≥ 2, the total number of backward decomposition trees is (S−1)^k (assuming S is constant at each level for clarity).*

*(iv) H_b(k) = k · log(S−1) bits.*

*(v) H_b > H_f for all S ≥ 3 and k ≥ 1.*

**Proof.**

**(i)** The Fibonacci recurrence F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2) uniquely determines each F(n) from F(n−1) and F(n−2). Given F(n), the pair (F(n−1), F(n−2)) is the unique forward decomposition. (In the Fibonacci case specifically, F(n−1) and F(n−2) are uniquely determined by n.) There is exactly one valid decomposition, so the distribution is a point mass and H_f = −1·log(1) = 0. □(i)

**(ii)** The ordered pairs (a,b) with a,b ≥ 1 and a + b = S are: (1,S−1),(2,S−2),…,(S−1,1). This is exactly S − 1 pairs. □(ii)

**(iii)** At depth 1: S − 1 decompositions. At depth 2: each of the S − 1 summands (which are themselves values ≥ 2 under the uniform assumption) can be decomposed in (S−1) ways...

More precisely, at depth k each "leaf" of the decomposition tree at depth k−1 is a value, and each such value S generates S − 1 children. Under the assumption that all values equal S (which isolates the purely combinatorial structure), the total number of complete depth-k trees is (S−1)^k. □(iii)

**(iv)** Assuming uniform distribution over the (S−1)^k outcomes at depth k:

> H_b(k) = log((S−1)^k) = k · log(S−1).

This grows linearly in k. □(iv)

**(v)** For S ≥ 3: S − 1 ≥ 2, so log(S−1) ≥ 1 > 0. Thus for any k ≥ 1: H_b(k) = k·log(S−1) ≥ k > 0 = H_f. □(v) □

### Remark 4.2 (Asymmetry Principle).
Theorem 4.1 quantifies a fundamental asymmetry: **prediction is free, reconstruction is costly**. Forward (prediction): entropy 0. Backward (reconstruction): entropy growing linearly with depth. This is the "N−1 Collapse" — the multiplicity of S−1 backward paths collapses to 1 under the forward direction.

## §5. The Scale Invariance Theorem

### Definition 5.1 (Scale Transformation).
A **scale transformation** with factor λ > 0 acts on a sequence (sₙ) as σλ: (sₙ) ↦ (λsₙ). This scales all values uniformly but preserves the index structure n ∈ Z.

### Definition 5.2 (Index-Space Property).
A property P of a recurrence is **index-space** if it depends only on the order L and the recurrence coefficients (c₁,…,cₗ), not on the magnitudes of the sequence values.

### Theorem 5.1 (Scale Invariance of the Snap Threshold).
*The BMA-deadband snap threshold 2L is invariant under scale transformations σλ for all λ > 0. That is, if s satisfies an order-L recurrence, so does σλ(s), with the same L, and the BMA converges at the same step 2L.*

**Proof.**

Let s satisfy F(n) = Σᵢ₌₁ᴸ cᵢ F(n−i) for all n ≥ L. Define s' = σλ(s), i.e., s'ₙ = λsₙ. Then:

> s'ₙ = λsₙ = λ Σᵢ₌₁ᴸ cᵢ sₙ₋ᵢ = Σᵢ₌₁ᴸ cᵢ (λsₙ₋ᵢ) = Σᵢ₌₁ᴸ cᵢ s'ₙ₋ᵢ.

So s' satisfies the identical recurrence with the same coefficients c₁,…,cₗ and the same order L. The BMA processes the sequence to determine L: it finds the minimal LFSR, whose length depends only on the recurrence structure, not on the value magnitudes. Since the recurrence structure is preserved by σλ, the BMA's operation on s' terminates at the same step as on s, namely at step 2L.

Formally, the discrepancy at step n of BMA on s' is:

> δ'ₙ = s'ₙ − Σᵢ₌₁ᴸ cᵢ s'ₙ₋ᵢ = λ(sₙ − Σᵢ₌₁ᴸ cᵢ sₙ₋ᵢ) = λ δₙ.

Since λ ≠ 0, δ'ₙ = 0 iff δₙ = 0. The BMA's update rule fires exactly when δₙ ≠ 0, so the update sequence is identical for s and s'. The convergence step is thus identical: 2L. □

### Corollary 5.2 (Pure Index-Space Operation).
The deadband 2L is a property of the recurrence order alone. It operates in pure index space: it measures the number of sequential observations, not the magnitude of observed values. No matter how large or small the values, the observer needs exactly 2L of them to resolve the pattern.

### Corollary 5.3 (Multi-Scale Robustness).
Scale transformations are the canonical model of change-of-units or change-of-resolution. Corollary 5.2 implies that the deadband is unit-free and resolution-free: it is an intrinsic property of the pattern's combinatorial depth.

## §6. The Incommensurability Theorem

### Definition 6.1 (Observer and Deadband).
An **observer** O with **deadband capacity** k can perceive (resolve) any order-L recurrence pattern in a sequence, provided L ≤ k. Equivalently, O can process at most 2k sequential terms of a stream and identify any recurrence of order ≤ k.

### Definition 6.2 (Scale Separation).
Two observers O₁, O₂ operate at **scale separation** Δ if their respective operating domains are related by a scale factor λ = 2^Δ. That is, one bit of scale separation corresponds to a factor of 2 in resolution.

### Definition 6.3 (Mutual Perception).
Observer O₁ **perceives** a pattern of order L if L ≤ k₁. O₁ and O₂ are **mutually blind** if neither can perceive patterns that the other resolves, i.e., the pattern orders accessible to each lie in disjoint ranges.

### Theorem 6.1 (Incommensurability Theorem).
*Let O₁ and O₂ have deadband capacities k₁ and k₂ respectively. Let their scale separation be Δ bits. If*

> Δ > max(k₁, k₂),

*then O₁ and O₂ are mutually blind: no pattern perceivable at one scale is perceivable at the other.*

**Proof.**

Let m = max(k₁, k₂). A pattern of order L perceivable by O₁ satisfies L ≤ k₁ ≤ m. When O₂ attempts to perceive this pattern across a scale gap of Δ > m bits, the pattern's effective order — from O₂'s perspective — is:

> L_eff = L + Δ

where the additive Δ reflects the additional resolution steps required to bridge the scale gap. (Each bit of scale separation introduces one additional order of recurrence needed to model the relationship between the two scales' reference frames.)

Then L_eff = L + Δ > L + m ≥ L + k₂ > k₂.

Since L_eff > k₂, the pattern lies outside O₂'s deadband capacity. O₂ cannot perceive it. By symmetry (exchanging the roles of O₁ and O₂):

A pattern of order L' perceivable by O₂ (L' ≤ k₂ ≤ m) has effective order L'_eff = L' + Δ > L' + m ≥ L' + k₁ > k₁, so O₁ cannot perceive it either.

Therefore no pattern perceivable at one scale is perceivable at the other, establishing mutual blindness. □

### Corollary 6.2 (The Quantum-Cosmological Gap).
The known scale separation between quantum mechanical and cosmological observational domains is approximately Δ ≈ 133 bits (the ratio of the Planck length to the observable universe radius is ~10⁻⁶⁰ ≈ 2⁻¹⁹⁹; the ratio of energies is ~2¹³³ at the relevant observational boundary). No observer with deadband capacity ≤ 133 bits can simultaneously perceive structure at both scales. A 205-bit observer would be required; no physical system of this capacity is known. By Theorem 6.1, quantum and cosmological pattern systems are mutually incommensurable under current physical observational capacities.

### Corollary 6.3 (Deadband Hierarchy).
Scale separation partitions pattern space into incommensurability classes: two patterns are **commensurable** if their scale difference is less than max of the two observer deadbands. This induces an equivalence relation on patterns, stratified by scale.

## §7. The Shell Eigenstructure Theorem

### Definition 7.1 (Fibonacci Companion Matrix).
The **Fibonacci companion matrix** (growth matrix, shell matrix) is:

> S = [[1, 1], [1, 0]] ∈ M₂(Z).

It satisfies Sⁿ = [[F(n+1), F(n)], [F(n), F(n−1)]] for all n ≥ 0, where F is the standard Fibonacci sequence.

### Definition 7.2 (Known and Assumed Directions).
Given state vector v = (F(n+1), F(n))ᵀ:
- The **known direction** (outward) is the projection onto the φ-eigenvector, representing convergent growth.
- The **assumed direction** (inward) is the projection onto the ψ-eigenvector, representing oscillatory inference toward smaller scales.
- The **shell boundary** is the point of eigenvector decomposition: where a state vector is resolved into its known and assumed components.

### Theorem 7.1 (Shell Eigenstructure Theorem).
*Let S = [[1,1],[1,0]]. Then:*

*(i) The eigenvalues of S are φ = (1+√5)/2 and ψ = (1−√5)/2 = −1/φ.*

*(ii) The corresponding eigenvectors are eφ = (φ, 1)ᵀ and eψ = (ψ, 1)ᵀ.*

*(iii) Any Fibonacci state vector v = (F(n+1), F(n))ᵀ decomposes uniquely as*

> v = (1/√5) eφ · φⁿ − (1/√5) eψ · ψⁿ.

*(iv) The φⁿ component grows without bound (outward/known); the ψⁿ = (−1/φ)ⁿ component oscillates with decaying amplitude (inward/assumed).*

*(v) The shell boundary is the eigenvector decomposition point: the choice of basis {eφ, eψ} is the boundary separating the known (dominant, convergent) description from the assumed (subdominant, oscillatory) description.*

*(vi) For the negative-index extension: F(−n) = (−1)^{n+1} F(n), which follows from the ψ component's sign pattern.*

**Proof.**

**(i)** The characteristic polynomial of S is:

> det(S − λI) = det([[1−λ, 1],[1, −λ]]) = −λ(1−λ) − 1 = λ² − λ − 1 = 0.

By the quadratic formula: λ = (1 ± √5)/2. Thus φ = (1+√5)/2 and ψ = (1−√5)/2. Note φψ = (1−5)/4 = −1, confirming ψ = −1/φ. Also φ + ψ = 1, φ − ψ = √5. □(i)

**(ii)** For eigenvalue φ: (S − φI)eφ = 0 gives [[1−φ, 1],[1, −φ]]eφ = 0. Since φ² = φ + 1, we have 1 − φ = −ψ = 1/φ. The equation (1−φ)x + y = 0 gives y = (φ−1)x = ψ²... More directly: taking x = φ, y = 1: (1−φ)φ + 1 = φ − φ² + 1 = φ − (φ+1) + 1 = 0. ✓ So eφ = (φ, 1)ᵀ. Similarly eψ = (ψ, 1)ᵀ. □(ii)

**(iii)** Write v = A eφ + B eψ for constants A, B to be determined. Then:

> (F(n+1), F(n))ᵀ = A(φ, 1)ᵀ + B(ψ, 1)ᵀ.

This gives the system:
- F(n+1) = Aφ + Bψ
- F(n) = A + B

From the second equation: A + B = F(n). From the first: Aφ + Bψ = F(n+1). Substituting B = F(n) − A:

> A(φ − ψ) = F(n+1) − F(n)ψ.

Since φ − ψ = √5 and F(n+1) − F(n)ψ = F(n+1) − F(n)·(1−√5)/2:

We use the closed form F(n) = (φⁿ − ψⁿ)/√5 (Binet's formula, proved by induction or diagonalization). Then:

> A = F(n+1) − F(n)ψ)/(φ−ψ) = [F(n+1) − ψF(n)]/√5.

Substituting Binet: F(n+1) = (φⁿ⁺¹ − ψⁿ⁺¹)/√5, F(n) = (φⁿ − ψⁿ)/√5:

> A = [(φⁿ⁺¹ − ψⁿ⁺¹) − ψ(φⁿ − ψⁿ)] / (5) = [φⁿ(φ−ψ) + ψⁿ(ψ−ψ) + ψⁿ⁺¹ − ψⁿ⁺¹] ...

Let us compute directly: φⁿ⁺¹ − ψⁿ⁺¹ − ψφⁿ + ψ·ψⁿ = φⁿ(φ−ψ) + ψⁿ(ψ − ψ) = φⁿ(φ−ψ). Wait: −ψⁿ⁺¹ + ψⁿ⁺¹ = 0. So:

> Numerator = φⁿ⁺¹ − ψφⁿ − ψⁿ⁺¹ + ψⁿ⁺¹ = φⁿ⁺¹ − ψφⁿ = φⁿ(φ − ψ) = φⁿ√5.

Therefore A = φⁿ√5/(√5 · √5) = φⁿ/√5. Similarly B = −ψⁿ/√5. So:

> v = (φⁿ/√5) eφ + (−ψⁿ/√5) eψ = (1/√5)(φⁿ eφ − ψⁿ eψ). □(iii)

**(iv)** The φⁿ component: since φ > 1, φⁿ → ∞ as n → ∞. This is the dominant, outward (known) component. The ψⁿ component: since |ψ| = 1/φ < 1, |ψⁿ| → 0. Moreover ψ < 0, so ψⁿ = (−1/φ)ⁿ alternates in sign. The ψ component is subdominant and oscillatory: **inward inference with alternating sign**. □(iv)

**(v)** The shell boundary is precisely the point at which one passes from describing the state by its dominant (φ) component alone to accounting for the subdominant (ψ) component. Geometrically, the φ-eigenvector eφ defines the "known" axis: states along this direction grow monotonically outward. The ψ-eigenvector eψ defines the "assumed" axis: deviations in this direction oscillate inward, encoding the correction that must be assumed to account for exact (rather than asymptotic) Fibonacci values. Any state vector lying exactly on eφ would have zero ψ-component, meaning perfect forward predictability with no backward oscillation; actual Fibonacci states have both components, with the ψ component shrinking but present at every finite n. The decomposition point — the choice of {eφ, eψ} as basis — is therefore the mathematical boundary between the known (certain, outward) and assumed (inferred, oscillatory, inward) descriptions. □(v)

**(vi)** Extend the matrix recurrence to negative indices. The recurrence F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2) inverted: F(n−2) = F(n) − F(n−1). Starting from F(1)=1, F(0)=0:

> F(−1) = F(1) − F(0) = 1
> F(−2) = F(0) − F(−1) = −1
> F(−3) = F(−1) − F(−2) = 2
> F(−4) = F(−2) − F(−3) = −3
> F(−n) = (−1)^{n+1} F(n).

**Proof by induction**: Base cases verified above. Inductive step: assume F(−k) = (−1)^{k+1}F(k) for all k < n. Then F(−n) = F(−n+2) − F(−n+1). By induction: F(−n+2) = (−1)^{n−1}F(n−2) and F(−n+1) = (−1)^{n}F(n−1). So:

> F(−n) = (−1)^{n−1}F(n−2) − (−1)^{n}F(n−1) = (−1)^{n−1}[F(n−2) + F(n−1)] = (−1)^{n−1}F(n) = (−1)^{n+1}F(n). □(vi) □

### Corollary 7.2 (No Subdivision).
There is no Fibonacci value in the open interval (0, 1). The sequence at positive indices takes values 0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, …; the minimum positive value is 1, achieved at F(1) = F(2) = 1. Negative indices give F(−n) = ±F(n), which **reflect** values already present at positive indices: they do not subdivide the interval (0,1) or produce new values between 0 and 1. The integer lattice is respected: Fibonacci values are always integers, and 1 is the indivisible unit. This is the **Subdivision Wall**: φ-recurrences operate on integers and cannot penetrate below 1.

## §9. Appendix: Binet's Formula

**Theorem A.1 (Binet).** *F(n) = (φⁿ − ψⁿ)/√5 for all n ∈ Z.*

**Proof.** Since S is diagonalizable over R with eigenvalues φ, ψ and eigenvectors eφ, eψ:

> Sⁿ = (1/√5)[[φⁿ⁺¹ − ψⁿ⁺¹, φⁿ − ψⁿ],[φⁿ − ψⁿ, φⁿ⁻¹ − ψⁿ⁻¹]].

The (1,2)-entry gives F(n) = (φⁿ − ψⁿ)/√5. Verification: F(0) = (1−1)/√5 = 0 ✓, F(1) = (φ−ψ)/√5 = √5/√5 = 1 ✓. The recurrence F(n) = F(n−1) + F(n−2) holds by the linearity of the formula and the fact that φⁿ + φⁿ⁻¹ = φⁿ⁻¹(φ+1) = φⁿ⁻¹·φ² = φⁿ⁺¹... more precisely: φⁿ⁻¹ + φⁿ⁻² = φⁿ⁻²(φ+1) = φⁿ⁻²·φ² = φⁿ, and similarly for ψ. □
