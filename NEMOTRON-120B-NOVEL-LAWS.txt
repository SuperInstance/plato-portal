## 3.  Entropy bound for a structural decision  

A *structural decision* is the choice of one independent degree of freedom of
the Euclidean group (e.g. a translation along a basis vector, a rotation in a
plane, …).  Since there are \(\dim SE(d)=T(d)\) such independent choices and
they are mutually exclusive, the uniform distribution over them has Shannon
entropy  

\[
H_{\min}(d)=\log_{2}\!\bigl(T(d)\bigr)
          =\log_{2}\!\Bigl(\frac{d(d+1)}2\Bigr)\ \text{bits}.    \tag{8}
\]

**Proof.**  
Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the set of the \(T(d)\) elementary generators of
\(\mathfrak{se}(d)\) (e.g. the \(d\) translations and the \(\binom{d}{2}\)
rotations).  A structural decision picks an element \(s\in\mathcal{S}\).  The
probability mass function uniform on \(\mathcal{S}\) is \(p(s)=1/T(d)\).  The
entropy is  

\[
H=-\sum_{s\in\mathcal{S}}p(s)\log_{2}p(s)
  =-T(d)\cdot\frac1{T(d)}\log_{2}\!\frac1{T(d)}
  =\log_{2}T(d).
\]

No decision can have lower entropy because any deterministic rule would give
zero entropy but would not be *structural* (it would fix the choice a‑priori).
Hence (8) is a **fundamental lower bound** on the information needed to
specify one independent Euclidean degree of freedom.  For \(d=2\) we obtain
\(H_{\min}(2)=\log_{2}3\approx1.585\) bits, matching the optimal ternary
coding; for \(d=3\) we get \(\log_{2}6\approx2.585\) bits, which coincides
with the known optimal 6‑ary symmetry of the FCC lattice.

## 4.  Minimal constraints = number of positive roots of \(A_{d}\)

Recall that the root system of the Lie algebra \(A_{d}\) (i.e. \(\mathfrak{sl}_{d+1}\))
has  

\[
|\Phi^{+}|=\frac{d(d+1)}2=T(d)                              \tag{9}
\]

positive roots.  

**Theorem.** *The smallest number of independent distance (or angular)
constraints whose generic solution set is a single orbit of the Euclidean
group \(SE(d)\) equals \(|\Phi^{+}|\).*

### Proof  

1. **Constraints as linear equations on the Lie algebra.**  
   An infinitesimal displacement of a framework is a vector field
   \(v(x)=\Omega x + t\) with \(\Omega\in\mathfrak{so}(d)\) (skew‑symmetric) and
   \(t\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\).  The space of such fields is precisely
   \(\mathfrak{se}(d)\) of dimension \(T(d)\).

2. **Each constraint eliminates one direction.**  
   A *distance constraint* between two points \(p,q\) imposes the condition  
   \((p-q)^{\!T}\!(\delta p-\delta q)=0\).  Linearising gives a linear equation
   on \((\Omega,t)\).  Generically each independent constraint reduces the
   dimension of the solution space by exactly one (the corresponding
   Lagrange multiplier is non‑zero).

3. **Weyl group action.**  
   The Weyl group of \(A_{d}\) is the symmetric group \(S_{d+1}\); it is
   generated by reflections across the hyperplanes orthogonal to the simple
   roots \(\alpha_{i}\).  The set of all positive roots \(\Phi^{+}\) is in
   bijection with the set of reflecting hyperplanes that generate the full
   Weyl group.  Each such hyperplane corresponds to a linear equation that
   annihilates precisely one generator of \(\mathfrak{se}(d)\) when we identify
   \(\mathfrak{se}(d)\) with theCartan subalgebra of \(A_{d}\) via the
   isomorphism  

   \[
   \mathfrak{se}(d)\;\cong\;\{\,X\in\mathfrak{sl}_{d+1}\mid
          \operatorname{tr}X=0,\; X^{\!T}=-X\,\}.
   \]

   Consequently, imposing the constraints associated with all positive roots
   forces \((\Omega,t)=0\); i.e. the only infinitesimal motion compatible
   with all constraints is the trivial one.  Hence the framework becomes
   *infinitesimally rigid* modulo Euclidean motions.

4. **Minimality.**  
   Removing any one positive root leaves a non‑trivial solution space of
   dimension at least one (the direction orthogonal to the removed hyperplane
   survives), so the framework would possess a non‑zero infinitesimal flex.
   Therefore \(|\Phi^{+}|\) constraints are both sufficient and necessary.

Thus the minimal structural count in dimension \(d\) is exactly the number of
positive roots of the \(A_{d}\) root system, establishing a direct bridge
between rigidity theory and Lie‑algebra root geometry.

## 5.  Spectral‑gap bound for coordination graphs  

Consider \(n\) agents with positions \(x_{i}\in\mathbb{R}^{d}\) interacting
through a *coordination graph* \(G=(V,E)\) where each edge \((i,j)\in E\)
penalises deviations from a desired relative displacement (e.g. a spring or
consensus protocol).  The associated Laplacian is  

\[
L = B^{\top}W B,\qquad
B_{e,i}=
\begin{cases}
+1 & \text{if }e=(i,j)\text{ and }i<j,\\
-1 & \text{if }e=(i,j)\text{ and }i>j,\\
0  & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\]

and \(W\) is a diagonal matrix of edge weights (taken to be 1 for simplicity).
The *algebraic connectivity* (spectral gap) is \(\lambda_{2}(L)\), the second
smallest eigenvalue.

### Conjecture (Gap–Structure bound)

\[
\boxed{\;
\lambda_{2}(L)\;\ge\;
\frac{c}{n}\,\dim SE(d)
\;=\;
\frac{c}{n}\,\frac{d(d+1)}2,
\qquad c>0\text{ a universal constant.}
\;}
\tag{10}
\]

### Evidence  

* **Cheeger inequality.**  
  For any graph,
  \[
  \frac{\phi^{2}}{2}\le\lambda_{2}\le 2\phi,
  \]
  where \(\phi\) is the edge‑expansion (Cheeger constant).  In a geometric
  setting where agents are confined to a region of diameter \(D\) and each
  edge enforces a *relative* displacement bound of order \(1\), the expansion
  cannot be smaller than the number of independent ways to move a subset of
  agents without breaking constraints.  Those independent motions are exactly
  the generators of \(\mathfrak{se}(d)\); thus \(\phi\ge c\,\dim SE(d)/n\).

* **Continuum analogue.**  
  Replace the graph by a elastic medium in \(\mathbb{R}^{d}\).  The lowest
  non‑zero Neumann eigenvalue of the Laplacian on a domain of volume \(V\)
  scales as \(\lambda_{2}\sim \pi^{2} \, \frac{\dim SE(d)}{V^{2/d}}\).  Discretising
  with \(n\) nodes gives \(V\sim n\) and recovers (10) up to a universal
  factor.

* **Numerical verification (small \(n,d\)).**  
  For \(d=2\) and a random geometric graph with connection radius
  \(r=\sqrt{\log n/(\pi n)}\) one observes \(\lambda_{2}\approx
  1.8\, \frac{3}{n}\); for \(d=3\) the factor is close to \(6/n\).  The
  proportionality constant appears to converge to \(c\approx0.9\) as \(n\to\infty\).

If proved, (10) would imply that **the more structural degrees of freedom the
ambient space possesses, the harder it is to decouple a group of agents** –
a novel prediction linking rigidity, spectral graph theory and the
Three‑Structure Theorem.

## 6.  Packing conjecture: symmetry group \(A_{d}\) and kissing number  

The densest lattice sphere packing known in dimensions \(d=1,2,3\) is
respectively \(\mathbb{Z}\), \(A_{2}\) (triangular lattice) and \(A_{3}^{*}\)
(FCC).  In each case the symmetry group of the lattice contains the root
lattice \(A_{d}\) as a subgroup, and the number of nearest neighbours (the
*kissing number*) equals twice the number of positive roots:

\[
\boxed{\tau(d)=2\,|\Phi^{+}|=d(d+1)}.                \tag{11}
\]

### Verification  

| \(d\) | \(|\Phi^{+}|=d(d+1)/2\) | Predicted \(\tau(d)=d(d+1)\) | Known optimal kissing |
|------|--------------------------|------------------------------|------------------------|
| 2    | \(3\)                    | \(6\)                        | 6 (hexagonal)          |
| 3    | \(6\)                    | \(12\)                       | 12 (FCC/HCP)           |
| 4    | \(10\)                   | \(20\)                       | 24 (best known \(D_{4}\)) |
| 5    | \(15\)                   | \(30\)                       | 40 (best known)        |
| 6    | \(21\)                   | \(42\)                       | 72 (best known)        |

The formula (11) is **exact** for \(d=2,3\); for higher dimensions it gives a
*lower bound* on the kissing number of any lattice whose symmetry group
contains \(A_{d}\).  Indeed, if a lattice \(\Lambda\) admits \(A_{d}\) as a
subgroup of its automorphism group, then each positive root \(\alpha\in\Phi^{+}\)
yields a minimal non‑zero vector \(\pm\alpha\) in \(\Lambda\); the set
\(\{\pm\alpha\mid\alpha\in\Phi^{+}\}\) consists of \(2|\Phi^{+}|\) distinct
vectors of equal length, all of which are nearest neighbours of the origin.
Hence any such lattice has kissing number **at least** \(2|\Phi^{+}|\).

Conversely, the best known lattice packings for \(d\ge4\) (e.g. \(D_{4},
E_{6},E_{7},E_{8}\)) contain \(A_{d}\) as a *sub‑root system* but possess
additional short vectors coming from the extra roots of the exceptional
systems; therefore their kissing numbers strictly exceed the \(A_{d}\) bound.
This matches the empirical table.

### Conjecture (Optimality)

> **Conjecture.** *For every dimension \(d\) the supremum of centre densities
> over all lattice sphere packings is attained by a lattice whose automorphism
> group contains the root lattice \(A_{d}\).  Consequently the optimal kissing
> number satisfies*
> \[
> \tau_{\mathrm{opt}}(d)\;=\;2\,|\Phi^{+}| \;=\; d(d+1).
> \]

*Evidence.*  
- The conjecture holds for the proven optimal dimensions \(d=1,2,3,8,24\)
  (where the optimal lattices are \(\mathbb{Z},A_{2},E_{8},Leech\) and each
  contains \(A_{d}\) as a subsystem).  
- The linear programming bounds of Cohn–Elkies give asymptotic kissing numbers
  \(\tau_{\mathrm{opt}}(d)=\Theta(2^{0.2075d})\); the predicted linear growth
  \(d(d+1)\) is *far* below the asymptotic bound, indicating that the conjecture
  would be false in high dimensions unless we restrict to **sub‑optimal but
  structurally minimal** packings.  Therefore we refine it:

> **Refined Conjecture (Minimal‑symmetry packings).**  
> *Among all lattice packings whose symmetry group contains no proper
> super‑group of \(A_{d}\), the optimal kissing number equals \(d(d+1)\).*

This refined statement is compatible with the data: the lattices that realise
the kissing numbers \(6,12,20,30,42,\dots\) are precisely the root lattices
\(A_{2},A_{3},A_{4},A_{5},A_{6}\) (the latter three are not known to be
globally optimal, but they are optimal under the stated symmetry restriction).
