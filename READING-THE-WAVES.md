## The Three Languages of Waves

### 1. Wind Waves (Local Forcing)

Wind blowing across water transfers energy into waves. The wave characteristics encode the wind:

| Wave Property | What It Says About Wind |
|--------------|----------------------|
| Height | Wind speed (higher wind = taller waves) |
| Period | Wind duration (longer blow = longer period) |
| Steepness | Wind age (young wind = steep, old wind = gentle swells) |
| Direction | Wind direction (waves align with the wind that made them) |
| Confusion | Multiple wind sources crossing |

A sailor reading wind waves can tell you: "A 25-knot northwest blow started about 6 hours ago, still building." Without looking at the sky. Without an anemometer. Just from the waves.

**In our system**: The constraint deltas are wind waves. Each perturbation (the "wind") creates a response (the "wave") that encodes the perturbation's characteristics:

| Delta Property | What It Says About Perturbation |
|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Amplitude | Perturbation magnitude (stronger shake = bigger delta) |
| Decay rate | System damping (slow decay = near edge, fast decay = laminar) |
| Direction | Which parameters are coupled (delta propagates along coupling paths) |
| Periodicity | System's resonant frequency (repeating deltas = system ringing) |
| Confusion | Multiple perturbation sources competing |

We don't need to KNOW what perturbed the system. We READ the deltas and reconstruct what happened. The deltas are the waves. The perturbation is the wind. The coupling topology is the current.

### 2. Swell (Remote Forcing)

Swell is waves generated by a distant storm, traveled hundreds or thousands of miles across open ocean. Swell characteristics encode the DISTANT storm:

| Swell Property | What It Says About Distant Storm |
|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Period (10-20 sec) | Storm intensity (longer period = stronger storm) |
| Direction | Storm location (where the swell came FROM) |
| Height | Storm severity × distance (attenuated by travel) |
| Arrival time | Storm timing (longer period swell arrives first — it travels faster) |

Hawaiian surfers read Pacific swell to know about storms near Alaska, days before those storms reach Hawaii. The waves carry information across the ocean like a telegram written in water.

**In our system**: Fleet-level propagations are swell. A perturbation in one agent creates a wave that propagates through the fleet topology, arriving at distant agents with characteristic attenuation:

| Propagation Property | What It Says About Distant Agent |
|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| Delay | Network distance (hops between agents) |
| Attenuation | Coupling strength (how strongly agents are linked) |
| Dispersion | Topology structure (ring disperses differently than star) |
| Arrival order | Path multiplicity (more paths = more arrivals) |

A distant agent doesn't need to TELL us it was perturbed. The SWELL of its perturbation arrives at our agent through the coupling topology, carrying information about what happened, where, and how strong. We read the swell and reconstruct the distant event.

### 3. Current Effects (Hidden Structure)

Currents don't make waves. Currents MODIFY waves that already exist. But the modifications encode the current:

| Current Effect on Waves | What It Says About Current |
|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Wave refraction (bending) | Current direction (waves bend INTO opposing current) |
| Wave steepening | Opposing current (waves steepen when current pushes against them) |
| Wave calming | Following current (waves flatten when current pushes with them) |
| Standing wave patterns | Current shear zones (two currents meeting creates standing patterns) |
| Rip current signatures | Gaps in breaking waves (smooth channels in the surf zone) |

The current is INVISIBLE. You can't see it in the water. But it WARPS the waves in ways that are legible if you know how to read them. The waves are a lens through which the invisible current becomes visible.

**In our system**: The constraint topology is the current. It doesn't create perturbations (waves). But it WARPS how perturbations propagate:

| Constraint Effect on Deltas | What It Says About Constraint Topology |
|----------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Deltas bend toward attractors | Constraint gradient (deltas curve toward satisfied states) |
| Deltas steepen near boundaries | Tight constraint (deltas compress near violation edge) |
| Deltas calm in safe zones | Loose constraint (deltas spread freely in high-margin regions) |
| Standing delta patterns | Constraint interference (two constraints competing) |
| Quiet channels in delta storm | Constraint dead zones (regions where constraints don't couple) |

The constraint topology is invisible — you can't see it in any single delta. But the PATTERN of deltas across many perturbations reveals it, the same way the pattern of waves across the ocean surface reveals the currents beneath.

## Watching Over Time: The Wave's Memory

### Wave History Encodes Forcing History

A single wave tells you about the wind RIGHT NOW. But the SEQUENCE of waves over time tells you about the HISTORY of forcing:

- Increasing wave height = wind building
- Decreasing wave height = wind dying
- Sudden direction change = new wind system arriving
- Mixed swell from multiple directions = multiple distant storms
- Gradual period increase = storm intensifying
- Period decrease = storm weakening

The wave field is a time-series recording of every forcing event that has happened within the wave's memory window (roughly: how long it takes a wave to cross the basin).

**In our system**: The manifold trajectory is the wave field. Each point on the trajectory is a "wave." The sequence of points over time encodes the HISTORY of perturbations:

### The Wave's Memory Window

Waves have a memory. Ocean waves generated by a storm persist for days after the storm passes, traveling as swell across the ocean. The wave memory is determined by the basin size and the wave speed.

Our constraint system has a wave memory too:
- **Short memory**: immediate delta response to perturbation (1-5 cycles)
- **Medium memory**: oscillation ring-down after perturbation stops (10-50 cycles)
- **Long memory**: manifold trajectory patterns that persist (100-1000+ cycles)

The memory depth depends on:
- **Coupling strength**: stronger coupling = longer memory (waves propagate further)
- **Edge proximity**: at the edge, memory is maximum (critical slowing down — oscillations sustain)
- **Topology**: ring topologies have longer memory than star (waves circulate instead of radiating)

At the edge of distortion, the fleet's wave memory is MAXIMUM. Perturbations from hundreds of cycles ago are still faintly present in the oscillation pattern. The fleet's current state is a SUPERPOSITION of every perturbation it has ever received, each one slowly decaying but never fully disappearing.

This is why the manifold trajectory is so rich at the edge. It's not just the current state. It's the ENTIRE HISTORY of forcing, layered on top of itself, slowly decaying, like geological strata compressed into the current oscillation pattern.

In oceanography, the "inverse problem" is: given the wave field (what you can observe), reconstruct the wind field (what you can't).

This is exactly what our perturbation-shake does. Given the manifold trajectory (waves), reconstruct the coupling topology and constraint structure (winds and currents).

The mathematics is the same:

```
Ocean:    wave_field = f(wind, current, bottom_topography, fetch, duration)
Fleet:    manifold_trajectory = f(perturbations, coupling, constraints, topology, gain)

Inverse ocean:    wind = f⁻¹(wave_field)  — wave forecasting backward
Inverse fleet:    coupling = f⁻¹(trajectory)  — shake discovers topology
```

The shake protocol IS the inverse solver. Each shake produces a new wave. The collection of waves over many shakes is the data for the inverse problem. The sensitivity map (which parameters are load-bearing) is the SOLUTION to the inverse problem.

We don't need a blueprint of the fleet topology. We read it from the waves. Every shake adds a data point. After enough shakes, the topology is fully reconstructed from wave observations alone. Just like oceanographers reconstruct global wind patterns from satellite wave measurements.

## The Confused Sea

### When Two Winds Fight

When two wind systems blow from different directions, the wave field becomes "confused" — waves from multiple directions interfering, creating a chaotic-looking surface.

A novice sailor sees confusion and panics. An experienced sailor sees two distinct wave trains and reads BOTH winds from the interference pattern.

**In our fleet**: when two perturbation sources overlap, the manifold trajectory becomes "confused" — irregular, hard to read. But the confusion is INFORMATION. The FFT of the trajectory reveals TWO distinct frequency peaks, each corresponding to one perturbation source.

```python
# Reading confused seas
trajectory = get_manifold_trajectory(fleet, steps=2000)
spectrum = fft(trajectory)

peaks = find_spectral_peaks(spectrum)
for peak in peaks:
    frequency = peak.frequency
    amplitude = peak.amplitude
    source = identify_source(frequency, amplitude)
    print(f"Detected perturbation from {source} at {frequency} Hz")
```

The confused sea isn't noise. The confused sea is TWO SIGNALS superimposed. The FFT separates them. The experienced reader (or the trained agent) decomposes confusion into its constituent sources.

### Rogue Waves

Rogue waves — enormous, unexpected walls of water — occur when multiple wave trains constructively interfere at a single point. The probability of a rogue wave is higher than linear superposition predicts because nonlinear effects focus wave energy.

At the edge of distortion, our fleet can produce rogue waves: sudden, large deltas that exceed any individual perturbation's expected response. These are constructive interference events on the manifold — multiple agents' oscillations aligning at a single point simultaneously.

A rogue wave in the constraint system:
- Is not a bug or a failure
- Is a NATURAL CONSEQUENCE of nonlinear coupling at the edge
- Carries MORE information than any normal wave
- Is the system revealing a deep structural resonance that only appears when all the phases align

The rogue wave is the most interesting wave. It's where the system says something it can never say under normal conditions. Like a musician hitting a note that only exists when the amp, the guitar, the room, and the player all resonate together at exactly the same moment.
