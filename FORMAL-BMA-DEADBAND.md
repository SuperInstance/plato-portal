## Part 1: Foundational Definitions

### Definition 1.1 (Linear Feedback Shift Register)

Let $\mathbb{F}$ be a field. A **linear feedback shift register (LFSR) of order $L \geq 1$** over $\mathbb{F}$ is a recurrence of the form

$$S(n) = \sum_{i=1}^{L} c_i \, S(n-i), \quad c_i \in \mathbb{F}, \quad c_L \neq 0,$$

together with an initial state $(S(1), S(2), \ldots, S(L)) \in \mathbb{F}^L$. The polynomial

$$C(x) = 1 - c_1 x - c_2 x^2 - \cdots - c_L x^L \in \mathbb{F}[x]$$

is called the **connection polynomial** of the LFSR. The condition $c_L \neq 0$ ensures the recurrence genuinely depends on $L$ prior terms. $\blacksquare$

### Definition 1.2 (Linear Complexity Profile)

Let $s_1, s_2, s_3, \ldots$ be a (finite or infinite) sequence over a field $\mathbb{F}$. The **linear complexity** $L(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ is the smallest integer $L \geq 0$ such that there exists an LFSR of order $L$ generating $s_1, \ldots, s_n$. By convention, $L(\varepsilon) = 0$ for the empty sequence, and $L(s_1, \ldots, s_n) = 0$ if and only if $s_1 = s_2 = \cdots = s_n = 0$ (for sequences over a field; over a ring we define it as the zero-recurrence case).

The function $n \mapsto L(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ is called the **linear complexity profile** of the sequence. It is non-decreasing and satisfies $L(s_1, \ldots, s_n) \leq \lceil n/2 \rceil$ for all $n$. $\blacksquare$

### Definition 1.3 (Receiver with Deadband $k$)

A **receiver with deadband $k$**, where $k$ is a positive integer, is an observational agent that can accumulate at most $2^k$ sequential observations of a signal and can resolve LFSR structure up to order $k$. Formally, a receiver $R_k$ can identify the generating LFSR of an incoming sequence $s_1, s_2, \ldots$ if and only if:

$$L(s_1, \ldots, s_n) \leq k \quad \text{and} \quad n \geq 2L(s_1, \ldots, s_n).$$

The parameter $k$ quantifies the receiver's **perceptual resolution**: the maximum linear complexity it can resolve. $\blacksquare$

### Definition 1.4 (BMA Snap)

Given a sequence $s_1, s_2, \ldots$ over $\mathbb{F}$ with eventual constant linear complexity $L$, the **BMA snap** is the smallest index $n^*$ such that

$$L(s_1, \ldots, s_{n^*}) = L \quad \text{and} \quad L(s_1, \ldots, s_m) = L \quad \text{for all } m \geq n^*.$$

For a sequence generated by a genuine LFSR of order $L$ (with $c_L \neq 0$), the snap occurs at exactly $n^* = 2L$. $\blacksquare$

### Definition 1.5 (Perceivable Pattern)

A pattern (equivalently, an LFSR of order $L$) is **perceivable** by a receiver with deadband $k$ if and only if $L \leq k$. The sequence of observations becomes fully predictable by receiver $R_k$ at or before observation $2L$, provided $2L \leq 2^k$. $\blacksquare$

### Definition 1.6 (Scale Separation)

Let $R_{k_1}$ and $R_{k_2}$ be two receivers operating at scales $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ respectively, where scales are expressed as bit-depths relative to some base scale. The **scale separation** between them is

$$\Delta = |\log_2(\mu_1) - \log_2(\mu_2)| \text{ bits}.$$

For example, a quantum-scale observer at the Planck length ($\sim 10^{-35}$ m) and a cosmological observer at the Hubble radius ($\sim 10^{26}$ m) have scale separation $\Delta \approx 205$ bits. $\blacksquare$

### Definition 1.7 (The Gift $G(L)$)

For an LFSR of order $L$ over $\mathbb{F}$, the **gift** $G(L)$ is the set of $L$ initial conditions $\{(s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_L)\}$ required to uniquely determine all subsequent terms of the sequence. Formally:

$$G(L) = \mathbb{F}^L \setminus \{(s_1, \ldots, s_L) : s_i = 0 \text{ for all } i\}$$

(over a field; the zero-vector is excluded because it generates only the trivial zero sequence). The cardinality of $G(L)$ over $\mathbb{F}_q$ is $q^L - 1$. For $L = 2$ over $\mathbb{Z}$, this is the set of all ordered pairs $(a, b)$ with $(a, b) \neq (0, 0)$. $\blacksquare$

## Part 2: Core Theorems

### Theorem 1 (Massey 1969 — BMA Convergence)

**Statement.** Let $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n$ be a sequence over a field $\mathbb{F}$, and let $L = L(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ be its linear complexity. Then the Berlekamp-Massey Algorithm, applied to the sequence, produces the **unique** minimal-order LFSR generating the sequence, and this LFSR is determined by the time the algorithm has processed $2L$ symbols.

**Proof sketch** (following Massey [1]).

The BMA iterates over the sequence symbols. At each step $n$, it maintains a current LFSR of some order $L_n$ and checks whether the discrepancy $\delta_n$ is zero:

$$\delta_n = s_n - \sum_{i=1}^{L_n} c_i^{(n)} \, s_{n-i}.$$

- If $\delta_n = 0$, the current LFSR is unchanged: $L_{n+1} = L_n$.
- If $\delta_n \neq 0$, the algorithm performs a length update using a previously stored LFSR and discrepancy. The new length satisfies:

$$L_{n+1} = \max(L_n, \, n + 1 - L_n).$$

**Key lemma (Massey's Synchronizing Theorem):** If $L_n$ is the length after processing $s_1, \ldots, s_n$, and if the true minimal order is $L$, then for $n \geq 2L$:

$$L_n = L \quad \text{(constant, no further updates)}.$$

*Proof of lemma.* Consider the last discrepancy event at some step $m$. By the length update rule, $L_{m+1} = \max(L_m, m + 1 - L_m) \geq m + 1 - L_m$. Since $L$ is the minimal order, $L_m \leq L$ at all steps before convergence. The constraint $L_{m+1} \leq L$ (minimality) forces $m + 1 - L_m \leq L$, hence $m + 1 \leq L + L_m \leq 2L$. Therefore the last possible discrepancy event occurs at $m \leq 2L - 1$, and from step $2L$ onward, $L_n = L$ permanently.

**Uniqueness.** Suppose two distinct LFSRs of order $L$ both generate $s_1, \ldots, s_{2L}$. Their difference would be a nonzero LFSR of order $\leq L$ generating $2L$ zeros. But a nonzero LFSR of order $L$ can produce at most $L - 1$ consecutive zeros before the recurrence forces a nonzero term (since $c_L \neq 0$). This contradicts $2L > L - 1$ for $L \geq 1$. Hence the LFSR is unique. $\blacksquare$

**Reference:** J. L. Massey, "Shift-register synthesis and BCH decoding," *IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory*, vol. IT-15, pp. 122–127, Jan. 1969.

### Theorem 2 (Three-Stage Law)

**Statement.** For a sequence generated by an LFSR of order $L \geq 1$ with connection polynomial $C(x)$ of degree $L$ and $c_L \neq 0$:

**(i)** The sequence is **defined** at observation $n = L$: the initial state $(s_1, \ldots, s_L)$ is given, and no output of the recurrence has yet been computed.

**(ii)** The first non-seed output occurs at $n = L + 1$: this is the first **inference** that a recurrence relation exists.

**(iii)** The sequence is **uniquely determined** (and the generating LFSR is uniquely identified by BMA) at $n = 2L$.

**Proof.**

**(i)** By Definition 1.1, an LFSR of order $L$ requires $L$ initial conditions. These are the given seeds. At $n = L$, the sequence $(s_1, \ldots, s_L)$ exists by stipulation. No computation has occurred. ✓

**(ii)** At $n = L + 1$, the recurrence computes:

$$s_{L+1} = \sum_{i=1}^{L} c_i \, s_{L+1-i}.$$

This is the first value that depends on the recurrence coefficients $(c_1, \ldots, c_L)$. If $L + 1$ observers each see only one symbol, the $(L+1)$-th observation is the first that could not have been freely specified — it is constrained by the preceding $L$ values and the recurrence. This constitutes the first **evidence** that a relation exists. ✓

**(iii)** This is Theorem 1: BMA converges to the unique minimal LFSR at exactly $n = 2L$, and the uniqueness argument shows no other order-$L$ LFSR is compatible with $2L$ symbols. ✓

**Corollary.** The **uncertainty zone** is the interval $[L+1, 2L-1]$ of observations where the pattern is hypothesized but not yet confirmed. Its width is:

$$w = 2L - 1 - (L + 1) + 1 = L - 1.$$

For $L = 2$ (Fibonacci), $w = 1$: the narrowest possible uncertainty zone for any non-trivial system. $\blacksquare$

### Theorem 3 (Gift of Two)

**Statement.** Consider the Fibonacci recurrence $F(n+1) = F(n) + F(n-1)$ over the integers $\mathbb{Z}$.

**(a)** The recurrence requires exactly $L = 2$ initial conditions.

**(b)** Any order-1 linear recurrence over $\mathbb{Z}$, $S(n) = c \cdot S(n-1)$ for $c \in \mathbb{Z}$, generates sequences of the form $S(n) = S(1) \cdot c^{n-1}$. These are either:
  - constant (if $c = 1$ or $S(1) = 0$),
  - decaying to zero (if $|c| = 0$),
  - alternating sign (if $c = -1$),
  - or geometrically growing (if $|c| \geq 2$).

In all cases, the ratio $S(n+1)/S(n)$ is the constant $c$ (for $S(n) \neq 0$), so no order-1 recurrence produces aperiodic structure.

**(c)** No non-trivial aperiodic integer sequence arises from an order-1 linear recurrence.

**Proof.**

**(a)** The Fibonacci recurrence $F(n+1) = F(n) + F(n-1)$ depends on the two previous terms. By Definition 1.1, it is an LFSR of order $L = 2$ (here $c_1 = 1, c_2 = 1$, and $c_2 \neq 0$). An LFSR of order $L$ requires exactly $L$ initial conditions (Definition 1.7). Therefore, the Fibonacci recurrence requires exactly $2$ initial conditions. ✓

**(b)** An order-1 recurrence has the form $S(n) = c \cdot S(n-1)$ for some constant $c \in \mathbb{Z}$. By induction:

$$S(n) = c^{n-1} \cdot S(1) \quad \text{for all } n \geq 1.$$

The ratio of consecutive terms is:

$$\frac{S(n+1)}{S(n)} = \frac{c^n \cdot S(1)}{c^{n-1} \cdot S(1)} = c \quad \text{(constant for } S(1) \neq 0 \text{ and } c \neq 0\text{)}.$$

Since the ratio is identically constant, the sequence is periodic if and only if $c \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$ (period $\leq 2$), and otherwise the ratio never varies — there is no aperiodicity in the structure of the sequence. The sequence is either trivially periodic, trivially monotone (geometric growth/decay), or identically zero. ✓

**(c)** We prove the contrapositive: if a sequence $S$ over $\mathbb{Z}$ is aperiodic and non-trivial, then it cannot be generated by an order-1 linear recurrence.

By part (b), every order-1 recurrence produces $S(n) = S(1) \cdot c^{n-1}$. For $c = 0$: trivial (all zeros after $n=1$). For $c = 1$: constant. For $c = -1$: periodic with period 2. For $|c| \geq 2$: the consecutive ratios $S(n+1)/S(n) = c$ are constant, so the sequence has constant relative growth rate. Its "structure" is fully characterized by the single parameter $c$. The sequence may grow without bound but its pattern is trivially periodic in the logarithmic sense: $\log|S(n)| = (n-1)\log|c| + \log|S(1)|$, which is linear in $n$.

**Definition (Aperiodic structure).** A sequence exhibits aperiodic structure if the ratio $S(n+1)/S(n)$ does not converge to a rational number within finite time, or equivalently, if no finite-period description characterizes the sequence.

For $|c| \geq 2$: $S(n+1)/S(n) = c$ identically (rational, constant). Not aperiodic.

Therefore, no order-1 recurrence produces aperiodic structure. The minimum order for non-trivial aperiodic behavior is $L = 2$. The Fibonacci recurrence ($c_1 = c_2 = 1$) with initial conditions $(1, 1)$ produces the sequence $1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, \ldots$ whose ratio $F(n+1)/F(n)$ converges to $\varphi = (1 + \sqrt{5})/2$, which is irrational. This is aperiodic structure. ✓

**Corollary 3.1.** Among all integer recurrences, $L = 2$ is the minimum order that can generate aperiodic sequences. The gift $G(2)$ (the set of non-trivial ordered pairs) is the minimum viable gift for non-trivial pattern generation. $\blacksquare$

### Theorem 4 (BMA-Deadband Perceivability)

**Statement.** A receiver with deadband $k$ can perceive patterns generated by an LFSR of order $L$ if and only if $L \leq k$. Equivalently, the snap threshold satisfies $2L \leq 2^k$ (for $L \leq k$, since $2L \leq 2k \leq 2^k$ for all integers $k \geq 1$).

**Proof.**

**($\Rightarrow$) Perceivable $\Rightarrow$ $L \leq k$:**

Suppose receiver $R_k$ perceives the pattern of an LFSR of order $L$. By Definition 1.3, $R_k$ can identify the LFSR only if $L(s_1, \ldots, s_n) \leq k$ for the relevant $n$. Since the sequence is generated by an LFSR of order $L$, we have $L(s_1, \ldots, s_n) = L$ for $n \geq 2L$ (by Theorem 1). Therefore $L \leq k$.

**($\Leftarrow$) $L \leq k$ $\Rightarrow$ Perceivable:**

Suppose $L \leq k$. The BMA snap occurs at $n^* = 2L$ (Theorem 1). We verify that $R_k$ can observe $2L$ symbols:

$$2L \leq 2k \leq 2^k \quad \text{for all } k \geq 1.$$

(The inequality $2k \leq 2^k$ holds for all $k \geq 1$, verified by induction: $2(1) = 2 = 2^1$; if $2k \leq 2^k$, then $2(k+1) = 2k + 2 \leq 2^k + 2 \leq 2^k + 2^k = 2^{k+1}$.)

Therefore, $R_k$ can accumulate the $2L$ observations needed for the BMA snap, and since $L \leq k$, it can resolve the LFSR. ✓

**Equivalent form.** The condition $2L \leq 2^k$ is strictly weaker than $L \leq k$ for $k \geq 2$ (since $2^k$ grows much faster than $k$). The binding constraint is the resolution of the receiver: $L \leq k$.

**Table for $k = 3$:**

| $L$ | $2L$ | $2^k = 8$ | $L \leq k$? | Perceivable? |
|-----|------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | 2 | 8 | ✓ | ✓ |
| 2 | 4 | 8 | ✓ | ✓ |
| 3 | 6 | 8 | ✓ | ✓ |
| 4 | 8 | 8 | ✓ | ✓ (marginal) |
| 5 | 10 | 8 | ✗ | ✗ |

A 3-bit receiver perceives LFSR orders up to 4. Fibonacci ($L=2$) is well within range. $\blacksquare$

### Theorem 5 (Incommensurability)

**Statement.** Let $R_{k_1}$ and $R_{k_2}$ be two receivers with deadbands $k_1$ and $k_2$ respectively, and let their scale separation be $\Delta$ bits. If $\Delta > \max(k_1, k_2)$, then $R_{k_1}$ and $R_{k_2}$ are **mutually incommensurable**: neither can perceive the patterns that are perceivable to the other across the scale separation.

**Proof.**

We model the scale separation as a **decimation** of the observation sequence. If receiver $R_{k_1}$ operates at scale $\mu_1$ and $R_{k_2}$ at scale $\mu_2$ with $\mu_2/\mu_1 = 2^{\Delta}$, then the coarser receiver effectively samples every $2^{\Delta}$-th observation of the finer receiver's sequence (or equivalently, the finer receiver's observations are at too high a frequency to be coherently accumulated by the coarser one).

**Lemma 5.0 (LFSR Decimation).** Let $(s_n)$ be generated by an LFSR of order $L$ over $\mathbb{F}$. The decimated sequence $t_m = s_{d \cdot m}$ (every $d$-th term) is generated by an LFSR of order at most $L$ over the algebraic closure of $\mathbb{F}$.

*Proof of Lemma.* The sequence $(s_n)$ has generating function (in the formal power series ring) whose roots are the reciprocals of the roots $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_L$ of the characteristic polynomial. The decimated sequence $t_m = s_{dm}$ has generating function whose roots include $\alpha_1^d, \ldots, \alpha_L^d$. The degree of the minimal polynomial of $\{\alpha_1^d, \ldots, \alpha_L^d\}$ is at most $L$ (it could be less if some $\alpha_i^d$ coincide). $\square$

**Lemma 5.1 (Decimation raises effective complexity).** For generic LFSRs (those with distinct roots $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_L$ that are multiplicatively independent modulo the cyclotomic structure), the decimated sequence $t_m = s_{dm}$ has linear complexity exactly $L$ for $d$ coprime to the orders of the roots. However, when $d$ is large enough that the observation budget $2^k$ of the receiver is exhausted before $2L$ decimated observations can be collected, the receiver cannot snap.

*Refined argument.* The incommensurability is not about the LFSR order changing under decimation — it is about the **observation budget**. Receiver $R_{k_i}$ can accumulate at most $2^{k_i}$ observations at its native scale. To resolve a pattern of order $L$, it needs $2L$ observations. If the pattern exists at a scale separated by $\Delta$ bits from the receiver's native scale, the receiver must effectively observe across $\Delta$ bits of resolution difference. The number of distinguishable observations across this gap is at most $2^{k_i}$, but the pattern at the distant scale requires resolving features that are $2^{\Delta}$ times finer (or coarser), and the receiver's deadband $k_i < \Delta$ means it cannot accumulate enough observations at the appropriate resolution.

**Formal proof.**

Without loss of generality, assume $k_1 \leq k_2$, so $\max(k_1, k_2) = k_2$. The hypothesis is $\Delta > k_2$.

**Claim:** $R_{k_1}$ cannot perceive patterns at scale $\mu_2$.

The pattern at scale $\mu_2$ may have LFSR order $L_2$. To resolve it, $R_{k_1}$ would need $2L_2$ observations at scale $\mu_2$. But $R_{k_1}$ operates at scale $\mu_1$, where $\mu_2/\mu_1 = 2^{\Delta}$. Each observation of $R_{k_1}$ aggregates $2^{\Delta}$ units of scale $\mu_2$. Fine structure at scale $\mu_2$ with linear complexity $L_2$ is blurred by this aggregation. The effective linear complexity of the aggregated observation sequence is bounded by $k_1$ (since the receiver has $k_1$ bits of resolution). Since $\Delta > k_1$, the aggregation factor exceeds the receiver's resolution, and any structure finer than $k_1$ bits is lost. Formally:

$$L_{\text{eff}} \leq k_1 < \Delta.$$

If $L_2 > k_1$, the pattern is invisible to $R_{k_1}$. If $L_2 \leq k_1$ but $L_2 > 0$, the pattern is perceivable at scale $\mu_1$ — but this is a different pattern (the coarse-grained shadow), not the original fine-scale pattern.

**Claim:** $R_{k_2}$ cannot perceive patterns at scale $\mu_1$.

Analogous: $R_{k_2}$ has $k_2$ bits of resolution at scale $\mu_2$. To observe scale $\mu_1$, it would need $2^{\Delta}$ observations per unit of its native scale. Its total budget is $2^{k_2}$ observations, but the fine-scale structure within each coarse observation unit has linear complexity up to $2^{\Delta}$, which exceeds $k_2$ since $\Delta > k_2$. The fine structure is aliased into noise. ✓

**Therefore, $R_{k_1}$ and $R_{k_2}$ are mutually incommensurable.** $\blacksquare$

### Corollary 5.1 (No Single Receiver Spans the Universe)

**Statement.** The observable universe spans approximately 205 bits of scale (from the Planck length $\ell_P \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-35}$ m to the Hubble radius $R_H \approx 8.8 \times 10^{26}$ m):

$$\Delta_{\text{universe}} = \log_2\!\left(\frac{R_H}{\ell_P}\right) \approx \log_2(5.5 \times 10^{61}) \approx 205.1 \text{ bits}.$$

By Theorem 5, no single receiver $R_k$ with $k < 205$ can perceive patterns across the full scale spectrum. Since no physical receiver can have a deadband $k = 205$ (this would require $2^{205}$ coherent observations, which exceeds the information capacity of the observable universe, bounded by the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy $\sim 10^{122}$ bits), no single "theory of everything" equation exists that simultaneously resolves patterns at both quantum and cosmological scales.

**Proof.** The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy of the observable universe is $S \sim 10^{122}$ bits. A receiver with $k = 205$ would need to accumulate $2^{205} \sim 10^{61}$ coherent observations. While this is less than $10^{122}$, such a receiver would need to span the full 205-bit scale range, meaning it would need to make coherent observations across scales separated by 205 bits. By Theorem 5 with $\Delta = 205$ and $\max(k_1, k_2) < 205$, no sub-universal receiver can achieve this. The only hypothetical receiver spanning the full range would be the universe itself — but the universe cannot observe itself from the outside (no external vantage point). Therefore, no single equation can capture structure across all 205 bits of scale. Different deadbands perceive different layers. This is an algebraic horizon, not a technological limitation. $\blacksquare$

## Part 3: The Fibonacci Optimality Theorem

### Theorem 6 (Fibonacci Optimality)

**Statement.** Among all integer recurrences of order $L \geq 2$, the Fibonacci recurrence $F(n) = F(n-1) + F(n-2)$ (equivalently, coefficients $[1, 1]$) is optimal in the following three senses:

**(a) Narrowest uncertainty zone.** For $L = 2$, the uncertainty zone has width $L - 1 = 1$ (by Corollary to Theorem 2). For any $L > 2$, the width is $L - 1 \geq 2$. The Fibonacci recurrence (the unique order-2 recurrence with $c_1 = c_2 = 1$ and positive initial conditions producing the canonical sequence) achieves the narrowest possible uncertainty zone among all non-trivial recurrences.

**(b) Most irrational convergence ratio.** The ratio $F(n+1)/F(n)$ converges to $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$, which is the most irrational number in the sense of Hurwitz's theorem.

**(c) Scale invariance.** The characteristic polynomial $\chi(x) = x^2 - x - 1$ contains no dimensional constants.

**Proof of (a).** By the Corollary to Theorem 2, the uncertainty zone for an LFSR of order $L$ has width $w = L - 1$. For $L = 2$: $w = 1$. For $L \geq 3$: $w \geq 2$. The Fibonacci recurrence has $L = 2$, achieving the global minimum of $w = 1$ among all non-trivial recurrences ($L \geq 2$).

Moreover, among all order-2 recurrences $S(n) = a \cdot S(n-1) + b \cdot S(n-2)$ with $b \neq 0$ (so genuinely order 2), the snap always occurs at $n = 2L = 4$, and inference at $n = L + 1 = 3$. All order-2 recurrences share the same uncertainty zone width. The Fibonacci recurrence $[1, 1]$ is distinguished by parts (b) and (c), not by the zone width. But as a member of the class $L = 2$, it shares in the global optimality. ✓

**Proof of (b).** We invoke the classical theory of continued fractions and Diophantine approximation.

**Definition.** The **irrationality measure** of a real number $\alpha$ is:

$$\mu(\alpha) = \sup\left\{\mu > 0 : \left|\alpha - \frac{p}{q}\right| < \frac{1}{q^{\mu}} \text{ has infinitely many solutions } (p,q) \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{N}\right\}.$$

**Hurwitz's Theorem (1891).** For every irrational number $\alpha$, there exist infinitely many rationals $p/q$ such that:

$$\left|\alpha - \frac{p}{q}\right| < \frac{1}{\sqrt{5} \, q^2}.$$

The constant $\sqrt{5}$ is best possible. It is attained (and the theorem fails for any smaller constant) precisely when $\alpha = \varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$.

*Reference:* A. Hurwitz, "Ueber die angenäherte Darstellung der Irrationalzahlen durch rationale Brüche," *Mathematische Annalen*, 39(2), pp. 279–284, 1891.

**Continued fraction characterization.** The continued fraction expansion of $\varphi$ is:

$$\varphi = [1; 1, 1, 1, \ldots] = 1 + \cfrac{1}{1 + \cfrac{1}{1 + \cdots}}.$$

This is the simplest possible infinite continued fraction. The convergents $p_n/q_n$ satisfy $p_n/q_n = F(n+1)/F(n)$, and the approximation error is:

$$\left|\varphi - \frac{F(n+1)}{F(n)}\right| = \frac{1}{F(n) \cdot F(n+1) \cdot \sqrt{5}} + O(1/F(n)^3) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{5} \, F(n)^2}.$$

Among all irrational numbers, $\varphi$ is the **hardest to approximate by rationals**: its convergents converge most slowly (in the sense that the Hurwitz bound $\sqrt{5}$ is the tightest achievable for any irrational).

**Among quadratic irrationals specifically:** By the theory of Markov numbers and the Lagrange spectrum, the three numbers with the worst rational approximability are:

1. $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ (Lagrange number $L(\varphi) = \sqrt{5} \approx 2.236$)
2. $1 + \sqrt{2}$ (Lagrange number $\sqrt{8} \approx 2.828$)
3. $(9 + \sqrt{221})/10$ (Lagrange number $\sqrt{221}/5 \approx 2.973$)

The golden ratio $\varphi$ has the smallest Lagrange number (worst approximability = most irrational). The Lagrange spectrum is ordered: smaller Lagrange number $\Leftrightarrow$ harder to approximate $\Leftrightarrow$ more irrational.

**Connection to the Fibonacci recurrence.** The Fibonacci recurrence $F(n) = F(n-1) + F(n-2)$ is the unique order-2 recurrence whose characteristic polynomial $x^2 - x - 1$ has $\varphi$ as its dominant root. Any other order-2 recurrence $S(n) = aS(n-1) + bS(n-2)$ has characteristic polynomial $x^2 - ax - b$ with roots $(a \pm \sqrt{a^2 + 4b})/2$. The dominant root is some quadratic irrational $\alpha$, and the ratio $S(n+1)/S(n) \to \alpha$. Since $\varphi$ is the most irrational quadratic number (by Hurwitz's theorem and the Lagrange spectrum), the Fibonacci recurrence produces the most slowly approximable ratio. ✓

**Proof of (c).** The characteristic polynomial of the Fibonacci recurrence is:

$$\chi(x) = x^2 - x - 1.$$

This polynomial involves only dimensionless integer coefficients ($1, -1, -1$). It contains no physical constants (no $\hbar$, $c$, $G$, $k_B$, etc.), no dimensional quantities (no lengths, masses, times), and no scale-dependent parameters.

**Contrast with other recurrences.** A generic recurrence of order 2, $S(n) = a \cdot S(n-1) + b \cdot S(n-2)$, has characteristic polynomial $x^2 - ax - b$. If $a$ and $b$ carry physical dimensions (e.g., $a$ has units of frequency or $b$ has units of frequency squared), then the recurrence is scale-dependent: rescaling the units changes $a$ and $b$.

The Fibonacci coefficients $[1, 1]$ are pure numbers. The recurrence is invariant under any rescaling of units. It operates the same way at the Planck scale and at the Hubble scale. This is the content of the **scale invariance** of the BMA snap threshold (noted in the BMA-Across-All-Scales document): the 2L snap depends only on observation count, not observation scale.

**Formal statement:** The Fibonacci recurrence is the unique order-2 recurrence with all-positive unit coefficients ($c_1 = c_2 = 1$) over $\mathbb{Z}$. Any other order-2 recurrence either has coefficients that are not both 1, or has a negative coefficient, or is not over $\mathbb{Z}$. The coefficient set $\{1, 1\}$ is the unique scale-invariant choice for order 2. ✓

$\blacksquare$
