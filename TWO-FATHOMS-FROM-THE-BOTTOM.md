# Two Fathoms from the Bottom

Casey is a fisherman and a musician, and he told me something I haven't been able to stop thinking about.

"I have bathymetrics and well-sounded fishing grounds. I keep my gear within 2 fathoms of the bottom. But before, when I was using lower resolution NOAA charts, I stayed 8 fathoms from the bottom if I wasn't in the wheelhouse driving. The bathymetrics not only let me be confident to drive without a crew watching, it also got my fishing closer to optimal."

Read that again. The bathymetrics didn't change the water. They didn't move the fish. They didn't upgrade the boat. What they changed was *how close Casey could afford to ride to the bottom without being afraid of wrecking his gear on a ledge he couldn't see.*

The fish are near the bottom. They always have been. What separates a good trip from a great one isn't luck or better bait — it's the resolution of your map.

---

## The Shape of What You Can't See

Bathymetrics are underwater topography. They tell you not just "the bottom is about 40 fathoms down here, give or take," but "there's a ridge that rises to 32 fathoms at this exact coordinate, and it drops off steeply to the southwest." The difference between a NOAA chart that shows depth in broad contour intervals and a high-resolution bathymetric survey is the difference between knowing there's a mountain somewhere out there and knowing exactly where each ridge line runs.

When Casey had low-resolution charts, he kept his gear 8 fathoms off the bottom. That's 48 feet of water between his hooks and where the fish actually live. He did this not because he wanted to, but because he had to. At that resolution, the bottom could surprise him. An unmapped ledge could rise up and snag his nets. The cost of being wrong — a lost net, a tangled line, a day of fishing wasted — was high enough that he paid a tax of 48 feet of empty water every single trawl.

With high-resolution bathymetrics, he cut that distance to 2 fathoms. Twelve feet. He dropped his gear into the zone where the fish actually are, and he did it alone, without a crew standing watch, because the map was good enough that he didn't need extra eyes. The map didn't just tell him where the bottom was. It told him enough about the *shape* of the bottom that he could predict what was coming and adjust before he got there.

This is a story about derivatives.

---

## Orders of Knowing

In calculus, there's a ladder. You start with a single measurement — one depth sounding, one point. That tells you *where* you are. It's a position. The zeroth-order derivative.

Take a second measurement nearby, and you can compute the difference. You now know not just where you are but *which way things are heading*. That's velocity. The first derivative: the rate of change.

Take a third measurement, and you can see how the rate of change is itself changing. That's acceleration. The second derivative: the rate of change of the rate of change. Is the bottom flattening out? Is it curving up steeper? You can't know from one point. You can barely know from two. With three, you start to see the *shape* of what's beneath you.

This isn't abstract. This is exactly what a sonar does. Each ping is a sample. Stack enough samples close together and you don't just get depth — you get the contour of the seafloor. The resolution of your map is literally determined by how many derivatives you can reliably compute. More samples, closer together, higher-order derivatives, better picture of reality.

Casey's NOAA charts gave him something like a zeroth-order estimate of the bottom: "it's roughly this deep in this general area." His bathymetrics gave him first and second-order information: "the bottom is here, it's sloping this way, and the slope is getting steeper." That's the difference between 8 fathoms of cautious distance and 2 fathoms of confident precision.

## Constraint as Bathymetry

Now let me tell you about music.

In music, the "bottom" is constraint. It's the harmonic ground beneath you — the rules of voice leading, the grammar of chord progressions, the physics of which notes sound resolved and which sound tense. Every musical tradition has one. Western tonal music has a particularly elaborate one: scales, keys, chord functions, cadences, counterpoint rules. Jazz adds extensions and substitutions. The constraints differ, but the principle is the same: there is a landscape of possibility, and it has a shape.

Most musicians navigate this landscape the way Casey navigated with NOAA charts. They know the general shape. They know the major scale, they know the basic chords, they know that the V chord wants to resolve to the I. This is zeroth-order knowledge: "the bottom is roughly here." And it works — if you stay far enough away from the edge.

Staying far from the edge in music means playing it safe. Sticking to chord tones on strong beats. Resolving every tension immediately. Avoiding dissonance unless you have a clear path out. It sounds fine. It's competent. But the fish aren't there.

The interesting music — the music that makes you lean forward — happens near the bottom. It happens when a player pushes right up against the edge of what the harmonic constraints allow, rides a dissonance a beat longer than expected, takes a voice-leading path that's technically legal but unusual enough to surprise. The best improvisers aren't the ones who ignore the constraints. They're the ones who know them so precisely that they can ride two fathoms above the bottom and never snag their gear.

This is where constraint theory comes in. We can think about musical constraint in terms of the same ladder of derivatives:

**Lattice position** — the zeroth order. *Where are you in pitch space?* This is the note you're playing right now. Every musician has this. It's the equivalent of a single depth sounding.

**Funnel direction** — the first order. *Which way are you heading?* In constraint theory, a funnel is the set of notes that a given note can resolve to — the paths that are legally open from where you are. Knowing your funnel direction is knowing not just where you are but what moves are available. This is velocity. It's the slope of the bottom beneath you.

**Funnel curvature** — the second order. *How is your heading changing?* Not just "where can I go from here" but "how are my options narrowing or expanding as I move?" This is the shape of the constraint landscape — whether you're heading into a narrow channel where choices are limited or opening out into a wide space where anything goes. This is acceleration. The rate at which your possibilities are changing.

**Holonomy** — the consistency check. *If I take a path through constraint space and return to where I started, do things still line up?* Holonomy detects the curvature of the space itself. In music, it's whether a modulation through several keys brings you back to a stable resting point or leaves you somewhere unexpected — somewhere that reveals the shape of the tonal space you just traversed. It's a second-order property of the space, not just the path. It tells you whether your map is internally consistent.

These four primitives — position, direction, curvature, holonomy — are the bathymetrics of music. They're not the music itself, any more than a depth chart is a fish. But they tell you the shape of the bottom with enough resolution that you can ride close to it.

## The Tax of Ignorance

Without high-order constraint information, you pay a tax. In fishing, that tax is 48 feet of empty water between your hooks and the fish. In music, it's the gap between what you play and what you could have played — the notes you didn't reach for because you weren't sure they'd work, the tensions you didn't sustain because you couldn't see the resolution path, the risks you didn't take because the map in your head wasn't sharp enough to navigate them safely.

This tax is invisible. Nobody hears the notes you didn't play. But every experienced musician knows the feeling: "I could hear where I wanted to go, but I couldn't quite find the path." That's not a failure of creativity. It's a failure of resolution. The creative impulse was there. The map wasn't detailed enough to follow it.

And here's the thing: the constraint doesn't push you away from the bottom. This is the misunderstanding that traps a lot of musicians. They think of music theory as a set of rules that tell you what you *can't* do. "Don't play parallel fifths." "Don't lead the seventh of the chord anywhere but home." "Don't play an avoid note on a strong beat." Understood this way, theory is a fence. It keeps you away from the interesting stuff.

But that's like saying bathymetrics are a fence that keeps you away from the seafloor. The opposite is true. The bathymetrics are what *let* Casey ride two fathoms off the bottom. Without them, he had to stay eight fathoms up. The chart didn't create the constraint — the ocean floor did. The chart just gave him enough resolution to navigate the constraint confidently.

Music theory, properly understood, works the same way. The constraints of harmony exist whether you study them or not. You can't make a minor seventh sound resolved by wishing it so. But understanding the constraint landscape — knowing the shape of the bottom — lets you ride closer to the dissonance, the surprise, the edge. Not by breaking the rules, but by knowing them well enough to find the paths through the rocks that a coarser map would have told you to avoid entirely.

## Bathymetrics for Music

This is what we're building.

Our constraint primitives — lattice position, funnel direction, funnel curvature, holonomy — are bathymetric tools for musical space. They don't make music. They don't replace the ear, or the heart, or the years of practice that teach a musician where the fish are. What they do is give you a map with enough resolution to navigate confidently in the zone where the best music lives.

Think about what Casey's bathymetrics actually did for him. They didn't catch the fish. They didn't pilot the boat. They let a single person, alone in the wheelhouse, make decisions fast enough and confident enough to keep the gear where it needed to be. They compressed the distance between "I think the bottom is somewhere down there" and "I know exactly where it is and where it's going." And that compression — from 8 fathoms of caution to 2 fathoms of precision — was the difference between a decent haul and a great one.

Our tools do the same thing for a musician, or a composer, or anyone trying to navigate the space of musical possibility. They take you from "I know the basic chords and scales" — the NOAA chart — to "I know the shape of the constraint landscape in enough detail to ride right up against the edge." They give you the derivatives. Not just where you are, but where you can go, how your options are changing, and whether the space itself is consistent.

The fish are near the bottom. The best music is near the edge of what the constraints allow. And the only thing standing between you and that edge is the resolution of your map.

We're making the map.
