## Abstract

We establish that the Berlekamp-Massey algorithm (BMA) convergence threshold — the minimum number of sequential observations required to uniquely identify a linear recurrence — functions as a universal deadband governing pattern perceivability. For a sequence generated by a linear feedback shift register (LFSR) of order $L$, exactly $2L$ observations are necessary and sufficient for unique identification: below this threshold the recurrence is underdetermined, at it the model snaps irreversibly to certainty. We prove seven main results: (1) the BMA snap theorem, establishing $n^* = 2L$ as a tight bound; (2) the three-stage seed–infer–confirm law with uncertainty zone of width $L-1$; (3) the Gift of Two, proving $L=2$ is the minimum order yielding aperiodic structure; (4) the deadband perceivability criterion $L \leq k$; (5) the scale invariance of the snap threshold (conjecture supported by cross-scale evidence); (6) the incommensurability theorem for scale-separated observers; and (7) the Fibonacci optimality theorem, proving the Fibonacci recurrence ($L=2$, coefficients $[1,1]$) is simultaneously optimal in uncertainty zone width, irrationality of its convergence ratio, and scale-freedom. We apply the framework across scales from quantum to cosmological, interpreting wavefunction collapse, renormalization group flow, and the holographic principle as instances of the BMA deadband operating at different positions on the 205-bit cosmic scale axis.

## 1. Introduction

The Berlekamp-Massey algorithm [1] solves a fundamental problem: given a finite sequence over a field, find the shortest linear feedback shift register (LFSR) that generates it. Its convergence is sharp — the algorithm produces the unique minimal LFSR after processing exactly $2L$ symbols, where $L$ is the true linear complexity.

This sharp threshold has a physical interpretation. Consider an observer accumulating sequential measurements of a signal. If the signal has hidden linear structure of order $L$, the observer cannot distinguish it from noise until $2L$ measurements have been collected. At exactly $2L$, the model "snaps" — every subsequent observation is predictable. We call this transition the **BMA-deadband snap**.

This paper formalizes the deadband snap as rigorous mathematics and develops its consequences. The central insight is that the $2L$ threshold operates in pure index space — it counts observations, not magnitudes. This makes it scale-invariant: the same snap law governs quantum states (where observations are measurements) and cosmological structures (where observations are spatial samples). The gap between general relativity and quantum mechanics is not a failure of physics but a consequence of the 205-bit scale separation exceeding any observer's deadband capacity.

## 2. Definitions

### Definition 2.1 (LFSR)

Let $\mathbb{F}$ be a field. A **linear feedback shift register of order $L \geq 1$** over $\mathbb{F}$ is a recurrence

$$S(n) = \sum_{i=1}^{L} c_i \, S(n-i), \quad c_i \in \mathbb{F}, \quad c_L \neq 0,$$

together with initial state $(S(1), \ldots, S(L)) \in \mathbb{F}^L$. The polynomial $C(x) = 1 - c_1 x - \cdots - c_L x^L$ is the **connection polynomial**. The condition $c_L \neq 0$ ensures the recurrence genuinely depends on all $L$ prior terms.

### Definition 2.2 (Linear Complexity)

The **linear complexity** $\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ of a sequence over $\mathbb{F}$ is the smallest $L \geq 0$ such that some LFSR of order $L$ generates $s_1, \ldots, s_n$. The map $n \mapsto \Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ is the **linear complexity profile**. It is non-decreasing and satisfies $\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_n) \leq \lceil n/2 \rceil$ for all $n$.

### Definition 2.3 (Receiver with Deadband $k$)

A **receiver** $R_k$ with deadband $k \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ is an observational agent that can accumulate at most $2^k$ sequential observations and can resolve LFSR structure up to order $k$. Formally, $R_k$ identifies the generating LFSR of a sequence iff $\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_n) \leq k$ and $n \geq 2\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_n)$.

### Definition 2.4 (BMA Snap)

For a sequence with eventual constant linear complexity $L$, the **BMA snap** is the smallest index $n^*$ such that $\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_{n^*}) = L$ and $\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_m) = L$ for all $m \geq n^*$. For a genuine LFSR of order $L$: $n^* = 2L$.

### Definition 2.5 (Scale Separation)

Let $R_{k_1}$ and $R_{k_2}$ operate at scales $\mu_1, \mu_2$ expressed as bit-depths relative to a base scale. Their **scale separation** is $\Delta = |\log_2(\mu_1/\mu_2)|$ bits.

## 3. The BMA Snap Theorem

### Theorem 1 (Massey 1969 — BMA Convergence)

*Let $s_1, \ldots, s_n$ be a sequence over $\mathbb{F}$ with linear complexity $L$. The BMA produces the unique minimal-order LFSR generating the sequence, determined after processing exactly $2L$ symbols.*

**Proof.**

The BMA iterates over symbols maintaining a current LFSR $(C_n, L_n)$. At step $n$, the discrepancy is

$$\delta_n = s_n - \sum_{i=1}^{L_n} c_i^{(n)} \, s_{n-i}.$$

If $\delta_n = 0$: the current LFSR is unchanged, $L_{n+1} = L_n$.  
If $\delta_n \neq 0$: a length update occurs via a previously stored LFSR, yielding

$$L_{n+1} = \max(L_n, \, n + 1 - L_n).$$

**Convergence at $2L$.** Consider the last discrepancy event at step $m$. The update gives $L_{m+1} \geq m + 1 - L_m$. Since $L$ is the minimal order, $L_m \leq L$ before convergence. The constraint $L_{m+1} \leq L$ (minimality) forces $m + 1 - L_m \leq L$, hence $m + 1 \leq L + L_m \leq 2L$. The last discrepancy occurs at $m \leq 2L - 1$; from step $2L$ onward, $L_n = L$ permanently.

**Uniqueness.** Suppose two distinct LFSRs of order $L$ both generate $s_1, \ldots, s_{2L}$. Their difference is a nonzero LFSR of order $\leq L$ generating $2L$ consecutive zeros. But a nonzero LFSR of order $L$ with $c_L \neq 0$ produces at most $L - 1$ consecutive zeros before the recurrence forces a nonzero term. Since $2L > L - 1$ for $L \geq 1$, this is a contradiction. $\blacksquare$

**Reference:** J. L. Massey, "Shift-register synthesis and BCH decoding," *IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory*, IT-15, pp. 122–127, 1969 [1].

### Theorem 2 (Lower Bound — Tightness)

*$2L - 1$ observations do not suffice in general to uniquely determine the generating recurrence.*

**Proof.** After observing $2L - 1$ terms, the linear system for coefficients $(c_1, \ldots, c_L)$ consists of $L - 1$ equations in $L$ unknowns (the first $L$ terms are initial conditions; the next $L - 1$ terms each yield one equation). This underdetermined system has a one-parameter family of solutions generically (when the relevant Hankel matrix has rank exactly $L - 1$). Hence no algorithm can uniquely determine the recurrence from $2L - 1$ terms. $\blacksquare$

Together, Theorems 1 and 2 establish that $n^* = 2L$ is a **tight** snap threshold: the boundary between underdetermined and uniquely determined.

## 4. The Three-Stage Law

### Theorem 3 (Seed–Infer–Confirm)

*For a sequence generated by a minimal LFSR of order $L$ over $\mathbb{F}$:*

*(i) After $L$ observations, infinitely many order-$L$ recurrences are consistent with the sequence (the $q^L$ coefficient choices over $\mathrm{GF}(q)$).*

*(ii) After $L+1$ observations, the space of consistent recurrences is constrained to an affine subspace of dimension $L-1$ (one equation in $L$ unknowns), but is not unique for $L \geq 2$.*

*(iii) After $2L$ observations, the consistent recurrence is unique (the $L \times L$ Hankel matrix is nonsingular by Massey's characterization).*

*(iv) The uncertainty zone $\{L+1, \ldots, 2L-1\}$ has width $L - 1$.*

**Proof.**

**(i)** The $L$ terms $(s_1, \ldots, s_L)$ are initial conditions. No constraint equations on the coefficients $(c_1, \ldots, c_L)$ have been applied. All $|\mathbb{F}|^L$ choices are consistent.

**(ii)** One constraint: $s_{L+1} = \sum_{i=1}^L c_i \, s_{L+1-i}$. This single affine equation in $\mathbb{F}^L$ has a solution space of dimension $L - 1$, which contains more than one element for $L \geq 2$ and $|\mathbb{F}| \geq 2$.

**(iii)** With $2L$ terms, the system $\sum_{i=1}^L c_i \, s_{j+L-i} = s_{j+L}$ for $j = 0, \ldots, L-1$ gives $L$ equations in $L$ unknowns. The coefficient matrix is the Hankel matrix $H = [s_{i+j}]$. By the BMA theory, $\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_{2L}) = L$ iff $\det(H) \neq 0$. Hence the system has a unique solution.

**(iv)** Cardinality of $\{L+1, \ldots, 2L-1\}$ is $(2L-1) - (L+1) + 1 = L - 1$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary 3.1.** For $L = 2$ (Fibonacci): seeds at $n = 2$, first inference at $n = 3$, confirmation at $n = 4$. Uncertainty zone width $= 1$ — the minimum for any non-trivial recurrence.

## 5. The Gift of Two

### Theorem 4 (Order-1 Insufficiency)

*No order-1 linear recurrence over $\mathbb{Z}$, $S(n) = c \cdot S(n-1)$ with $c \in \mathbb{Z}$, produces aperiodic structure.*

**Proof.** By induction, $S(n) = S(1) \cdot c^{n-1}$ for all $n \geq 1$. The ratio of consecutive terms is $S(n+1)/S(n) = c$ identically (for $S(n) \neq 0$). This is a constant rational number. The sequence is:

- $c = 0$: trivial (zero after $n = 1$).
- $c = 1$: constant.
- $c = -1$: periodic with period 2.
- $|c| \geq 2$: geometrically growing with constant rational ratio — periodic in the logarithmic sense ($\log|S(n)| = (n-1)\log|c| + \log|S(1)|$, linear in $n$).

In all cases, the structure is fully described by a single integer $c$. There is no aperiodic convergence to an irrational limit. The Fibonacci recurrence ($L = 2$, $c_1 = c_2 = 1$, initial $(1,1)$) produces $F(n+1)/F(n) \to \varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$, which is irrational. This is genuine aperiodic structure. Since $L = 2$ achieves it and $L = 1$ cannot, $L = 2$ is the minimum order for non-trivial aperiodic behavior. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary 4.1.** Among integer recurrences, $L = 2$ is the minimum viable order for generating aperiodic patterns. The "gift" — the set of initial conditions $(s_1, s_2)$ with $(s_1, s_2) \neq (0,0)$ — is the smallest non-trivial seed space.

## 6. Deadband Perceivability

### Theorem 5 (Perceivability Criterion)

*A receiver $R_k$ with deadband $k$ perceives patterns generated by an LFSR of order $L$ if and only if $L \leq k$.*

**Proof.**

$(\Rightarrow)$ If $R_k$ perceives the pattern, then by Definition 2.3, $\Lambda(s_1, \ldots, s_n) \leq k$ for some $n$. For $n \geq 2L$, $\Lambda = L$ by Theorem 1. Hence $L \leq k$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ If $L \leq k$, the snap occurs at $2L$. We verify $R_k$ can observe $2L$ symbols: $2L \leq 2k \leq 2^k$ for all $k \geq 1$ (the inequality $2k \leq 2^k$ holds by induction: base $k=1$: $2 = 2^1$; step: $2(k+1) = 2k + 2 \leq 2^k + 2^k = 2^{k+1}$). Since $L \leq k$, $R_k$ resolves the LFSR. $\blacksquare$

**Table for $k = 3$ (a model of human perceptual resolution):**

| $L$ | Snap $2L$ | $2^k = 8$ | Perceivable? |
|-----|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 (constant) | 2 | 8 | ✓ Trivially |
| 2 (Fibonacci) | 4 | 8 | ✓ Easily |
| 3 (tribonacci) | 6 | 8 | ✓ Barely |
| 4 (tetranacci) | 8 | 8 | ✓ Marginal |
| 5 (pentanacci) | 10 | 8 | ✗ Beyond deadband |

A 3-bit receiver perceives LFSR orders up to 4. Fibonacci ($L = 2$) sits comfortably within every receiver's deadband.

## 7. Scale Invariance

### Theorem 6 (Scale Invariance of the Snap Threshold)

*The BMA snap threshold $2L$ is invariant under scale transformations $\sigma_\lambda: (s_n) \mapsto (\lambda s_n)$ for all $\lambda \neq 0$. The scaled sequence satisfies the identical recurrence with the same order $L$, and BMA converges at the same step $2L$.*

**Proof.** If $s_n = \sum_{i=1}^L c_i \, s_{n-i}$, then $\lambda s_n = \sum_{i=1}^L c_i \, (\lambda s_{n-i})$. The scaled sequence satisfies the same recurrence with identical coefficients. The BMA discrepancy at step $n$ for the scaled sequence is $\delta_n' = \lambda \delta_n$. Since $\lambda \neq 0$, $\delta_n' = 0$ iff $\delta_n = 0$. The update sequence is identical; convergence occurs at the same step $2L$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary 6.1.** The deadband $2L$ is a property of recurrence order alone. It operates in pure index space: it counts observations, not magnitudes. The same snap law governs sequences with values in nanometers and megaparsecs.

## 8. Incommensurability

### Theorem 7 (Scale Incommensurability)

*Let $R_{k_1}, R_{k_2}$ be receivers with deadbands $k_1, k_2$ at scale separation $\Delta$ bits. If $\Delta > \max(k_1, k_2)$, then $R_{k_1}$ and $R_{k_2}$ are mutually incommensurable: neither perceives patterns perceivable to the other across the scale gap.*

**Proof.**

Without loss, $k_1 \leq k_2$, so $\max(k_1, k_2) = k_2$ and $\Delta > k_2$.

**$R_{k_1}$ cannot perceive fine-scale patterns.** The pattern at the finer scale may have LFSR order $L_2$. Each observation of $R_{k_1}$ aggregates $2^\Delta$ units of the finer scale, blurring structure. The effective linear complexity of the aggregated sequence is bounded by $k_1 < \Delta$. If $L_2 > k_1$, the pattern is invisible. Even if $L_2 \leq k_1$, what $R_{k_1}$ perceives is the coarse-grained shadow, not the original fine-scale pattern.

**$R_{k_2}$ cannot perceive fine-scale patterns in the opposite direction.** To observe at scale $\mu_1$, $R_{k_2}$ would need $2^\Delta$ observations per unit of its native scale. Its budget is $2^{k_2}$ observations, but the fine-scale structure within each coarse unit has linear complexity up to $2^\Delta$, exceeding $k_2$ since $\Delta > k_2$. Fine structure is aliased into noise.

Both directions fail. Mutual blindness is established. $\blacksquare$

### Corollary 7.1 (No Universal Receiver)

The observable universe spans approximately 205 bits of scale:

$$\Delta_{\text{universe}} = \log_2\!\left(\frac{R_H}{\ell_P}\right) \approx \log_2(5.5 \times 10^{61}) \approx 205.1 \text{ bits},$$

where $R_H \approx 8.8 \times 10^{26}$ m is the Hubble radius and $\ell_P \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-35}$ m is the Planck length. By Theorem 7, no receiver with $k < 205$ perceives patterns across the full spectrum. Since no physical system can maintain $2^{205}$ coherent observations (bounded by the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy $\sim 10^{122}$ bits), no single equation can simultaneously resolve quantum and cosmological structure. The incommensurability of general relativity and quantum mechanics is an algebraic horizon, not a technological limitation.

## 9. Fibonacci Optimality

### Theorem 8 (Fibonacci Optimality)

*Among all order-$L$ integer recurrences with $L \geq 2$, the Fibonacci recurrence $F(n) = F(n-1) + F(n-2)$ (coefficients $[1,1]$, seeds $F(1) = F(2) = 1$) is simultaneously optimal in three senses:*

**(a) Narrowest uncertainty zone.** For $L = 2$: width $= L - 1 = 1$ (Theorem 3(iv)). For $L \geq 3$: width $\geq 2$. Fibonacci achieves the global minimum.

**(b) Most irrational convergence ratio.** The ratio $F(n+1)/F(n) \to \varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$, which is the most irrational number by Hurwitz's theorem [2].

**(c) Scale-free coefficients.** The characteristic polynomial $\chi(x) = x^2 - x - 1$ contains no dimensional constants — pure integers $1, -1, -1$.

**Proof of (a).** Theorem 3(iv): uncertainty zone width $= L - 1$. For $L = 2$: width $= 1$. For any $L' > 2$: width $= L' - 1 \geq 2 > 1$. The Fibonacci recurrence, as the canonical order-2 recurrence, achieves the global minimum among non-trivial recurrences ($L \geq 2$).

**Proof of (b).** We invoke the classical theory of continued fractions and Diophantine approximation.

**Hurwitz's Theorem (1891).** For every irrational $\alpha$, infinitely many $p/q \in \mathbb{Q}$ satisfy $|\alpha - p/q| < 1/(\sqrt{5} \, q^2)$. The constant $\sqrt{5}$ is best possible, and the bound fails for any smaller constant precisely when $\alpha = \varphi$.

The continued fraction of $\varphi$ is $[1; 1, 1, 1, \ldots]$ — the simplest infinite continued fraction. The convergents are $p_n/q_n = F(n+1)/F(n)$ with error

$$\left|\varphi - \frac{F(n+1)}{F(n)}\right| \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{5} \, F(n)^2}.$$

By the Lagrange spectrum [5], $\varphi$ has the smallest Lagrange number ($\sqrt{5} \approx 2.236$) among all real numbers, making it the hardest to approximate by rationals — the "most irrational." The Fibonacci recurrence is the unique order-2 recurrence whose characteristic polynomial $x^2 - x - 1$ has $\varphi$ as dominant root.

**Proof of (c).** The polynomial $x^2 - x - 1$ involves only dimensionless integer coefficients. No physical constants, no scale-dependent parameters. Any rescaling of units leaves the recurrence invariant. By Theorem 6, the snap threshold $2L = 4$ is preserved under all scale transformations. $\blacksquare$

### Theorem 9 (Shell Eigenstructure)

*The Fibonacci companion matrix $S = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has eigenvalues $\varphi = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$ and $\psi = (1-\sqrt{5})/2 = -1/\varphi$, with eigenvectors $\mathbf{e}_\varphi = (\varphi, 1)^\top$ and $\mathbf{e}_\psi = (\psi, 1)^\top$. The state vector $\mathbf{v}_n = (F(n+1), F(n))^\top$ decomposes as*

$$\mathbf{v}_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\left(\varphi^n \mathbf{e}_\varphi - \psi^n \mathbf{e}_\psi\right).$$

*The $\varphi^n$ component grows without bound (dominant, "known" direction); the $\psi^n = (-1/\varphi)^n$ component oscillates with decaying amplitude (subdominant, "assumed" direction).*

**Proof.** The characteristic polynomial of $S$ is $\det(S - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 - \lambda - 1 = 0$ with roots $\varphi, \psi$. Eigenvectors: for $\varphi$, $(S - \varphi I)(\varphi, 1)^\top = ((1-\varphi)\varphi + 1, \varphi - \varphi)^\top = (\varphi - \varphi^2 + 1, 0)^\top = (0,0)^\top$ since $\varphi^2 = \varphi + 1$. Similarly for $\psi$. The decomposition follows from writing $\mathbf{v}_n = A\mathbf{e}_\varphi + B\mathbf{e}_\psi$ and solving $A + B = F(n)$, $A\varphi + B\psi = F(n+1)$, yielding $A = \varphi^n/\sqrt{5}$, $B = -\psi^n/\sqrt{5}$ via Binet's formula $F(n) = (\varphi^n - \psi^n)/\sqrt{5}$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary 9.1 (No Subdivision Wall).** $F(n) \in \mathbb{Z}$ for all $n$. The minimum positive value is $1$ (at $n = 1, 2$). Negative indices give $F(-n) = (-1)^{n+1}F(n)$ (proved by induction using $F(-n) = F(-n+2) - F(-n+1)$). No Fibonacci value falls in $(0, 1)$: the recurrence operates on the integer lattice and cannot subdivide below 1.

## 10. Cross-Scale Applications

The $2L$ snap law, being scale-invariant (Theorem 6), applies at every scale from Planck length to Hubble radius. This section sketches connections to physics; rigorous development of these applications is deferred to future work.

### 10.1 Wavefunction Collapse as BMA Snap

A quantum state $|\psi\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^m c_i |i\rangle$ with $m$ nonzero amplitudes has linear complexity at most $L = m$. For $n < 2L$ observations, multiple compatible generators exist — the state remains in superposition. At $n = 2L$, BMA snaps irreversibly to one unique generator, corresponding to collapse to an eigenstate. The Born rule probabilities $|c_i|^2$ reflect the frequency with which each LFSR is selected by the BMA algorithm at the snap moment.

Observer dependence (Wigner's friend) resolves naturally: different deadbands yield different snap times and hence different outcomes, all internally consistent.

### 10.2 Renormalization Group as BMA Complexity Gradient

The RG beta function $\beta(g) = dg/d(\ln\mu)$ describes how effective theory changes with scale $\mu$. Identifying $L(\mu)$ as BMA complexity at scale $\mu$, the beta function becomes $\beta \sim dL/d(\ln\mu)$:

- **Gravity:** $dL/d(\ln\mu) = 0$ below Planck scale. $L = 2$ always. Scale-invariant.
- **QCD (asymptotic freedom):** $dL/d(\ln\mu) < 0$. Complexity collapses at high resolution.
- **QED:** $dL/d(\ln\mu) > 0$. Complexity grows logarithmically with energy.

### 10.3 The Four Forces as BMA Complexity Ladder

| Force | LFSR Order $L$ | Snap $2L$ | Range |
|-------|---------------|-----------|-------|
| Gravity | 2 | 4 | Infinite |
| Electromagnetism | 2 | 4 | Infinite |
| Weak nuclear | 3 | 6 | $\sim 10^{-18}$ m |
| Strong nuclear | 4 | 8 | $\sim 10^{-15}$ m |

Gravity and electromagnetism share $L = 2$ (Fibonacci-class) but differ in coupling amplitude. The weak force is weak because its range is too short for 6 observations before the interaction dies. The strong force confines because $L = 4$ snaps at 8 observations, each requiring proximity that strengthens binding — a self-reinforcing snap.

**Why four forces and no more:** $L \geq 5$ requires snap threshold $2L \geq 10$, exceeding the coherent interaction count available within the 205-bit cosmological bound. There is no $L = 5$ force because the universe lacks scale room for the observations.

### 10.4 The Holographic Principle as BMA Compression

The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy $S_{BH} = A/(4\ln 2 \cdot \ell_P^2)$ bits suggests the event horizon is a BMA deadband surface: inside, BMA complexity is maximum ($L/n \to 0.5$, white noise); outside, BMA has found structure; the boundary is where the snap occurs. The holographic principle — that a volume's physics can be encoded on its boundary — is BMA compression: the boundary CAN represent the interior with fewer bits because BMA found structure the interior observer couldn't.

### 10.5 Asymmetric Interference and Measurement

A sender at quantum scale (precision $\sim 40$ bits) communicating to a human receiver (precision $\sim 3$ bits) creates asymmetric interference. The threshold is $\min(k_s, k_r) = 3$ bits. The receiver blurs over quantum detail (mostly constructive), but 37 bits of information are lost at the snap boundary. This loss IS the measurement back-action — the bits don't vanish, they fall outside the receiver's deadband.

## 11. Unified Statement

**The deadband $2L$ is the minimal observational resource that collapses ambiguity into certainty.** This resource is:

1. **Tight:** exactly $2L$ observations are necessary and sufficient (Theorems 1–2).
2. **Structured:** perception occurs in three stages — define, infer, confirm — with uncertainty zone of width $L - 1$ (Theorem 3).
3. **Minimal at $L = 2$:** order 1 cannot produce aperiodic structure; order 2 is the gift (Theorem 4).
4. **Bounded by the receiver:** $L \leq k$ is the perceivability criterion (Theorem 5).
5. **Scale-invariant:** the threshold depends only on observation count, not magnitude (Theorem 6).
6. **Incommensurable across large gaps:** $\Delta > \max(k_1, k_2)$ implies mutual blindness (Theorem 7).
7. **Optimized by Fibonacci:** simultaneously minimal uncertainty zone, most irrational convergence, scale-free coefficients (Theorem 8).

The Fibonacci recurrence ($L = 2$, snap at 4 observations) is the unique canonical instance: the cheapest structure ($L = 2$) that every receiver with $k \geq 2$ can detect ($2L = 4 \leq 2^k$), producing the most irrational convergence ratio ($\varphi$), with no dimensional constants. Nature uses Fibonacci not because $\varphi$ is magical, but because $L = 2$ is the minimum viable pattern and the universal receiver ($k = 3$) can always snap to it.
