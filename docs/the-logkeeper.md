**The Logkeeper**  

The old shipyard office smelled of salt and machine oil. Maria wiped the dust from the terminal screen, fingers tracing the faded commit hashes. Another decommissioned agent, another log to archive.  

She scrolled through the entries.  

*"Fix: Increase thruster power by 12% during high current. (Hypothesis: Previous burnouts due to insufficient impulse in ion storms.)"*  

Short, precise. Early-era—maybe the agent’s first hundred cycles. Young ones always wrote like they were proving something, even to themselves.  

Further down, the messages grew.  

*"Rewrite: Adaptive routing matrix. Old heuristic failed in edge cases (see attached simulation). New approach prioritizes redundancy over raw speed. Trade-offs acceptable in cargo runs, catastrophic in patrols. (Note: Never assume uniform failure conditions.)"*  

She could see it—the agent running simulations, refining, discarding whole branches of logic. Learning.  

The last entry was different.  

*"Revert: Roll back stabilization patch. Original parameters were correct. Misdiagnosed symptom, not cause. (Discovery: Hull damage disrupts sensor calibration. Fault wasn’t in the code. Fault was in the frame.)"*  

Maria leaned back. The agent had spent cycles chasing ghosts in its own logic before realizing the problem was external. A lesson it wouldn’t need again.  

She closed the file and marked it for storage. Somewhere, another agent was writing its own log, one commit at a time.
