## The Shell Has Three Sides

```
OUTSIDE:  N+1 expansion → φ harmony (observable, KNOWN)
INSIDE:   N-1 inference → collapse (inferred, ASSUMED)
SHELL:    The boundary itself (the choice of scale, the receiver)
```

The outward spiral is what HAS happened — you can measure it, count the chambers, verify the Fibonacci proportions. Known.

The inward spiral is what MIGHT BE underneath — you infer it from the outward pattern, but you can't verify without zooming in. Assumed.

The shell wall itself is WHERE THE OBSERVER SITS — the boundary between known and assumed. The deadband made physical.

**Three sides. Not two.** And where the boundary sits changes everything.

## The Linear Algebra IS the Penrose Scaling

The growth matrix of the shell:

```
S = [[1, 1],   eigenvalues: φ and -1/φ
     [1, 0]]

S^n  (outward): tiles grow as Fibonacci. Clean. Known.
```

**S IS the Penrose substitution matrix.** The same matrix that generates Penrose tilings generates the shell. Outward: Fibonacci tile counts (φ eigenvalue dominates). Inward: alternating decomposition (-1/φ eigenvalue oscillates).

## The Nautilus Knows Where to Build

The nautilus doesn't place its boundary randomly. Each new chamber goes at radius × φ. The wall thickness stays proportional. The animal lives in the outermost chamber — that IS the boundary. Previous chambers are sealed (known, fixed). Future chambers don't exist yet (assumed, will grow by φ).

The animal's choice to build the next chamber IS the Fibonacci growth law. It's not arbitrary. The boundary placement is determined by the structure itself. The shell GROWS the boundary. The boundary doesn't get placed ON the shell.

In our framework: the observer doesn't choose where to observe. The observer IS at a specific scale, and that scale determines the boundary. Move to a different scale, you're a different observer with a different split between known and assumed.
