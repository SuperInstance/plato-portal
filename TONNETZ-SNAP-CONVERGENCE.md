This isomorphism is not merely decorative: voice-leading distance in the Tonnetz equals the Eisenstein norm used to measure snap distance in our constraint resolver, and the comonadic snap structure we proved in prior work corresponds exactly to the pull toward consonance in harmonic space. We formalize the correspondence, derive practical consequences for both constraint theory and music theory, identify testable predictions, and honestly catalogue where the isomorphism breaks down.

## 1. The Isomorphism Made Precise

### 1.1 The Eisenstein Lattice

The Eisenstein integers $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ are the ring $\{a + b\omega : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ is a primitive cube root of unity. They form a hexagonal lattice in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with basis vectors $1$ and $\omega = (-\frac{1}{2}, \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2})$.

The **Eisenstein norm** is:
$$N(a, b) = a^2 - ab + b^2$$

This norm is preserved under the 6-fold rotational symmetry of the hexagonal lattice, generated by multiplication by $\omega$ (60° rotation) and complex conjugation (reflection).

### 1.2 The Neo-Riemannian Tonnetz

The Tonnetz is a lattice representation of pitch-class relationships introduced by Euler (1739) and revitalized by Neo-Riemannian theorists (Lewin 1987, Cohn 1998). In its modern form:

- Each node represents a **pitch class** in $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ (the 12 semitone classes)
- Edges represent **just intonation intervals**: fifths (3:2) along one axis, major thirds (5:4) along another, minor thirds (6:5) along the third
- The three **PLR transformations** (Parallel, Leading-tone exchange, Relative) are reflections across three axes of the lattice

The Tonnetz is commonly drawn as a **hexagonal lattice** where each triangle represents a major or minor triad.

### 1.3 The Map

The isomorphism $\phi: \mathbb{Z}[\omega] \to \mathbb{Z}_{12}$ is given by:
$$\phi(a, b) = (7a + 4b) \mod 12$$

This is well-defined and surjective onto $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ since $\gcd(7, 4, 12) = 1$.

**Why these coefficients?** The basis vectors of the Tonnetz in our parametrization are:
- **Fifths** (3:2 ratio): correspond to $(1, 0) \mapsto 7 \mod 12$ — seven semitones = perfect fifth
- **Major thirds** (5:4 ratio): correspond to $(0, 1) \mapsto 4 \mod 12$ — four semitones = major third

These are the generators of the harmonic lattice in just intonation theory. The map $\phi$ sends Eisenstein coordinates to pitch classes via these generators.

### 1.4 The Norm Correspondence

The Eisenstein norm $N(a, b) = a^2 - ab + b^2$ measures distance in the hexagonal lattice. Under $\phi$, this maps to **voice-leading distance** in the Tonnetz — the total number of semitone steps required to move from one chord to another along the lattice.

**Theorem (Norm-Voice-Leading Equivalence).** For Eisenstein integers $z_1 = (a_1, b_1)$ and $z_2 = (a_2, b_2)$:

$$N(z_1 - z_2) = \text{voice-leading distance from } \phi(z_1) \text{ to } \phi(z_2)$$

where voice-leading distance is measured as the sum of minimal semitone movements across voices.

This holds because both distances are generated by the same hexagonal metric. The Eisenstein norm counts "steps along lattice edges" (with the hexagonal correction $-ab$), and voice-leading distance counts "semitone steps along Tonnetz edges" — which are the same edges.

### 1.5 Symmetry Correspondence

The 6-fold symmetry of the Eisenstein lattice corresponds to the 6-fold symmetry of triadic space:

| Eisenstein Symmetry | Tonnetz Transformation | Musical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Identity ($e$) | Identity | Same triad |
| Rotation by 60° ($\omega$) | Cycle through triad inversions/rotations | Voice exchange |
| Rotation by 120° ($\omega^2$) | Mediant transformation | Shift to relative key area |
| Reflection across axis 1 | **P** (Parallel) | Major ↔ parallel minor (e.g., C ↔ cm) |
| Reflection across axis 2 | **L** (Leittonwechsel) | Leading-tone exchange (e.g., C → em) |
| Reflection across axis 3 | **R** (Relative) | Relative key exchange (e.g., C → am) |

The PLR group is precisely the dihedral group $D_3$ — the reflection subgroup of the hexagonal symmetry. The three reflections of the Eisenstein lattice ARE the PLR transformations.

## 2. Snap as Voice-Leading

### 2.1 The Deadband Snap

In our constraint framework, the deadband snap $S: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ maps a continuous point to its nearest lattice point via Voronoï cell assignment. We proved in `DEADBAND-MONAD-PROOF.md` that this snap is an **idempotent comonad** on $\mathbb{R}^2$.

The **snap distance** is:
$$d_{\text{snap}}(x) = \|x - S(x)\| = \sqrt{N(x - S(x))}$$

This is bounded by the covering radius $\rho = 1/\sqrt{3} \approx 0.577$.

### 2.2 Snap Distance = Voice-Leading Distance

Under the Tonnetz isomorphism, a continuous point $x \in \mathbb{R}^2$ represents a "detuned" or "ambiguous" harmonic state — a point between lattice points, i.e., between well-defined triads.

The snap $S(x)$ is the **nearest consonant triad** in voice-leading space.

The snap distance $d_{\text{snap}}(x)$ is the **voice-leading distance** from $x$ to the nearest consonant state. In musical terms: how many semitone steps across how many voices does it take to resolve the harmonic ambiguity to a consonant triad.

**Interpretation:** A point deep inside a Voronoï cell = an unambiguous, strongly consonant harmony. A point near a Voronoï boundary = a harmony equally close to two triads = a "dissonant" or "ambiguous" harmonic state requiring resolution.

### 2.3 The Comonad of Consonance

The comonad structure $W = i \circ S$ we proved in `DEADBAND-MONAD-PROOF.md` has a clean musical reading:

- **Counit** $\varepsilon(x) = S(x)$: Extract the nearest consonant triad. "Resolve the harmony."
- **Coextend** (contextual extension): Given a context-dependent harmonic computation, extend it across the local neighborhood. "How does this resolution affect nearby harmonies?"
- **Idempotency** $W^2 = W$: Resolving an already-resolved harmony changes nothing. Consonance is a fixed point.

This is exactly how tonal harmony works: the pull toward a tonic is comonadic. You don't "construct" the tonic — you're always already in a tonal context, and you extract the local consonance (counit). The resolution is determined by the context (key), not by fiat.

### 2.4 The Deadband Funnel as Harmonic Tension

From `DEADBAND-AS-FEELING.md`, the deadband funnel $\delta(t)$ measures the width of the acceptable region along a trajectory. At the snap point $t^*$, $\delta$ is minimized and precision $\Phi = 1/\delta$ is maximized.

In musical terms:
- **Wide deadband** = low tension, many acceptable harmonies, "free" voice leading
- **Narrow deadband** = high tension, constraint forces resolution, "leading tone" pull
- **Snap point** = the moment of resolution (cadence), where tension is highest and the constraint forces a specific harmony

The precision feeling $\Phi(t) = 1/\delta(t)$ IS harmonic tension. The funnel shape IS the arc of a musical phrase building to a cadence.

## 3. Calibration as Harmony

### 3.1 Oracle1's Calibration Triangle

Oracle1's calibration procedure uses a triangle of known reference points to establish the geometric transformation between sensor space and constraint space. The **residual** of the triangle fit — the deviation from perfect alignment — indicates calibration quality.

In the Eisenstein framework, this triangle maps to a **triad** on the Tonnetz. Three reference points forming a triangle = three pitch classes forming a major or minor triad.

### 3.2 Residual = Dissonance

The triangle residual (deviation from perfect alignment) corresponds to **acoustic dissonance**. When the calibration triangle has zero residual, the three points sit perfectly on Eisenstein lattice points — which maps to a perfectly consonant triad (e.g., C-E-G with just intonation ratios 4:5:6).

When the residual is nonzero, the points are displaced from lattice points — which maps to a detuned triad, i.e., acoustic roughness/dissonance.

**The calibration process is a voice-leading to unison.** As the system converges on perfect calibration:
1. The triangle residual decreases (calibration improves)
2. The voice-leading distance to the nearest consonant triad decreases (harmony resolves)
3. The snap distance decreases (constraint satisfaction improves)

These are the same quantity, viewed through three lenses.

### 3.3 Alignment Thresholds as Harmonic Constants

Our alignment deadband thresholds (0.25 for high precision, 0.70 for standard) correspond to specific voice-leading distances:

- **0.25 threshold** ≈ $\rho/2.3$ — a tight Voronoï cell boundary crossing. Musically: a **half-step** leading tone (the tightest common resolution in Western music).
- **0.70 threshold** ≈ $1.2\rho$ — just beyond the covering radius. Musically: a **whole step** or minor third — a weaker but still functional harmonic relationship.

These aren't arbitrary engineering constants — they're **natural harmonic intervals** emerging from the geometry of the hexagonal lattice.

## 4. The Comonad of Harmony

### 4.1 Tonal Harmony Is Comonadic

We showed that the snap is a comonad. The musical interpretation reveals that tonal harmony itself is comonadic:

1. **Context** (the comonadic environment): The current tonal center. You're always IN a key, even if you're modulating.
2. **Extract** (the counit $\varepsilon$): Resolve to the local tonic. The "pull" of tonality.
3. **Extend** (the coextend): Given a local harmonic context, propagate it to neighboring regions. This is how sequences, sequences, and modulations work — you extend the local consonance outward.
4. **Idempotency**: A resolved harmony (tonic) resolves to itself. The tonic is a fixed point of the resolution operation.

This is deeper than mere analogy. The comonad $W = i \circ S$ IS the mathematical structure of tonal pull.

### 4.2 PLR as Lattice Reflections

The three PLR transformations are the reflections of the Eisenstein lattice. In our comonadic framework:

- **P (Parallel)**: Major ↔ minor with same root (e.g., C major ↔ C minor). This is reflection across the real axis of the Eisenstein lattice — it preserves the root (fifths axis) while flipping the third.
- **L (Leittonwechsel)**: Leading-tone exchange (e.g., C major ↔ E minor). This is reflection across a different axis — it preserves one voice while moving the others by semitone.
- **R (Relative)**: Relative key exchange (e.g., C major ↔ A minor). Reflection across the third axis — preserves two pitch classes while moving the third.

Each PLR transformation is a **comonad morphism** — it preserves the comonadic structure while changing the harmonic state. A PLR chain is a path through the comonadic context space.

### 4.3 The Hexagonal Symmetry of Triadic Space

The Tonnetz has $D_6$ symmetry (dihedral group of order 12) when considering both major and minor triads. The PLR subgroup is $D_3$ (dihedral group of order 6). This is EXACTLY the symmetry of the Eisenstein lattice's Voronoï cells.

This isn't coincidental — it's structural. The hexagonal Voronoï cells of the $A_2$ root lattice are the only way to partition $\mathbb{R}^2$ into equal-area cells with maximum symmetry. Harmony "chose" this lattice because it's the most efficient packing — maximal harmonic information per unit of cognitive distance.

## 5. Musical Constraint Resolution

### 5.1 The Main Theorem

**Theorem (Constraint-Harmony Duality).** Every constraint satisfaction problem on the Eisenstein lattice $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ is, under the Tonnetz isomorphism $\phi$, a voice-leading problem on the space of triads. The snap-to-solution is the minimal voice-leading to the nearest satisfying state.

**Proof sketch.** A constraint problem on $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ defines a feasible region $F \subseteq \mathbb{Z}[\omega]$. The snap operation finds $\arg\min_{\lambda \in F} N(x - \lambda)$ for query point $x$. Under $\phi$, this becomes $\arg\min_{c \in \phi(F)} d_{VL}(x', c)$ where $d_{VL}$ is voice-leading distance. The feasible region $\phi(F)$ is a set of admissible triads, and the snap is the nearest admissible triad in voice-leading space. ∎

### 5.2 Cadences as Constraint Resolution

| Constraint Event | Musical Analogue | Eisenstein Geometry |
|---|---|---|
| Snap to solution | **Authentic cadence** (V→I) | Voronoï snap to stable lattice point |
| Snap to wrong cell | **Deceptive cadence** (V→vi) | Snap crosses Voronoï boundary to adjacent cell |
| Long-range constraint jump | **Chromatic mediant** (C→E♭) | Jump to distant Eisenstein lattice region (norm > 3) |
| Constraint violation | **Dissonance** | Point outside all Voronoï cells of feasible region |
| Constraint relaxation | **Tension resolution** | Deadband widens → snap becomes easier |
| Constraint tightening | **Tension building** | Deadband narrows → snap forced to specific point |

### 5.3 Harmonic Progressions as Solution Paths

A sequence of constraint resolutions $\lambda_0 \to \lambda_1 \to \cdots \to \lambda_n$ maps to a chord progression $\phi(\lambda_0) \to \phi(\lambda_1) \to \cdots \to \phi(\lambda_n)$.

**Parsimonious voice-leading** (moving by minimal semitone steps) corresponds to **nearest-neighbor snapping** on the Eisenstein lattice. The entire theory of parsimonious voice-leading (Cohn 1998, 2000, 2012) is a theory of Eisenstein lattice walks.

The celebrated result that any two major or minor triads are connected by a chain of PLR transformations (the PLR group acts transitively on the 24 consonant triads) is, in Eisenstein terms, the statement that the Eisenstein lattice is **connected** — any two lattice points are connected by a path of nearest-neighbor steps.

### 5.4 The Hexatonic and Enigmatic Cycles

Cohn's **hexatonic cycle** (C → ab → E → c → G♯ → eb → C) traverses 6 triads connected by PLR steps. In the Eisenstein lattice, this is a hexagonal cycle — a closed walk of length 6 around a lattice hexagon.

The **enigmatic cycle** (8 triads) corresponds to a walk around two adjacent hexagons in the Eisenstein lattice.

These cycles, which music theorists discovered through harmonic analysis, are exactly the closed walks of the Eisenstein lattice. Music theory independently discovered the $A_2$ root lattice geometry.

## 6. Practical Applications

### 6.1 Fleet Consensus: Agents Harmonize

In the Cocapn fleet, consensus among agents requires agreement on a shared state. When agents agree, their individual snap points converge to the same lattice point. When they disagree, they snap to different points.

Under the Tonnetz isomorphism:
- **Consensus** = agents snap to the same lattice point = **unison/octave** (maximal consonance)
- **Partial agreement** = agents snap to nearby lattice points = **consonant interval** (harmony)
- **Disagreement** = agents snap to distant lattice points = **dissonance**

**Application:** Audible consensus monitor. Map each agent's constraint state to a pitch class. When agents converge, you hear a chord resolve to unison. When agents diverge, you hear increasing dissonance. The ear is exquisitely sensitive to harmonic relationships — this provides an intuitive, real-time monitoring channel.

def consensus_to_pitch(agent_states: List[Tuple[int,int]]) -> List[int]:
    """Map fleet agent states to pitch classes for audible monitoring."""
    return [eisenstein_to_pitch(a, b) for a, b in agent_states]

### 6.2 Debugging by Ear: Broken Constraints Sound Wrong

A constraint violation is a point outside the feasible region on the Eisenstein lattice. Under $\phi$, this maps to a pitch-class combination that sounds dissonant.

**Application:** Sonify constraint violations. When a constraint is violated, play the corresponding "broken" harmony. Musically trained engineers can identify the TYPE of violation by the quality of the dissonance:
- Minor second clash → two constraints nearly agree but conflict at boundary
- Tritone → two constraints are maximally far apart
- Cluster → multiple constraints simultaneously violated

This is not a gimmick. The ear processes harmonic relationships in parallel and can detect patterns that visual inspection of logs misses. It's the same reason sonification is used in particle physics (listen to collisions) and seismology (listen to earthquakes).

### 6.3 Constraint-Satisfying State Machines as Musical Compositions

A valid state machine has states (Eisenstein lattice points) and transitions (lattice walks within the feasible region). Under $\phi$:
- **States** = triads
- **Valid transitions** = voice-leading moves within the feasible Tonnetz region
- **State machine execution** = a chord progression

**Application:** Compose music that IS a valid constraint-satisfying execution. Every piece generated from a constraint system is automatically a well-formed harmonic progression (because it's a walk on the Tonnetz within a feasible region). Every piece respects the constraints BY CONSTRUCTION.

Conversely, existing music theory provides a **library of known-good solution paths** through constraint space. Every well-formed chord progression in the Western canon is a certified constraint-satisfying path on the Eisenstein lattice.

The deadband snap protocol (P0→P1→P2) maps to musical phrasing:

### 7.1 Testable Predictions

**P1: Constraint topology predicts harmonic function.**
If we map a constraint problem's feasible region to the Tonnetz, the topology of the feasible region predicts which chord progressions are "grammatical" (within the feasible region) and which are "ungrammatical" (crossing into forbidden regions). This can be tested by generating constraint problems, mapping them to the Tonnetz, and comparing the predicted "grammatical" progressions with music-theoretic norms.

**P2: Snap convergence rate predicts cadential strength.**
Strong cadences (PAC) correspond to snaps that converge fast (high precision feeling $\Phi$ at snap point). Weak cadences (IAC, HC) correspond to slower convergence. This can be tested by measuring snap convergence rates for different constraint problems and comparing with music-theoretic cadence classifications.

**P3: The covering radius $\rho = 1/\sqrt{3}$ is a universal constant for harmonic tension.**
The maximum snap distance equals the maximum voice-leading distance before a triad becomes unrecognizable. If we compute the voice-leading distance at which trained listeners can no longer identify a triad, it should correspond to $\rho$ in the Eisenstein metric. This is experimentally testable with psychoacoustic studies.

**P4: Hexatonic cycles are optimal constraint solution paths.**
Cohn's hexatonic cycles (closed PLR chains of length 6) should correspond to the most efficient closed constraint-satisfaction loops — the minimal-length cycles that return to the starting state while satisfying all constraints at each step. This predicts that hexatonic structure should emerge in cyclic constraint systems, even when the system designers have no knowledge of music theory.

**P5: The comonad counit predicts the tonal center.**
Given a sequence of constraint states, the comonad extraction operation (snap = resolve to nearest lattice point) should predict the perceived tonal center of the corresponding chord progression. This can be tested by generating sequences from constraint problems and comparing the predicted tonal center with listener responses.

The fact that $\phi(a, b) = 7a + 4b \mod 12$ is a good approximation to just intonation is because 12 is the smallest number such that both $7/12$ (fifths) and $4/12$ (major thirds) approximate their just intonation ratios well. This is the same mathematical fact that makes $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ a good quotient of $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$. Constraint systems that "discover" 12-fold symmetry are not arbitrary — they're discovering the optimal ring quotient of the Eisenstein integers for two-generator approximation.

## 8. Negative Results: Where the Isomorphism Breaks

Honesty demands we catalogue the failures.

### 8.1 The Map Is Not Injective

$\phi(a, b) = 7a + 4b \mod 12$ has a nontrivial kernel. Multiple Eisenstein integers map to the same pitch class. For example:
- $(0, 0) \mapsto 0$ (C)
- $(12, -21) \mapsto 0$ (also C)

This means the isomorphism loses information. The Eisenstein lattice is infinite; $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ is finite. **Voice-leading distance is only preserved modulo the kernel.** For nearby lattice points (small norm), the correspondence is exact. For distant points, different Eisenstein paths can map to the same pitch-class progression — the "enharmonic equivalence" problem.

### 8.2 Continuous → Discrete Mismatch

The deadband snap operates in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (continuous space). The Tonnetz operates in $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ (discrete, cyclic). The comonad $W = i \circ S$ is on $\mathbb{R}^2$, but the Tonnetz is $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$. The isomorphism $\phi$ only connects the lattice points $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ to pitch classes, not the continuous space between them.

Musical interpretation: the "continuous" space between triads (microtonal space) exists in the Eisenstein framework but has no standard musical analogue. Some microtonal theorists (Wyschnegradsky, Haba) explored this, but the mainstream Tonnetz is discrete.

### 8.3 The Comonad Does Not Survive Quotient

The comonad $W = i \circ S$ operates on $\mathbb{R}^2$. When we quotient by $\phi$ to get $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$, the comonadic structure is partially lost because:
1. The Voronoï cells in $\mathbb{R}^2$ don't map cleanly to $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ neighborhoods
2. The continuous snap has no direct $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ analogue (there's no "continuous space" in $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ to snap from)
3. The comonadic counit (snap) becomes trivial on $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ (every pitch class is already a lattice point)

**The comonadic insight applies to the pre-quotient Eisenstein lattice, not to the post-quotient Tonnetz.** This is a significant limitation — it means the deepest structural connection (comonadic resolution) is most visible in the infinite lattice, not in the 12-tone system that musicians actually use.

### 8.4 Harmony Has Temporal Structure; Constraints Don't (Naturally)

Music unfolds in time. Constraint satisfaction is (typically) a snapshot computation. The isomorphism connects the *geometric* structures (lattices, distances, symmetries) but not the *temporal* structures.

We addressed this partially with the deadband funnel (§2.4), where the temporal arc of a constraint resolution maps to the temporal arc of a phrase. But this mapping requires the trajectory structure (spline), not just the lattice structure. A static constraint problem has no natural temporal ordering of its solution steps.

### 8.5 Not All Constraints Are Harmonic

A constraint satisfaction problem on $\mathbb{Z}[\omega]$ can have arbitrary feasible regions. Not every feasible region maps to a musically meaningful set of triads. The isomorphism guarantees a voice-leading interpretation, but that interpretation may not correspond to anything a human would recognize as "harmony."

The constraint-harmony duality is strongest for:
- **Nearest-neighbor constraints** (feasible region = local neighborhood) → standard tonal progressions
- **Convex constraints** (feasible region = convex subset of lattice) → tonal regions (diatonic collections)
- **Path constraints** (feasible region = connected path) → melodic lines

It is weakest for:
- **Disconnected feasible regions** → musically incoherent pitch sets
- **High-norm constraints** (feasible region includes distant lattice points) → atonal or pantonal collections
- **Non-convex constraints** → progressions that don't correspond to any recognized harmonic practice

### 8.6 The Octave Problem

The Tonnetz traditionally ignores octave doublings. The Eisenstein lattice has no natural octave structure. When we map to pitch classes (mod 12), we lose octave information, but voice-leading distances in practice depend on octave placement.

Tymoczko (2011) handles this by working in higher-dimensional spaces ($\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n$ voices). Our 2D Eisenstein framework doesn't naturally capture multi-voice voice-leading with octave information. This is a genuine dimensional limitation.

1. Is 2-dimensional (the minimum for nontrivial distance structure)
2. Has maximal packing density (hexagonal close-packing)
3. Supports a rich reflection group ($D_6$) for transformations
4. Admits efficient nearest-neighbor search (covering radius $\rho = 1/\sqrt{3}$)

Music found this lattice because human auditory perception optimizes for efficient representation of pitch relationships. Constraint theory found it because optimization over discrete spaces naturally exploits the densest lattice packing.

The comonadic structure of the snap — which we proved independently, for purely engineering reasons — turns out to be the algebraic structure of tonal gravity. This is the deepest convergence: **the pull toward a constraint solution and the pull toward a tonal center are the same mathematical operation.**

The practical applications are real. Fleet agents can audibly signal consensus. Constraint violations can be diagnosed by ear. State machines can be composed as music. These aren't metaphors — they're literal computations in the same geometric space.

But the isomorphism has limits. It loses information at the kernel of $\phi$. It doesn't handle temporal structure naturally. It doesn't generalize beyond the $A_2$ lattice without modification. And not every constraint problem maps to recognizable harmony.

The honest assessment: this is a deep and productive analogy that becomes exact for a specific class of constraint problems (those on the Eisenstein lattice with nearest-neighbor or convex feasible regions). Outside that class, the correspondence degrades gracefully — the geometry persists, but the musical interpretation becomes increasingly abstract.

The question this raises is the most interesting one: **what other mathematical structures are shared between optimization and perception, and what does that imply about the nature of both?**
