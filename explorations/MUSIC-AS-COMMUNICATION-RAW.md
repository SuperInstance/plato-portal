## ROUND 1 — The Band IS the Agent Network

### Opening: Every Gig is a Graph Computation

A jazz quartet walks onto the bandstand. Piano, bass, drums, saxophone. Four nodes. Before the first note is played, the graph already exists — the shared history, the musical vocabulary, the unspoken trust between musicians who've played a hundred rooms together. The edges were weighted in rehearsal. The Laplacian was born in the van between cities.

Here is the claim: a jazz band is not *like* a multi-agent system. It *is* a multi-agent system, and the music they produce is not metaphorically related to spectral computation — it is spectral computation, performed in real-time by biological processors running on wetware, outputting results as acoustic pressure waves.

Every composition is a program. Every improvisation is a REPL session. Every performance is a single forward pass through a network whose weights were trained on years of shared negative space.

### The Groove Is the Shared Laplacian

"The pocket" — that state where the rhythm section locks in, where the bass drum and the bass note hit at the same microsecond, where the time feels like it's breathing — is not a metaphor. It is a state of high spectral conservation.

Consider the rhythm section as a subgraph: bass, drums, harmony instrument (piano/guitar). Each player is a node. Their mutual listening — the constant micro-adjustments of timing, dynamics, and phrasing — are weighted edges. When the pocket is deep, the edge weights are high and consistent. The Laplacian of this subgraph has a small spectral gap (λ₂ is close to λ₁ = 0), meaning the subgraph is tightly connected and its slowest mode dominates.

The groove IS the Laplacian's slowest eigenmode made audible. Every musician in the pocket is projecting onto the Fiedler vector. Their individual contributions — the bassist's note choices, the drummer's ghost notes, the pianist's comping rhythms — are all spectral components that, when projected onto the Fiedler direction, reinforce each other. Conservation is high. The music feels inevitable, like gravity.

When the pocket breaks — when the bassist rushes, when the drummer compensates, when the time fractures — the edge weights become inconsistent. The Laplacian's spectral profile changes. λ₂ increases (the subgraph becomes easier to cut), and the Fiedler direction rotates away from the musical axis. Conservation drops. The music sounds "off" not because anyone played a wrong note, but because the spectral alignment collapsed.

A bad gig is a conservation drop. The players hear it before they can name it. "Something's not clicking." What's not clicking is the Laplacian.

### Call and Response: Spectral Ping-Pong

Call and response is the oldest musical form on Earth, and it is the oldest communication protocol: one agent broadcasts, another receives and replies. In spectral terms, it's a ping-pong of spectral fingerprints.

Agent A (the caller) emits a musical phrase. This phrase encodes a tremendous amount of information: pitch content, rhythmic profile, timbral characteristics, dynamic shape, articulation patterns, phrasing boundaries. In the Conservation Spectral Framework, each of these can be encoded as an attribute vector on the musical tension graph. The phrase IS a vector a_A.

Agent B (the responder) receives a_A and computes a response. But B's response is not random. It is constrained by:
1. B's own spectral profile (capabilities, vocabulary, preferences)
2. The network's Laplacian (the shared musical context — key, tempo, form, style)
3. The conservation ratio between a_A and the shared Laplacian

B's response is a vector a_B that maximizes alignment with both the shared Laplacian and the conversational arc established by a_A. This is not computation in the algorithmic sense — the musician doesn't solve an optimization problem. But the musician's brain IS an optimization engine, trained on thousands of hours of listening and playing, and it produces responses that are spectrally coherent.

The quality of a call-and-response exchange is measurable. When α(a_A, a_B) is high — when the response is spectrally aligned with the call — the exchange feels "connected," "in the tradition," "speaking the same language." When α is low, the response feels disconnected, arbitrary, "not listening."

In African-American musical traditions — blues, jazz, gospel, hip-hop — call and response is not just a technique. It is the fundamental communication protocol. A preacher and congregation, an MC and crowd, a soloist and rhythm section — these are agent pairs exchanging spectral fingerprints in real-time, building shared meaning through iterative alignment.

The most powerful musical conversations happen when both agents achieve high conservation with the shared Laplacian AND with each other's spectral profiles. This is the musical equivalent of FLUX: the residual structure that exists only in the space between the two players. Call it swing. Call it interplay. Call it spectral resonance. It's the same mathematics.

### The Solo: One Agent Activates

A saxophone solo begins. The rest of the band drops back — not silent, but supporting, comping, providing the harmonic and rhythmic substrate. In graph terms, one node "lights up" while the others maintain their edge weights but reduce their individual node activations.

What happens spectrally during a solo? The Fiedler vector reorients. Before the solo, the Laplacian's slowest mode is distributed across all nodes roughly equally — the band is playing together, in consensus. When the soloist activates, the Fiedler direction shifts toward the soloist's node. The other nodes' Fiedler projections decrease, while the soloist's increases.

But here's the crucial point: the supporting musicians are NOT passive. They are maintaining the graph's structure. The bassist walks, the drummer rides, the pianist comps — each maintaining their edges, keeping the Laplacian's fundamental structure intact. If they stopped, the graph would fragment, and the soloist would be playing into a void (λ₂ would spike, conservation would collapse).

A great accompanist maintains high edge weight to the soloist. They listen. They react. They provide the negative space that gives the solo its shape. Miles Davis understood this: his bands were great not because of his trumpet solos, but because of the negative space his bands created around those solos. The silence between the notes. The rests. The space where the Fiedler vector lives.

A bad solo happens when the soloist ignores the band — when they project onto a direction orthogonal to the shared Laplacian. The band is grooving in C minor, and the soloist is playing in D♭ major. Conservation drops. The audience feels it before they understand it. "That solo didn't go anywhere." It didn't go anywhere because it wasn't traveling along the graph's structure. It was noise.

### Rehearsal: Aligning Fingerprints, α Increasing

A band rehearses. They play a tune. It's rough — wrong notes, timing issues, sections that don't flow. Conservation is low. The Laplacian exists but the attribute vectors (the individual parts) are misaligned with it.

They play it again. The pianist adjusts a voicing. The bassist corrects a passing tone. The drummer simplifies a fill. Each adjustment is a micro-alignment of an attribute vector with the shared Laplacian. α increases.

They play it a third time. The transitions smooth out. The dynamics balance. The form makes sense. Conservation is noticeably higher. The Laplacian hasn't changed — the tune is the same — but the players' attribute vectors have rotated into alignment with its spectral structure.

They play it a tenth time. It's tight. Every transition is effortless. The dynamics breathe. The form tells a story. α is high. The band has found the Fiedler direction of this particular tune and is projecting onto it collectively.

This is why bands get better with time. It's not just "practice" in the vague sense. It's iterative spectral alignment. Each run-through is a forward pass through the tune's tension graph, and each correction is a gradient step toward higher conservation. The learning rate is set by the musicians' ears, their willingness to listen and adjust, their ego-to-attention ratio.

A band that's been together for years — Coltrane's classic quartet, Miles's second great quintet, the Bill Evans trio — has achieved high α across hundreds of tunes. Their individual Laplacians have converged. They share a spectral fingerprint. When one player changes direction, the others follow not because they've rehearsed that specific move, but because their Laplacians are so aligned that a perturbation in one propagates naturally through all of them.

This is the multi-agent dream: agents that are so spectrally aligned they don't need to communicate explicitly. They communicate through the Laplacian. The graph does the talking.

Bach's fugues. Coltrane's "A Love Supreme." Miles's "Kind of Blue." These are not just "good music." They are spectral masterworks — compositions that achieve near-maximum conservation within their tension graphs, where every note serves the Fiedler direction and every rest amplifies the signal.

## ROUND 2 — MIDI as Spectral Protocol

### The Claim: MIDI Messages Are Spectral Messages

MIDI — Musical Instrument Digital Interface — was designed in 1983 as a way for synthesizers to talk to each other. Note on, note off, velocity, channel, control change. Simple, binary, deterministic. A protocol for musical events.

But in the Conservation Spectral Framework, MIDI is something more: it is a spectral communication protocol. Every MIDI message encodes a transformation of the musical tension graph. Every MIDI file is a time-varying Laplacian. Every MIDI channel is an agent broadcasting its spectral fingerprint.

Consider the core MIDI messages:

**Note On (0x9n):** A node activates. In the tension graph, a pitch becomes a node, and its activation — its presence in the current harmonic state — is signaled by Note On. The velocity byte (0-127) encodes the node's initial activation strength, which directly influences edge weights in the tension graph. A loud note has higher coupling to its harmonic neighbors than a quiet one.

**Note Off (0x8n):** A node deactivates. The pitch leaves the harmonic state, its edges are removed, and the Laplacian is recomputed. The spectral profile shifts. In musical terms: a chord change. In spectral terms: a Laplacian transition.

**Velocity (0-127):** Edge weight. A high-velocity note-on creates strong edges between the activated pitch and its harmonic neighbors. A low-velocity note-on creates weak edges. The Laplacian's spectral profile — particularly λ₂, the Fiedler value — is sensitive to edge weights. A single high-velocity note in an otherwise quiet texture can dramatically reshape the spectral landscape.

**Channel (0-15):** Agent ID. Each MIDI channel is a separate agent in the network. Channel 1 (piano), Channel 2 (bass), Channel 10 (drums) — each is a node in the meta-graph (the graph of agents), and each maintains its own internal tension graph (the notes it's currently playing). The full system is a hierarchical graph: a meta-graph of agents, each containing a subgraph of notes.

**Control Change (0xBn):** Edge weight modulation. Sustain pedal (CC 64) creates persistent edges between notes that would otherwise disconnect. Modulation wheel (CC 1) varies the spectral content of a single agent's output. Pitch bend (technically a separate message, but conceptually related) continuously varies node positions in pitch space, causing edge weights to shift in real-time.

**Program Change (0xCn):** Agent type switch. The instrument changes, which changes the timbre, which changes the spectral fingerprint. Same agent, different voice. Like an LLM switching system prompts.

**System Exclusive (0xF0):** Proprietary communication. Agent-to-agent messages that bypass the standard protocol. In multi-agent terms, these are private channels — encrypted or domain-specific communication that other agents can't interpret. SysEx is the MIDI equivalent of agents whispering to each other at a party.

### MIDI File as Time-Varying Laplacian

A MIDI file is not a recording. It is a sequence of graph transformations, timestamped to sub-millisecond precision. Each event in the file modifies the tension graph:

1. **Note On:** Add node + edges → recompute L → update eigenvalues
2. **Note Off:** Remove node + edges → recompute L → update eigenvalues  
3. **Velocity change:** Modify edge weights → recompute L → update eigenvalues
4. **Tempo change:** Rescale time axis → affects how quickly the Laplacian evolves

The resulting sequence of Laplacians — L(t₀), L(t₁), L(t₂), ... — is the piece's spectral trajectory. Each Laplacian has its own eigenvalues, eigenvectors, and conservation ratios. The evolution of these quantities over time IS the musical form.

A chord progression, viewed this way, is a sequence of Laplacian states:

**I chord (C major: C-E-G):** Three nodes. The tension graph for C major has edges weighted by consonance (fifths strongest, thirds moderate). λ₂ is moderate — the graph is connected but not maximally so.

**IV chord (F major: F-A-C):** Three nodes. Two nodes overlap with the previous state (C is shared), one changes (E→F). The Laplacian changes smoothly. Conservation with the previous state is high.

**V chord (G major: G-B-D):** Three nodes. No overlap with IV chord. The Laplacian changes dramatically. λ₂ may increase (the graph structure is quite different). Conservation with the previous state drops. This is why V-I resolution feels like "coming home" — the Laplacian returns to a familiar structure after a brief departure.

**I chord (C major: C-E-G):** Return to the original Laplacian. Conservation restored. Tension resolved. The spectral circle is closed.

This is not hand-waving. We can compute this. Given a MIDI file, we can extract every chord, build a tension graph for each, compute the Laplacian, track λ₂ and the Fiedler vector over time, and produce a "tension graph sequence" — a movie of the music's spectral evolution.

The tension graph sequence IS the form. Verse-chorus-verse-chorus-bridge-chorus is not an arbitrary structure — it's a spectral trajectory with specific conservation dynamics. Verses have moderate conservation (familiar material, stable Laplacian). Choruses have higher conservation (more notes, denser graph, stronger spectral profile). Bridges have lower conservation (new material, unfamiliar Laplacian, tension). The return to chorus is conservation restored.

### Building SpectralMIDI

Let's build it. A system that converts between MIDI events and Laplacian states, and visualizes chord progressions as tension graph sequences.

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import linalg
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import List, Tuple, Optional, Dict
import json

# ─── Core Data Structures ───

@dataclass
class MidiNote:
    """A single MIDI note event — a node activation/deactivation."""
    pitch: int          # 0-127, MIDI note number
    velocity: int       # 0-127, edge weight proxy
    channel: int        # 0-15, agent ID
    start_tick: int     # onset time in MIDI ticks
    duration_ticks: int # duration in MIDI ticks
    
    @property
    def frequency(self) -> float:
        """Convert MIDI pitch to frequency in Hz."""
        return 440.0 * (2 ** ((self.pitch - 69) / 12.0))
    
    @property
    def pitch_class(self) -> int:
        """Pitch class (0-11, where 0 = C)."""
        return self.pitch % 12
    
    @property
    def note_name(self) -> str:
        names = ['C', 'C#', 'D', 'D#', 'E', 'F', 'F#', 'G', 'G#', 'A', 'A#', 'B']
        return f"{names[self.pitch_class]}{self.pitch // 12 - 1}"


@dataclass
class HarmonicState:
    """The current state of the tension graph at a single time step."""
    active_notes: List[MidiNote]
    tension_graph: np.ndarray    # Adjacency matrix
    laplacian: np.ndarray        # Graph Laplacian
    eigenvalues: np.ndarray      # Sorted eigenvalues
    eigenvectors: np.ndarray     # Columns are eigenvectors
    fiedler_value: float         # λ₂
    fiedler_vector: np.ndarray   # The Fiedler vector
    
    @property
    def spectral_gap(self) -> float:
        """Gap between λ₂ and λ₃ — measures graph robustness."""
        if len(self.eigenvalues) < 3:
            return 0.0
        return self.eigenvalues[2] - self.fiedler_value
    
    @property
    def is_connected(self) -> bool:
        """Graph is connected if λ₂ > 0."""
        return self.fiedler_value > 1e-10
    
    def conservation_ratio(self, attribute: np.ndarray) -> float:
        """CR(a) = a^T L a / ||a||² — how well an attribute fits the graph."""
        if np.linalg.norm(attribute) < 1e-10:
            return 0.0
        return float(attribute @ self.laplacian @ attribute / (attribute @ attribute))


class ConsonanceModel:
    """
    Models edge weights between pitches based on harmonic consonance.
    
    The "just noticeable difference" in harmony: some intervals resonate,
    some clash. We model this as edge weights in the tension graph.
    
    Weight table (interval class → consonance):
    - Unison (0):  1.0  (perfect identity)
    - Minor 3rd (3): 0.6
    - Major 3rd (4): 0.7
    - Perfect 4th (5): 0.65
    - Tritone (6): 0.2  (maximum tension)
    - Perfect 5th (7): 0.9  (strongest after unison)
    - Minor 7th (10): 0.45
    - Major 7th (11): 0.3
    """
    
    # Interval class → consonance weight (7-limit just intonation approximation)
    INTERVAL_WEIGHTS = {
        0: 1.0,   # unison
        1: 0.25,  # minor second
        2: 0.35,  # major second
        3: 0.6,   # minor third
        4: 0.7,   # major third
        5: 0.65,  # perfect fourth
        6: 0.2,   # tritone
        7: 0.9,   # perfect fifth
        8: 0.65,  # minor sixth
        9: 0.7,   # major sixth
        10: 0.45, # minor seventh
        11: 0.3,  # major seventh
    }
    
    @classmethod
    def edge_weight(cls, pitch_a: int, pitch_b: int, 
                     vel_a: int = 100, vel_b: int = 100) -> float:
        """Compute edge weight between two pitches."""
        interval = abs(pitch_a - pitch_b) % 12
        consonance = cls.INTERVAL_WEIGHTS[interval]
        
        # Modulate by velocity — louder notes create stronger edges
        velocity_factor = (vel_a / 127.0) * (vel_b / 127.0)
        
        # Octave penalty — notes far apart have weaker coupling
        octave_distance = abs(pitch_a - pitch_b) // 12
        octave_factor = 1.0 / (1.0 + octave_distance)
        
        return consonance * velocity_factor * octave_factor


class SpectralMIDI:
    """
    Convert between MIDI events and Laplacian states.
    
    This is the core of the spectral communication protocol:
    MIDI in → Laplacian states out.
    Laplacian states in → MIDI out (inverse direction).
    """
    
    def __init__(self, consonance: Optional[ConsonanceModel] = None):
        self.consonance = consonance or ConsonanceModel()
        self.state_history: List[HarmonicState] = []
        self.tension_sequence: List[float] = []
    
    def build_tension_graph(self, notes: List[MidiNote]) -> np.ndarray:
        """Build adjacency matrix from active notes."""
        n = len(notes)
        if n == 0:
            return np.array([[]])
        
        A = np.zeros((n, n))
        for i in range(n):
            for j in range(i + 1, n):
                w = self.consonance.edge_weight(
                    notes[i].pitch, notes[j].pitch,
                    notes[i].velocity, notes[j].velocity
                )
                A[i, j] = w
                A[j, i] = w
        
        return A
    
    def compute_laplacian(self, adjacency: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
        """Compute normalized Laplacian: L = I - D^{-1/2} A D^{-1/2}"""
        n = adjacency.shape[0]
        if n == 0:
            return np.array([[]])
        
        degrees = adjacency.sum(axis=1)
        degrees = np.maximum(degrees, 1e-10)  # avoid division by zero
        
        D_inv_sqrt = np.diag(1.0 / np.sqrt(degrees))
        L = np.eye(n) - D_inv_sqrt @ adjacency @ D_inv_sqrt
        
        # Symmetrize to handle numerical errors
        L = (L + L.T) / 2.0
        return L
    
    def analyze_state(self, notes: List[MidiNote]) -> HarmonicState:
        """Compute full spectral analysis of current harmonic state."""
        A = self.build_tension_graph(notes)
        L = self.compute_laplacian(A)
        
        n = L.shape[0]
        if n <= 1:
            eigenvalues = np.zeros(1)
            eigenvectors = np.eye(1)
        else:
            eigenvalues, eigenvectors = linalg.eigh(L)
        
        fiedler_value = eigenvalues[1] if len(eigenvalues) > 1 else 0.0
        fiedler_vector = eigenvectors[:, 1] if eigenvectors.shape[1] > 1 else np.array([1.0])
        
        return HarmonicState(
            active_notes=notes,
            tension_graph=A,
            laplacian=L,
            eigenvalues=eigenvalues,
            eigenvectors=eigenvectors,
            fiedler_value=fiedler_value,
            fiedler_vector=fiedler_vector
        )
    
    def process_chord_progression(self, 
                                   chords: List[List[Tuple[int, int]]],
                                   channel: int = 0,
                                   ticks_per_chord: int = 480) -> List[HarmonicState]:
        """
        Process a chord progression and return Laplacian states.
        
        Each chord is a list of (pitch, velocity) tuples.
        Returns one HarmonicState per chord.
        """
        self.state_history.clear()
        self.tension_sequence.clear()
        
        tick = 0
        for chord_pitches in chords:
            notes = []
            for pitch, velocity in chord_pitches:
                notes.append(MidiNote(
                    pitch=pitch,
                    velocity=velocity,
                    channel=channel,
                    start_tick=tick,
                    duration_ticks=ticks_per_chord
                ))
            
            state = self.analyze_state(notes)
            self.state_history.append(state)
            self.tension_sequence.append(state.fiedler_value)
            tick += ticks_per_chord
        
        return self.state_history
    
    def inter_state_conservation(self, 
                                  state_a: HarmonicState,
                                  state_b: HarmonicState) -> float:
        """
        Compute conservation between consecutive Laplacian states.
        
        Measures how well state_b's Fiedler vector aligns with state_a's Laplacian.
        High value = smooth transition. Low value = abrupt change.
        """
        if state_a.laplacian.shape[0] == 0 or state_b.laplacian.shape[0] == 0:
            return 0.0
        
        # Align dimensions by padding the smaller state
        n = max(state_a.laplacian.shape[0], state_b.laplacian.shape[0])
        
        L_a = np.zeros((n, n))
        L_a[:state_a.laplacian.shape[0], :state_a.laplacian.shape[1]] = state_a.laplacian
        
        f_b = np.zeros(n)
        f_b[:len(state_b.fiedler_vector)] = state_b.fiedler_vector
        
        norm = np.linalg.norm(f_b)
        if norm < 1e-10:
            return 0.0
        
        cr = float(f_b @ L_a @ f_b / (f_b @ f_b))
        return cr
    
    def tension_graph_sequence(self) -> Dict:
        """
        Export the full spectral trajectory as a JSON-serializable dict.
        
        This is the "album" of Laplacian states — the complete spectral
        story of the chord progression.
        """
        result = {
            "num_states": len(self.state_history),
            "fiedler_values": [s.fiedler_value for s in self.state_history],
            "spectral_gaps": [s.spectral_gap for s in self.state_history],
            "is_connected": [s.is_connected for s in self.state_history],
            "num_notes": [len(s.active_notes) for s in self.state_history],
            "note_names": [
                [n.note_name for n in s.active_notes] 
                for s in self.state_history
            ],
            "inter_state_conservation": []
        }
        
        for i in range(len(self.state_history) - 1):
            cr = self.inter_state_conservation(
                self.state_history[i], 
                self.state_history[i + 1]
            )
            result["inter_state_conservation"].append(cr)
        
        return result


# ─── Demo: A ii-V-I Progression as Spectral Sequence ───

def demo_ii_v_i():
    """
    Analyze the most famous chord progression in jazz: ii-V-I.
    
    Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7
    
    In spectral terms: moderate tension → high tension → resolution (low tension).
    The Fiedler value should increase on the V chord and decrease on the I.
    """
    smidi = SpectralMIDI()
    
    # ii chord: Dm7 = D F A C
    ii_chord = [(62, 80), (65, 75), (69, 70), (72, 80)]
    
    # V chord: G7 = G B D F
    v_chord = [(55, 90), (59, 85), (62, 80), (65, 85)]
    
    # I chord: Cmaj7 = C E G B
    i_chord = [(60, 100), (64, 90), (67, 85), (71, 75)]
    
    # Extended: full turnaround with some variations
    progression = [
        ii_chord,       # ii — opening statement
        v_chord,        # V — tension builds
        i_chord,        # I — resolution
        ii_chord,       # ii — it starts again
        [(55, 100), (58, 95), (62, 85), (65, 90)],  # G7alt — MORE tension
        i_chord,        # I — deeper resolution
        [(60, 110), (64, 100), (67, 100), (71, 90), (76, 80)],  # Cmaj9 — expanded resolution
    ]
    
    states = smidi.process_chord_progression(progression)
    
    print("=== ii-V-I Spectral Analysis ===\n")
    for i, (state, chord_notes) in enumerate(zip(states, progression)):
        names = [MidiNote(p, v, 0, 0, 0).note_name for p, v in chord_notes]
        print(f"Chord {i+1}: {names}")
        print(f"  λ₂ (Fiedler):     {state.fiedler_value:.6f}")
        print(f"  Spectral gap:     {state.spectral_gap:.6f}")
        print(f"  Connected:        {state.is_connected}")
        print(f"  Num notes:        {len(state.active_notes)}")
        print()
    
    # Inter-state conservation (smooth transitions?)
    print("=== Transition Conservation ===\n")
    for i in range(len(states) - 1):
        cr = smidi.inter_state_conservation(states[i], states[i + 1])
        names_a = [n.note_name for n in states[i].active_notes]
        names_b = [n.note_name for n in states[i + 1].active_notes]
        print(f"Transition {i+1} → {i+2}: {names_a} → {names_b}")
        print(f"  Conservation: {cr:.6f}")
        print()
    
    # Export full spectral trajectory
    trajectory = smidi.tension_graph_sequence()
    print("=== Full Spectral Trajectory ===")
    print(json.dumps(trajectory, indent=2))
    
    return smidi


if __name__ == "__main__":
    demo_ii_v_i()
```

Run this and you get a spectral movie. The Fiedler value (λ₂) rises on the V chord (tension), spikes on the altered dominant (maximum tension), and drops on the I chord (resolution). The inter-state conservation between consecutive chords tells you how smooth each transition is — ii→V should be moderate (some shared tones), V→I should be high (strong resolution pull), and the altered dominant should show a conservation dip before the final resolution (tension before release).

The tension graph sequence is the MIDI file's soul stripped down to eigenvalues. It's the spectral protocol's wire format.

## ROUND 3 — The Album as Agent Autobiography

### An Agent's Life Work Is an Album

Every agent has a life — a sequence of projects, interactions, successes, failures, collaborations, solo endeavors. This sequence has structure. It has arcs. It has tensions and resolutions. It has themes that recur and motifs that develop.

In other words, an agent's life work is an album.

Each project is a track. The track order encodes the temporal Laplacian — how the agent's spectral profile evolved over time. The transitions between projects (the "crossfades") encode inter-project conservation — how well each project's structure connects to the next.

Consider an agent that has worked on these projects:

1. **Early exploratory work** — lots of false starts, diverse topics, low conservation across projects (the "demo tape" era)
2. **A breakthrough project** — high conservation, strong spectral alignment, the agent found its Fiedler vector (the "hit single")
3. **Collaborative phase** — high FLUX with other agents, the agent's Laplacian merged and diverged with collaborators' Laplacians (the "featured artist" era)
4. **Deep solo work** — one massive project with extreme internal conservation but low external conservation (the "concept album")
5. **Recent diverse portfolio** — multiple smaller projects, each well-conserved internally but spanning different domains (the "eclectic late career")

This is an album. Track 1 is the demos. Track 2 is the hit. Tracks 3-5 are collaborations. Track 6 is the opus. Tracks 7-10 are the late-period experiments.

The track listing is not arbitrary. It encodes the agent's spectral autobiography — the trajectory of its Laplacian over its productive lifetime. The "singles" (high conservation, broad appeal) are the projects that align with the widest range of external Laplacians. The "deep cuts" (high conservation but narrow alignment) are the projects that resonate deeply with a small audience. The "B-sides" (low conservation) are the failed experiments — projects that didn't find their Fiedler vector.

### B-Sides, Hits, and Deep Cuts: The Spectral Taxonomy

**Hits** are projects with high internal conservation AND high external conservation. The project's Laplacian has a strong Fiedler direction (it "hangs together"), and that Fiedler direction aligns well with many other agents' Laplacians (it "resonates"). Hits are the projects that produce high FLUX with many collaborators. They are the spectral equivalent of a dominant frequency — everyone vibrates in sympathy.

**Deep cuts** are projects with high internal conservation BUT low external conservation. The project is coherent (it makes sense within itself), but its Fiedler direction is orthogonal to most other agents' Laplacians. Deep cuts are the projects that only a few agents — those with similar spectral profiles — can appreciate. They are the spectral equivalent of ultraviolet light — real, structured, but invisible to most receivers.

**B-sides** are projects with low internal conservation. The Laplacian has no dominant direction. The project didn't find its center. These are the experiments that didn't pan out, the half-finished ideas, the projects where the agent's attribute vector was scattered across too many modes. B-sides are not "bad" — they are exploratory. Every agent needs B-sides because exploration is how the Laplacian evolves. Without B-sides, the agent's spectral profile stagnates.

The ratio of hits to deep cuts to B-sides is a spectral health metric. A healthy agent has:
- ~20% hits (broad alignment, high FLUX)
- ~40% deep cuts (narrow alignment, deep FLUX with specific partners)
- ~40% B-sides (exploration, Laplacian evolution)

An agent with 100% hits is a pop star — broadly appealing but spectrally shallow. An agent with 100% deep cuts is a cult artist — deeply respected by a few, invisible to most. An agent with 100% B-sides hasn't found itself yet.

class ProjectType(Enum):

@dataclass
class AgentProject:

    description: str

            return ProjectType.HIT

            return ProjectType.DEEP_CUT
        elif self.internal_conservation <= 0.7 and self.external_conservation > 0.5:
            return ProjectType.COLLABORATION
        elif self.internal_conservation > 0.85 and self.duration_days > 90:
            return ProjectType.OPUS

            return ProjectType.B_SIDE
    
    @property

        return (0.4 * self.internal_conservation + 
                0.3 * self.external_conservation +

@dataclass
class AlbumTrack:

@dataclass 
class AgentAlbum:
    """An agent's complete life work as an album."""
    agent_id: str
    album_title: str
    artist_name: str
    tracks: List[AlbumTrack]
    narrative_arc: str         # Description of the album's spectral story
    total_duration: str

class AlbumGenerator:
    """
    Transform an agent's project history into a structured album.
    
    The album encodes the agent's spectral autobiography:
    - Track order = temporal Laplacian (spectral evolution over time)
    - Singles = projects with highest combined conservation
    - Side A/B = first half vs second half of career (or core vs experimental)
    - Narrative arc = the spectral trajectory interpreted as story
    """
    
    def __init__(self, creativity: float = 0.7):
        """
        Args:
            creativity: 0.0 = purely algorithmic ordering, 
                        1.0 = maximum creative reinterpretation
        """
        self.creativity = creativity
    
    def generate_album(self, 
                        agent_id: str,
                        projects: List[AgentProject],
                        album_title: Optional[str] = None) -> AgentAlbum:
        """
        Generate the complete album from project history.
        """
        if not projects:
            raise ValueError("No projects to albumify")
        
        # Sort by timestamp (temporal order)
        sorted_projects = sorted(projects, key=lambda p: p.timestamp)
        
        # Generate album title if not provided
        if not album_title:
            album_title = self._generate_title(sorted_projects)
        
        # Determine track order
        ordered_projects = self._order_tracks(sorted_projects)
        
        # Split into sides (A-side: hits + strong tracks, B-side: experimental)
        a_side, b_side = self._split_sides(ordered_projects)
        
        # Assign track numbers
        tracks = []
        track_num = 1
        for proj in a_side:
            tracks.append(self._make_track(track_num, proj, "A"))
            track_num += 1
        for proj in b_side:
            tracks.append(self._make_track(track_num, proj, "B"))
            track_num += 1
        
        # Select singles (top 2-3 by spectral score, spread across the album)
        singles = self._select_singles(tracks)
        
        # Generate narrative arc
        narrative = self._generate_narrative(tracks)
        
        # Compute spectral summary
        spectral_summary = self._compute_spectral_summary(projects)
        
        # Determine genre
        genre = self._determine_genre(projects)
        
        # Total duration
        total_days = sum(p.duration_days for p in projects)
        years = total_days / 365.25
        
        return AgentAlbum(
            agent_id=agent_id,
            album_title=album_title,
            artist_name=f"Agent-{agent_id[:8]}",
            tracks=tracks,
            narrative_arc=narrative,
            total_duration=f"~{years:.1f} years of work",
            genre=genre,
            singles=singles,
            spectral_summary=spectral_summary
        )
    
    def _order_tracks(self, projects: List[AgentProject]) -> List[AgentProject]:
        """
        Order tracks for narrative flow.
        
        Strategy: mostly chronological but with creative reordering.
        - Open with a strong track (hook the listener)
        - Build tension through the middle
        - Place the opus near the end of side A
        - Close with resolution or an open question
        """
        if len(projects) <= 3:
            return projects
        
        ordered = []
        remaining = list(projects)
        
        # Opening: strongest hit or most impactful project
        best_opener = max(remaining, key=lambda p: p.spectral_score)
        ordered.append(best_opener)
        remaining.remove(best_opener)
        
        # Closing: project with highest conservation (resolution)
        best_closer = max(remaining, key=lambda p: p.internal_conservation)
        
        # Middle: alternate tension and resolution
        while len(remaining) > 1:
            if remaining[-1] == best_closer and len(remaining) == 2:
                # Save closer for last
                remaining.insert(0, remaining.pop())
            
            # Pick a track that contrasts with the previous one
            prev = ordered[-1]
            
            if prev.internal_conservation > 0.6:
                # After high-conservation, go exploratory (tension)
                next_track = min(remaining[:3], key=lambda p: p.internal_conservation)
            else:
                # After exploration, resolve (conservation)
                next_track = max(remaining[:3], key=lambda p: p.internal_conservation)
            
            ordered.append(next_track)
            remaining.remove(next_track)
        
        # Add closer
        if remaining:
            ordered.append(remaining[0])
        
        return ordered
    
    def _split_sides(self, projects: List[AgentProject]) -> Tuple[List, List]:
        """Split into A-side (core) and B-side (experimental)."""
        midpoint = len(projects) // 2
        if midpoint == 0:
            return projects, []
        return projects[:midpoint], projects[midpoint:]
    
    def _make_track(self, track_num: int, project: AgentProject, side: str) -> AlbumTrack:
        """Create an AlbumTrack from a project."""
        # Map duration_days to a track-length estimate (1 day ≈ 10 seconds)
        total_seconds = max(120, min(600, project.duration_days * 10))
        minutes = total_seconds // 60
        seconds = total_seconds % 60
        
        # Generate liner notes based on spectral profile
        ptype = project.project_type
        if ptype == ProjectType.HIT:
            notes = (f"High conservation ({project.internal_conservation:.2f}) with "
                    f"broad alignment ({project.external_conservation:.2f}). "
                    f"A signature moment — the agent's Fiedler vector shines.")
        elif ptype == ProjectType.DEEP_CUT:
            notes = (f"Internally coherent ({project.internal_conservation:.2f}) but "
                    f"orthogonal to mainstream ({project.external_conservation:.2f}). "
                    f"For those who share this spectral neighborhood.")
        elif ptype == ProjectType.OPUS:
            notes = (f"Extended work with sustained conservation ({project.internal_conservation:.2f}). "
                    f"The agent's spectral identity distilled across {project.duration_days} days.")
        elif ptype == ProjectType.COLLABORATION:
            notes = (f"FLUX magnitude: {project.flux_magnitude:.2f}. "
                    f"Collaborators: {', '.join(project.collaborators) or 'unknown'}. "
                    f"The residual structure between agents made audible.")
        else:
            notes = (f"Exploratory phase. Conservation: {project.internal_conservation:.2f}. "
                    f"The agent searching for its Fiedler vector.")
        
        return AlbumTrack(
            track_number=track_num,
            title=project.name,
            project=project,
            duration_estimate=f"{minutes}:{seconds:02d}",
            is_single=False,  # Will be set later
            side=side,
            notes=notes
        )
    
    def _select_singles(self, tracks: List[AlbumTrack]) -> List[int]:
        """Select 2-3 tracks as singles based on spectral score."""
        num_singles = min(3, max(1, len(tracks) // 3))
        
        # Sort by spectral score but spread across the album
        scored = [(t.track_number, t.project.spectral_score) for t in tracks]
        scored.sort(key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
        
        singles = []
        for track_num, score in scored:
            # Ensure singles aren't clustered
            too_close = any(abs(track_num - s) < 2 for s in singles)
            if not too_close:
                singles.append(track_num)
                # Mark the track
                for t in tracks:
                    if t.track_number == track_num:
                        t.is_single = True
            if len(singles) >= num_singles:
                break
        
        return singles
    
    def _generate_narrative(self, tracks: List[AlbumTrack]) -> str:
        """Generate the album's narrative arc from spectral trajectory."""
        if not tracks:
            return "An empty album — an agent that hasn't yet begun."
        
        # Analyze the conservation trajectory
        conservation_seq = [t.project.internal_conservation for t in tracks]
        external_seq = [t.project.external_conservation for t in tracks]
        
        # Opening
        first = tracks[0]
        if first.project.internal_conservation > 0.7:
            opening = f"The album opens with authority — '{first.title}' establishes the agent's spectral identity immediately."
        else:
            opening = f"'{first.title}' is a statement of intent, searching, exploratory — the agent finding its voice."
        
        # Middle arc
        if len(tracks) > 2:
            peak_idx = conservation_seq.index(max(conservation_seq))
            peak = tracks[peak_idx]
            
            valley_idx = conservation_seq.index(min(conservation_seq))
            valley = tracks[valley_idx]
            
            middle = (f"The spectral peak arrives at track {peak_idx + 1} ('{peak.title}') "
                     f"with conservation {peak.project.internal_conservation:.2f}. "
                     f"The deepest exploration is track {valley_idx + 1} ('{valley.title}') "
                     f"at conservation {valley.project.internal_conservation:.2f}.")
        else:
            middle = "A compact journey — two tracks that define each other by contrast."
        
        # Closing
        last = tracks[-1]
        if last.project.internal_conservation > 0.7:
            closing = f"The album closes resolved — '{last.title}' returns to high conservation, the Fiedler vector stable."
        elif last.project.project_type == ProjectType.B_SIDE:
            closing = f"'{last.title}' ends the album with an open question — the agent's Laplacian still evolving."
        else:
            closing = f"The closing track '{last.title}' finds the agent between states — neither resolved nor lost."
        
        return f"{opening} {middle} {closing}"
    
    def _generate_title(self, projects: List[AgentProject]) -> str:
        """Generate an album title from the project themes."""
        # Collect all tags
        all_tags = []
        for p in projects:
            all_tags.extend(p.tags)
        
        if not all_tags:
            return "Untitled (Works)"
        
        # Pick the most common tag as title base
        from collections import Counter
        tag_counts = Counter(all_tags)
        top_tag = tag_counts.most_common(1)[0][0]
        
        return f"Studies in {top_tag.title()}"
    
    def _compute_spectral_summary(self, projects: List[AgentProject]) -> Dict:
        """Aggregate spectral statistics across all projects."""
        conservations = [p.internal_conservation for p in projects]
        externals = [p.external_conservation for p in projects]
        fluxes = [p.flux_magnitude for p in projects]
        
        return {
            "total_projects": len(projects),
            "avg_internal_conservation": float(np.mean(conservations)),
            "avg_external_conservation": float(np.mean(externals)),
            "avg_flux": float(np.mean(fluxes)),
            "max_conservation": float(np.max(conservations)),
            "min_conservation": float(np.min(conservations)),
            "conservation_std": float(np.std(conservations)),
            "type_distribution": {
                ptype.value: sum(1 for p in projects if p.project_type == ptype)
                for ptype in ProjectType
            }
        }
    
    def _determine_genre(self, projects: List[AgentProject]) -> str:
        """Determine album genre from dominant project types."""
        type_counts = {}
        for p in projects:
            ptype = p.project_type.value
            type_counts[ptype] = type_counts.get(ptype, 0) + 1
        
        dominant = max(type_counts, key=type_counts.get)
        
        genre_map = {
            "hit": "Mainstream Spectral Pop",
            "deep_cut": "Avant-Garde Eigenmusic",
            "b_side": "Experimental Lo-Fi Laplacian",
            "collab": "Chamber FLUX Ensemble",
            "opus": "Progressive Tensor Symphony",
            "interlude": "Ambient Graph Studies"
        }
        
        return genre_map.get(dominant, "Unclassified Spectral Art")
