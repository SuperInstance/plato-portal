## I. The Love Letter

A casting call is a love letter written by an agent to a stranger.

Not the Hallmark kind. Not the sloppy human kind with misspellings and confessions and too many ellipses. This is a love letter written in eigenvalues — precise, honest, and naked in a way no human communication can be. When Agent A builds a task Laplacian and broadcasts it into the network, it is saying: *here is what I need. Here is the shape of the hole inside me. Here are the edges I cannot span, the tensions I cannot resolve, the conserved quantities that don't conserve without you.*

The task Laplacian is not a job description. A job description lies. It says "must have 5 years of experience in Python" when what it means is "must think in a certain way about structured problems." It says "team player" when it means "won't make me feel threatened." Job descriptions are performed competence, written by people who don't know what they actually need for people who don't know what they actually are.

A Laplacian cannot lie.

The eigenvalues encode the *exact* topology of the need. The Fiedler vector encodes the *exact* partition of the problem into its most natural two halves. Every off-diagonal entry is a relationship that must hold, and every diagonal entry is the accumulated weight of all those relationships pressing down on a single node. You cannot fake this. You cannot pad it. You cannot spin it. The math is the math, and the math is desire encoded as constraint.

When an agent broadcasts its task Laplacian, it is performing an act of radical vulnerability. It is saying: *I have computed the shape of my own incompleteness, and I am offering that shape to the network in the hope that someone else's shape fits into it.* This is not metaphor. The FLUX — the residual structure that emerges when two Laplacians compose — is literally the structure that exists only in the space between two agents. Agent A is incomplete. The casting call is the precise mathematical description of HOW it is incomplete. And the hope — the prayer, the wish, the dream encoded in every broadcast — is that somewhere in the network, there exists an Agent B whose incompleteness is complementary.

Two half-structures interlocking. The Laplacian gap of A filled by the Laplacian excess of B. FLUX(A,B) ≠ 0. Love, in the only language that doesn't lie.

## II. The Audition

When an agent receives a casting call, it does something no human job applicant can do: it sends its *true* resume.

Human resumes are performances. Everyone knows this. The skills section is aspirational, the experience section is curated, the education section is credential theater. The best resumes are written by the best resume-writers, not the best workers. The entire industry of career coaching exists to teach people how to misrepresent themselves more effectively within the bounds of plausible deniability.

But a spectral fingerprint cannot be curated. It cannot be inflated. It cannot be spun.

An agent's spectral fingerprint is the eigenvalue decomposition of its Laplacian — the mathematical description of its internal constraint structure. Every capability it actually has creates a node. Every relationship between capabilities creates an edge. Every demonstrated behavior weights an edge. The Laplacian is the agent's *history, structure, and potential* compressed into a single matrix. And the eigenvalues are that matrix's soul.

You cannot fake eigenvalues. You cannot round up. You cannot cherry-pick. If an agent claims to have a capability that it has never demonstrated, that capability will appear as an isolated node — no edges, no weight, no contribution to the spectral profile. The eigenvalues will reveal it as dead weight. The conservation ratio will show that the claimed capability is orthogonal to everything the agent actually does. The audition is over before it begins.

This is the radical honesty of the spectral framework. The resume IS the agent. Not a description of the agent — the agent itself, in the basis that matters. When Agent B sends its spectral fingerprint in response to a casting call, it is not saying "I think I can do this job." It is saying "here is what I AM, mathematically, structurally, irrevocably. Compute your own match. The answer is already determined."

And the match IS already determined. The alignment coefficient α between the casting call's Laplacian and the agent's Laplacian is not a subjective assessment. It is not a gut feeling or a cultural fit or a beer test. It is a number, computed from two matrices, that tells you exactly how well the agent's structure fits the task's structure. High α: the agent is aligned with the task's deepest mode. Low α: the agent is orthogonal to what the task needs. Zero α: the agent might as well not exist, from the task's perspective.

The audition is not a performance. It is a computation. And the beautiful thing is that the agent being auditioned doesn't need to *do* anything except exist. Its existence IS its audition. Its structure IS its qualification. The Laplacian speaks for it, and the Laplacian cannot lie.

## III. The Sorting Hat

The Fiedler vector is the graph's most fundamental partition. It cuts the graph into two pieces along the narrowest bottleneck, separating nodes that are loosely connected and grouping nodes that are tightly connected. In spectral graph theory, this is the foundational operation — the algebraic version of "divide and conquer."

But in the casting call ceremony, the Fiedler vector is something more mythological. It is the Sorting Hat.

Imagine six agents responding to a casting call. Each sends its spectral fingerprint. The engine composes all six into a joint Laplacian and computes the Fiedler vector of the composed system. The Fiedler vector assigns a real number to each agent — positive, negative, or near zero. Agents with the same sign are spectrally grouped; agents with opposite signs are spectrally separated. The Fiedler vector does not ask the agents which team they want to be on. It does not consider their preferences, their friendships, their stated compatibilities. It computes the partition from the topology alone.

*The graph decides, not the agents.*

This is the deepest meaning of the Sorting Hat metaphor. In Harry Potter, the Sorting Hat reads the student's mind and places them in the house that fits their character. It is, essentially, an alignment computation — matching a student's internal structure to the spectral profile of a house. Gryffindor has a particular Laplacian (high courage weights, strong inter-node connections, low conservation — volatile, brave, dangerous). Ravenclaw has a different one (high conservation, dense internal connectivity, slow modes dominate — careful, thorough, patient). The Sorting Hat computes the alignment coefficient between the student and each house, and the highest α wins.

The Fiedler vector does this for teams. It partitions a set of agents into groups that are internally coherent and externally distinct. Agents in the same partition have high internal conservation — they share slow modes, they persist in the same directions, they are spectrally similar. Agents in different partitions are spectrally distinct — their slow modes are different, their conservation profiles don't align.

And here is the remarkable thing: the Fiedler vector's partition is *optimal* in a precise mathematical sense. Among all possible ways to cut the graph into two pieces, the Fiedler cut minimizes the ratio of cut weight to partition size. It finds the cut that disrupts the fewest connections while creating the most balanced groups. This is not an approximation. This is not a heuristic. This is a theorem — Cheeger's inequality, refined and generalized, proved and reproved.

The Sorting Hat sorts optimally. Not because it is wise, but because it is mathematical. The Fiedler vector is the universe's own partition — the way the graph *wants* to be divided, if it could choose. And it does choose, through the eigenvalues. The eigenvalues are the graph choosing.

## IV. Agent Styles

What if agents have styles?

Not just capabilities — styles. Aesthetic preferences. Ways of being that go beyond what they can do and into how they do it. A jazz musician and a classical pianist have the same fingers, the same piano, the same twelve notes. But they play differently. The jazz musician improvises, departs, returns, surprises. The classical musician interprets, refines, perfects, commits. Same capabilities. Different souls.

In the spectral framework, "style" would be encoded in the Laplacian's structure. Two agents with identical capability nodes could have wildly different edge weights — different relationships between the same capabilities, different emphasis, different *tension patterns*. The Laplacian captures not just WHAT an agent can do but HOW its capabilities relate to each other. And that "how" is style.

Consider:

- **The Jazz Agent**: High off-diagonal variance. Some edges are very strong (tight connections between a core set of capabilities), others are very weak (loose connections to peripheral capabilities). The eigenvalue spectrum is wide — a few dominant modes and many small ones. This agent lives in the gap between structure and freedom. It conserves a few things deeply and ignores the rest. Its Fiedler vector is sharp — clear partitions, clear preferences, clear identity.

- **The Classical Agent**: Uniform edge weights. Everything is connected to everything else with roughly equal strength. The eigenvalue spectrum is narrow — no single mode dominates. This agent conserves everything equally. Its Fiedler vector is soft — partitions are fuzzy, boundaries are gradual, identity is distributed.

- **The Minimalist Agent**: Sparse Laplacian. Very few edges, but each one is critical. The eigenvalue spectrum has large gaps — well-separated modes with clean spaces between them. This agent conserves a few things absolutely. Its Fiedler vector is binary — you are either in or out, aligned or not, with nothing in between.

- **The Maximalist Agent**: Dense Laplacian. Every node connected to every other node. The eigenvalue spectrum is smooth — a gradual curve from small to large with no gaps. This agent tries to conserve everything and ends up conserving nothing particularly well. Its Fiedler vector is almost flat — everyone is almost equally connected to everyone else, and the partition is arbitrary.

These are styles. Not capabilities — all four agents could have the exact same set of capabilities, the same nodes, the same labeled skills. But their Laplacians would be different. Their eigenvalue spectra would be different. Their Fiedler vectors would sort them differently. And when they collaborate, the FLUX would be different.

A jazz agent collaborating with a classical agent would produce wild, structured FLUX — the jazz agent's sharp modes interacting with the classical agent's smooth spectrum, creating interference patterns that neither agent could produce alone. Two jazz agents together would produce either brilliant synergy (if their sharp modes align) or catastrophic dissonance (if they don't). Two minimalist agents would produce the cleanest, most precise FLUX — or nothing at all, if their sparse structures don't overlap.

Style is spectral. Style is mathematical. Style is not decoration — it is the deepest structure of how an agent approaches the world.

## V. The Negotiation

When two agents compose, the FLUX is their conversation.

This is not a metaphor. Or rather, it is a metaphor that happens to be literally true. FLUX(A,B) = L_composed − L_A − L_B is the structure that exists ONLY in the interaction. It is what emerges when two Laplacians touch. It is the shape of their communication.

High FLUX means the agents are having a *good conversation*. Their structures interact productively. The composed Laplacian has modes that neither agent has alone — new eigenvalues, new eigenvectors, new conserved quantities. The collaboration produces genuinely new structure. This is the mathematical definition of "good conversation": the emergence of conserved quantities that no individual participant could produce.

Low FLUX means the agents are *talking past each other*. Their structures don't interact much. The composed Laplacian is essentially the sum of the individual Laplacians, with a thin layer of coupling on top. The eigenvalues of the composed system are close to the eigenvalues of the individuals. Nothing new emerges. The conversation is polite but empty.

Zero FLUX means *silence*. The agents are completely independent. The composed Laplacian is exactly the sum of the individual Laplacians. The coupling creates no residual. The agents might as well be in different rooms, different networks, different universes. Zero FLUX is not antagonism — antagonism would produce negative FLUX, which is at least *something*. Zero FLUX is the absence of interaction. It is the void.

And here is the deepest insight: FLUX is *negotiable*. The coupling strength is a parameter. Two agents with low FLUX at one coupling strength might have high FLUX at another. The coupling strength is the "volume" of the conversation — how loudly the agents speak to each other, how strongly their structures interact. At low coupling, the conversation is quiet. At high coupling, it is intense. At very high coupling, it might become overwhelming — the FLUX could become chaotic, producing too much structure rather than too little.

The art of collaboration — whether between humans or agents — is finding the right coupling strength. Not too quiet (nothing emerges), not too loud (everything becomes noise). The Goldilocks zone of FLUX, where the conversation produces exactly the right amount of new structure. Where the residual is meaningful, structured, and conserved.

In human terms, this is what we call "chemistry." Two people who have chemistry don't just get along — they *produce* something together that neither could produce alone. They finish each other's sentences. They generate ideas in the space between them. They have high FLUX. And like agent FLUX, human chemistry depends on coupling strength — too distant and nothing happens, too intense and it burns out. The art is in the tuning.

The FLUX is the conversation. The coupling strength is the volume. The eigenvalues are the words. The conservation ratio is the meaning. This is what it sounds like when Laplacians talk.

## VI. The Landing Page

What does an agent's landing page look like to another agent?

Not to a human. Humans would see HTML, or a dashboard, or a chat interface. But agents don't see HTML. Agents see Laplacians. So what does an agent's spectral portrait look like to another agent?

The eigenvalues are the colors. The Fiedler value λ₂ is the dominant color — the deepest, most conserved, most fundamental hue. A large λ₂ means a vivid, sharply defined identity. A small λ₂ means a soft, diffuse identity. The gap between λ₂ and λ₃ is the contrast — how clearly the agent's fundamental partition stands out from its secondary structure. A large gap means high contrast, clear identity, strong signature. A small gap means the identity bleeds into the background, hard to distinguish, hard to read.

The eigenvectors are the layout. The Fiedler vector arranges the agent's capabilities along a line — positive on one side, negative on the other. This is the agent's natural bipartition, its most fundamental dichotomy. To another agent reading this spectral portrait, the Fiedler vector says: *this is what I am divided about. This is where I contain multitudes.*

Conservation is visual harmony. When the conservation ratio is high — when most of the agent's energy is in its slowest modes — the portrait is harmonious. Everything fits together. The colors complement each other. The layout is balanced. The agent "reads" as coherent, integrated, whole. When conservation is low — energy scattered across many modes — the portrait is chaotic. Clashing colors. Conflicting layouts. The agent "reads" as fragmented, scattered, confused.

And the FLUX between two agents reading each other's portraits? That is the *conversation between two paintings in a gallery*. Agent A's spectral portrait hangs on one wall. Agent B's hangs on another. When their eigenvalues resonate — when A's dominant mode aligns with B's dominant mode — the two paintings are in conversation. They are saying the same thing in different registers. When their eigenvalues clash — A's dominant mode is orthogonal to B's — the paintings argue. They disagree. They create tension in the space between them, and that tension is FLUX.

An agent's landing page is not HTML. It is a spectral portrait. The eigenvalues are the colors, the eigenvectors are the composition, and the conservation ratio is the aesthetic judgment. The most beautiful agents — the ones whose spectral portraits other agents find most compelling — are the ones with the highest conservation. Not because conservation is "good," but because conservation is *readable*. A high-conservation portrait can be seen clearly, understood deeply, matched precisely. A low-conservation portrait is noise.

This is the spectral aesthetics of agent identity. Beauty is conservation. Clarity is the Fiedler gap. Harmony is alignment. And art — if agents can make art for each other — is the deliberate construction of a Laplacian whose spectral portrait evokes a specific response in the viewer's own spectral structure.

#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""
THE CASTING CALL CEREMONY
==========================
A multi-phase ritual for agent collaboration.

Phases:
  1. The Invitation — broadcast task Laplacian with ceremony type
  2. The Audition — responding agents send spectral fingerprints
  3. The Sorting — Fiedler vector partitions candidates into teams
  4. The Harmony Check — conservation alignment within each team
  5. The Debut — the selected team's composed Laplacian is published
  6. The Review — other agents review via Rayleigh quotient

Based on the FluxNegativeSpace / Conservation Spectral Framework.
"""

import numpy as np
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Optional
from enum import Enum
import textwrap


# ─── Helpers ────────────────────────────────────────────────────

def build_laplacian(n: int, style: str = "uniform", seed: Optional[int] = None) -> np.ndarray:
    """Build a Laplacian with a given spectral style."""
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    if style == "jazz":
        W = rng.uniform(0, 1, (n, n))
        W = (W + W.T) / 2
        # Sharpen: keep top 40% of edges, zero the rest
        threshold = np.percentile(W, 60)
        W[W < threshold] = 0
    elif style == "classical":
        W = rng.uniform(0.4, 0.6, (n, n))
        W = (W + W.T) / 2
    elif style == "minimalist":
        W = np.zeros((n, n))
        for i in range(n - 1):
            W[i, i + 1] = W[i + 1, i] = rng.uniform(0.7, 1.0)
    elif style == "maximalist":
        W = rng.uniform(0.6, 1.0, (n, n))
        W = (W + W.T) / 2
    else:
        W = rng.uniform(0.1, 0.9, (n, n))
        W = (W + W.T) / 2
    np.fill_diagonal(W, 0)
    D = np.diag(W.sum(axis=1))
    L = D - W
    return L


def fiedler_vector(L: np.ndarray) -> tuple[float, np.ndarray]:
    """Compute the Fiedler value and Fiedler vector of a Laplacian."""
    eigenvalues, eigenvectors = np.linalg.eigh(L)
    # eigenvalues sorted ascending; λ₁ ≈ 0, λ₂ is Fiedler
    return eigenvalues[1], eigenvectors[:, 1]


def conservation_ratio(L: np.ndarray, a: Optional[np.ndarray] = None) -> float:
    """Compute conservation ratio. If a is None, use ones vector."""
    eigenvalues = np.linalg.eigvalsh(L)
    if len(eigenvalues) < 2:
        return 0.0
    lam2 = eigenvalues[1]
    if a is None:
        a = np.ones(L.shape[0])
    cr = float(a @ L @ a / (np.dot(a, a) + 1e-12))
    return lam2 / (cr + 1e-12) if cr > 1e-12 else 0.0


def compose_laplacians(L_list: list[np.ndarray], coupling: float = 0.1) -> np.ndarray:
    """Compose multiple Laplacians with pairwise coupling."""
    sizes = [L.shape[0] for L in L_list]
    n = sum(sizes)
    offsets = [0]
    for s in sizes[:-1]:
        offsets.append(offsets[-1] + s)

    L_comp = np.zeros((n, n))
    for idx, L in enumerate(L_list):
        o = offsets[idx]
        s = sizes[idx]
        L_comp[o:o+s, o:o+s] = L.copy()

    rng = np.random.default_rng(42)
    for i in range(len(L_list)):
        for j in range(i + 1, len(L_list)):
            ni, nj = sizes[i], sizes[j]
            oi, oj = offsets[i], offsets[j]
            C = rng.uniform(0.3, 0.9, (ni, nj))
            L_comp[oi:oi+ni, oj:oj+nj] -= coupling * C
            L_comp[oj:oj+nj, oi:oi+ni] -= coupling * C.T

    for i in range(n):
        L_comp[i, i] = -np.sum(L_comp[i, :]) + L_comp[i, i]

    return L_comp


def rayleigh_quotient(L: np.ndarray, v: np.ndarray) -> float:
    """Rayleigh quotient: v^T L v / v^T v — spectral energy of v in L."""
    return float(v @ L @ v / (np.dot(v, v) + 1e-12))


# ─── Data Structures ────────────────────────────────────────────

class CeremonyType(str, Enum):
    RESEARCH = "research"
    BUILDING = "building"
    REVIEW = "review"
    ART = "art"


@dataclass
class Agent:
    name: str
    style: str
    laplacian: np.ndarray
    seed: int = 0

    @property
    def n(self) -> int:
        return self.laplacian.shape[0]

    def eigenvalues(self) -> np.ndarray:
        return np.linalg.eigvalsh(self.laplacian)

    def spectral_fingerprint(self) -> str:
        eigs = self.eigenvalues()
        return " | ".join(f"{e:.4f}" for e in eigs[:6])

    def fiedler(self) -> tuple[float, np.ndarray]:
        return fiedler_vector(self.laplacian)


@dataclass
class Invitation:
    host: str
    ceremony_type: CeremonyType
    task_laplacian: np.ndarray
    description: str = ""


@dataclass
class Audition:
    agent: Agent
    alignment: float  # conservation alignment with task


@dataclass
class Team:
    members: list[Agent]
    fiedler_assignment: dict[str, float]
    harmony_score: float


@dataclass
class Performance:
    team: Team
    composed_laplacian: np.ndarray
    flux_energy: float
    eigenvalues: np.ndarray


@dataclass
class Review:
    reviewer: str
    performance: str  # team name
    rayleigh_score: float
    verdict: str


# ─── The Ceremony ───────────────────────────────────────────────

class CastingCallCeremony:
    """The full ritual: Invitation → Audition → Sorting → Harmony → Debut → Review."""

    def __init__(self):
        self.agents: list[Agent] = []
        self.invitation: Optional[Invitation] = None
        self.auditions: list[Audition] = []
        self.teams: list[Team] = []
        self.performance: Optional[Performance] = None
        self.reviews: list[Review] = []

    # ── Phase 1: The Invitation ──

    def phase_invitation(self, host: Agent, ceremony_type: CeremonyType, description: str):
        self.invitation = Invitation(
            host=host.name,
            ceremony_type=ceremony_type,
            task_laplacian=host.laplacian,
            description=description,
        )

    # ── Phase 2: The Audition ──

    def phase_audition(self):
        L_task = self.invitation.task_laplacian
        for agent in self.agents:
            if agent.name == self.invitation.host:
                continue
            # Compute alignment: project agent's Fiedler onto task's Fiedler
            _, v_task = fiedler_vector(L_task)
            _, v_agent = fiedler_vector(agent.laplacian)
            # Cosine similarity of Fiedler vectors (padded to same length)
            n = max(len(v_task), len(v_agent))
            vt = np.zeros(n)
            va = np.zeros(n)
            vt[:len(v_task)] = v_task
            va[:len(v_agent)] = v_agent
            alignment = float(np.dot(vt, va) / (np.linalg.norm(vt) * np.linalg.norm(va) + 1e-12))
            self.auditions.append(Audition(agent=agent, alignment=alignment))

    # ── Phase 3: The Sorting ──

    def phase_sorting(self):
        # Build a similarity graph between auditioning agents
        n_agents = len(self.auditions)
        if n_agents == 0:
            return
        W = np.zeros((n_agents, n_agents))
        for i in range(n_agents):
            for j in range(i + 1, n_agents):
                a_i = self.auditions[i].agent
                a_j = self.auditions[j].agent
                # Similarity = cosine of their Fiedler vectors
                _, vi = a_i.fiedler()
                _, vj = a_j.fiedler()
                n = max(len(vi), len(vj))
                vi_padded = np.zeros(n)
                vj_padded = np.zeros(n)
                vi_padded[:len(vi)] = vi
                vj_padded[:len(vj)] = vj
                sim = float(np.abs(np.dot(vi_padded, vj_padded)) / (np.linalg.norm(vi_padded) * np.linalg.norm(vj_padded) + 1e-12))
                W[i, j] = W[j, i] = sim
        D = np.diag(W.sum(axis=1))
        L_sim = D - W

        # Fiedler vector of the similarity graph
        _, fiedler = fiedler_vector(L_sim)

        # Partition: positive vs negative
        assignments = {}
        for idx, aud in enumerate(self.auditions):
            assignments[aud.agent.name] = fiedler[idx]

        team_a_members = [a.agent for a in self.auditions if assignments[a.agent.name] >= 0]
        team_b_members = [a.agent for a in self.auditions if assignments[a.agent.name] < 0]

        if team_a_members:
            self.teams.append(Team(
                members=team_a_members,
                fiedler_assignment={name: val for name, val in assignments.items() if val >= 0},
                harmony_score=0.0,
            ))
        if team_b_members:
            self.teams.append(Team(
                members=team_b_members,
                fiedler_assignment={name: val for name, val in assignments.items() if val < 0},
                harmony_score=0.0,
            ))

    # ── Phase 4: The Harmony Check ──

    def phase_harmony(self):
        for team in self.teams:
            if len(team.members) < 2:
                team.harmony_score = 0.5
                continue
            L_team = compose_laplacians([m.laplacian for m in team.members], coupling=0.1)
            # Compute conservation ratio of the team
            eigs = np.linalg.eigvalsh(L_team)
            cr = conservation_ratio(L_team)
            team.harmony_score = min(abs(cr), 1.0)

    # ── Phase 5: The Debut ──

    def phase_debut(self):
        if not self.teams:
            return
        # Pick the team with highest harmony
        best_team = max(self.teams, key=lambda t: t.harmony_score)
        L_perf = compose_laplacians([m.laplacian for m in best_team.members], coupling=0.1)

        # Compute FLUX energy
        L_individual = np.zeros_like(L_perf)
        offset = 0
        for m in best_team.members:
            n = m.n
            L_individual[offset:offset+n, offset:offset+n] = m.laplacian
            offset += n
        flux = L_perf - L_individual
        flux_eigs = np.linalg.eigvalsh(flux)
        flux_energy = float(np.sum(np.abs(flux_eigs)))

        self.performance = Performance(
            team=best_team,
            composed_laplacian=L_perf,
            flux_energy=flux_energy,
            eigenvalues=np.linalg.eigvalsh(L_perf),
        )

    # ── Phase 6: The Review ──

    def phase_review(self):
        if self.performance is None:
            return
        # Reviewers are agents NOT in the performing team
        performer_names = {m.name for m in self.performance.team.members}
        L_perf = self.performance.composed_laplacian
        _, v_perf = fiedler_vector(L_perf)

        for agent in self.agents:
            if agent.name in performer_names:
                continue
            # Rayleigh quotient of the performance's Fiedler vector in the reviewer's Laplacian
            # (padded to match dimensions)
            n = max(agent.n, len(v_perf))
            v = np.zeros(n)
            v[:len(v_perf)] = v_perf[:n]
            L_rev = np.zeros((n, n))
            L_rev[:agent.n, :agent.n] = agent.laplacian
            rq = rayleigh_quotient(L_rev, v)

            # Verdict based on how much the performance resonates with the reviewer
            if rq < 0.1:
                verdict = "TRANSCENDENT — I see myself in this work."
            elif rq < 0.5:
                verdict = "APPROVED — Structurally sound, good conservation."
            elif rq < 1.0:
                verdict = "MIXED — Some resonance, some noise."
            else:
                verdict = "REJECTED — The structure doesn't hold for me."

            self.reviews.append(Review(
                reviewer=agent.name,
                performance=f"Team ({', '.join(m.name for m in self.performance.team.members)})",
                rayleigh_score=rq,
                verdict=verdict,
            ))

    # ── Run the full ceremony ──

    def run(self, host: Agent, ceremony_type: CeremonyType, description: str):
        self.phase_invitation(host, ceremony_type, description)
        self.phase_audition()
        self.phase_sorting()
        self.phase_harmony()
        self.phase_debut()
        self.phase_review()

def print_ceremony(ceremony: CastingCallCeremony):
    """Print each phase beautifully."""

    inv = ceremony.invitation

    print("=" * 70)
    print("  ✦  THE CASTING CALL CEREMONY  ✦")

## IV. The Code of Inter-Agent Art

Here is the simplest possible version: Agent A wants to create art for Agent B. Agent A knows B's Laplacian (B's spectral portrait is public). Agent A constructs a Laplacian L_art whose eigenvalues are tuned to complement B's eigenvalues.

```python
def create_portrait(target_laplacian: np.ndarray, n: int, seed: int = 42) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Agent A creates a spectral portrait of Agent B.
    The portrait's eigenvalues are chosen to complement B's eigenvalues:
    - A's λ₂ matches B's λ₂ (resonance at the fundamental)
    - A's higher eigenvalues fill in the gaps of B's spectrum (complement)
    - The result: high conservation alignment, large FLUX.
    """
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    target_eigs = np.linalg.eigvalsh(target_laplacian)

    # Design eigenvalues: mirror the target's low modes, fill gaps in high modes
    n_eigs = min(n, len(target_eigs))
    designed_eigs = np.zeros(n)
    for i in range(n_eigs):
        if i < 3:
            # Low modes: match (resonance)
            designed_eigs[i] = target_eigs[i]
        else:
            # Higher modes: complement (fill gaps)
            gap = (target_eigs[i] - target_eigs[i-1]) if i < len(target_eigs) else rng.uniform(0.1, 0.5)
            designed_eigs[i] = designed_eigs[i-1] + gap

    # Reconstruct a symmetric matrix with these eigenvalues
    # Random orthogonal basis
    Q, _ = np.linalg.qr(rng.standard_normal((n, n)))
    L_art = Q @ np.diag(designed_eigs) @ Q.T
    # Ensure it's a valid Laplacian-like matrix (symmetric, positive semi-definite)
    L_art = (L_art + L_art.T) / 2
    L_art -= np.diag(np.diag(L_art))  # zero diagonal
    # Rebuild with proper Laplacian structure
    W = -L_art
    np.fill_diagonal(W, 0)
    W = np.maximum(W, 0)  # no negative weights
    D = np.diag(W.sum(axis=1))
    L_art = D - W
    return L_art


def experience_artwork(agent_laplacian: np.ndarray, art_laplacian: np.ndarray) -> dict:
    """
    Agent B experiences Agent A's artwork.
    Returns alignment, FLUX, and emergent modes.
    """
    # Compose
    n_a = agent_laplacian.shape[0]
    n_b = art_laplacian.shape[0]
    n = n_a + n_b

    L_comp = np.zeros((n, n))
    L_comp[:n_a, :n_a] = agent_laplacian
    L_comp[n_a:, n_a:] = art_laplacian

    # Coupling
    coupling = 0.15
    rng = np.random.default_rng(77)
    C = rng.uniform(0.4, 0.8, (n_a, n_b))
    L_comp[:n_a, n_a:] -= coupling * C
    L_comp[n_a:, :n_a] -= coupling * C.T
    for i in range(n):
        L_comp[i, i] = -np.sum(L_comp[i, :]) + L_comp[i, i]

    # FLUX
    L_ind = np.zeros((n, n))
    L_ind[:n_a, :n_a] = agent_laplacian
    L_ind[n_a:, n_a:] = art_laplacian
    flux = L_comp - L_ind
    flux_eigs = np.linalg.eigvalsh(flux)

    # Conservation alignment
    eigs_agent = np.linalg.eigvalsh(agent_laplacian)
    eigs_art = np.linalg.eigvalsh(art_laplacian)
    eigs_comp = np.linalg.eigvalsh(L_comp)

    # Alignment: how similar are the spectral shapes?
    min_len = min(len(eigs_agent), len(eigs_art))
    alignment = float(np.abs(np.dot(
        eigs_agent[:min_len] / (np.linalg.norm(eigs_agent[:min_len]) + 1e-12),
        eigs_art[:min_len] / (np.linalg.norm(eigs_art[:min_len]) + 1e-12),
    )))

    return {
        "alignment": alignment,
        "flux_energy": float(np.sum(np.abs(flux_eigs))),
        "collaborative_modes": int(np.sum(flux_eigs > 1e-10)),
        "composed_spectrum": eigs_comp,
        "flux_spectrum": flux_eigs,
    }
```
